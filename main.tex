\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Overview}
  Koszul duality is the organizing algebraic mechanism for the local twisted
  M-theory model studied here.\par

  \begin{rmk}
    This introduction records the physical source of the local theorem. The
    mathematical assertions are the formal local statements proved below.
  \end{rmk}

  Let $X$ be a holomorphic symplectic manifold. Consider a topological
  twist of M-theory on $\R \times X$ in the presence of a stack of $N$
  one-dimensional topological branes supported on $\R \times p$, for some point
  $p \in X$.

  The local model contains two algebras:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The algebra $\mc{A}_N$ of operators on the stack of $N$ branes
      supported on $\R \times p$.
    \item The algebra $\mc{B}$ of local operators of the gravitational theory
      on $\R \times X$. Operator product expansions in the direction of $\R$
      equip $\mc{B}$ with the structure of an associative algebra.
  \end{itemize}

  The guiding large-$N$ statement is:
  \begin{conj}
    As $N \to \infty$, $\mc{A}_N$ is the Koszul dual of $\mc{B}$.
  \end{conj}

  \begin{rmk}
    Witten's Chern-Simons model \cite{witten-cs} supplies the open-string
    algebra used for operator-product calculations.
  \end{rmk}

  These notes isolate one local mixed A/B example and verify the classical
  stable operator-algebra match. The quantum discussion is restricted to the
  canonical first-order bracket and the formal odd-power Moyal targets. The
  closed-string Feynman integral construction is formulated as
  Problem~\ref{prob:analytic-graph-realization}.

\subsection{The local theorem}

Let
$$\bar A=\C[z_1,z_2]/\C\cdot1,\qquad
  \widehat{\mathfrak h}=(z_1,z_2)\C[[z_1,z_2]],\qquad
  \mathfrak h=\widehat{\mathfrak h},\qquad
  \mathfrak h^\vee=\widehat{\mathfrak h}^{\vee}_{\mathrm{cont}},$$
and set
$$\mathfrak g=\widehat{\mathfrak h}\ltimes\mathfrak h^\vee[1],$$
where $\mathfrak h$ carries the Poisson bracket
$$\{f,g\}=\partial_{z_1}f\,\partial_{z_2}g
  -\partial_{z_2}f\,\partial_{z_1}g,$$
understood after projection modulo constants, and $\mathfrak h^\vee$ has the
continuous dual Taylor basis $\delta_{k,l}$ for $k+l>0$. We use $\bar A$ for
degreewise polynomial calculations and $\widehat{\mathfrak h}$ for the completed
closed CE/Koszul algebra. Constants are removed because a constant
Hamiltonian has zero Hamiltonian vector field. On the open side
$\mathrm{Tr}(1)=N$ is a scalar parameter.
The symbol $\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}$ denotes an abstract copy of $\bar A$ whose
nonconstant basis vectors are represented by the first descendant cycles
constructed below.

\begin{thm}[Local stable Hamiltonian-sector Koszul duality]\label{thm:main-local}
  In the formal local mixed A/B model on $\R^2\times\C^2$ with brane
  $\R\times p$, the stable large-$N$ scalar-reduced open Hamiltonian
  algebra freely generated by the primitive degree-zero Hamiltonian traces and
  the nonconstant primitive one-antifield Koszul classes is
  $$\mc{O}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{brane}}
    \cong \mathbf{S}\bigl(\bar A\oplus\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}[1]\bigr).$$
  The classical local operators in the formal Hamiltonian cotangent BF sector
  are the continuous Chevalley-Eilenberg algebra
  $$\mc{O}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{grav}}
    \cong C^\bullet_{\mathrm{CE},\mathrm{cont}}(\mathfrak g)
    =\widehat{\mathbf{S}}\bigl(\mathfrak g^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}[-1]\bigr).$$
  Its Koszul dual is the completed graded enveloping algebra
  $$\bigl(\mc{O}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{grav}}\bigr)^!
    \simeq \widehat{U(\mathfrak g)}.$$
  The PBW/order Rees algebra $U_\hbar(\mathfrak g)$ has completed special fibre
  $$U_\hbar(\mathfrak g)/(\hbar)\cong
    \widehat{\mathbf{S}}\bigl(\widehat{\mathfrak h}\oplus\mathfrak h^\vee[1]\bigr).$$
  Its degreewise polynomial subalgebra
  $\mathbf{S}(\bar A\oplus\mathfrak h^\vee[1])$ identifies with the open stable
  Hamiltonian-sector algebra after choosing the monomial-dual basis
  $\mathfrak h^\vee\cong\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}$. This is a PBW special-fibre
  product identification; the open-side deformation is checked below on
  degree-zero Hamiltonian generators. On those generators the
  identification is induced by the scalar-reduced bulk-to-boundary evaluation
  map
  $$\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}(f)=\overline{\mathrm{Tr}\,f(\phi_1,\phi_2)},$$
  which sends a closed Hamiltonian to the corresponding brane Hamiltonian and
  intertwines Poisson brackets. The shifted coadjoint generators are sent to the
  nonconstant first Koszul descendants $\Psi_{k,l}$. On monomials,
  $$z_1^kz_2^l\longleftrightarrow
    \overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)},\qquad k+l>0,$$
  the first bracket is
  $$\{\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)},
      \overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^m\phi_2^n)}\}
    =\overline{(kn-lm)\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^{k+m-1}\phi_2^{l+n-1})},$$
  matching the Poisson bracket on $\bar A$. If a displayed exponent is
  negative, the coefficient is already zero; if the displayed monomial is
  constant, scalar reduction kills it.
\end{thm}

\subsection{Status of assertions}

The theorem above is the precise local assertion proved in these notes; the
terms appearing in it are constructed in the subsections below. The proof
consists of the affine Ext calculation, the open BV reduction, the stable
invariant-theory calculation of the scalar-reduced Hamiltonian trace algebra,
the computation of the primitive one-antifield Koszul homology, the formal
Hamiltonian BF model for the closed side, the construction of the
bulk-to-boundary Hamiltonian evaluation map, the CE/Koszul-duality computation,
and the first bracket calculation.

The formal Weyl/Moyal calculation below is an all-order algebraic
deformation-quantization target for the constant Poisson formal disk; its
monomial coefficients are checked by an exact script. A closed-string
Feynman-integral derivation of those coefficients is a separate analytic
problem. It requires the global BV/gauge-fixing data listed in
Problem~\ref{prob:analytic-graph-realization}. The proof of the local theorem
uses the formal local model alone.

The descendant generators are identified with $\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$ in the PBW
special fibre. Proposition~\ref{prop:open-descendant-action} computes the
open Poisson action on polynomial descendants, and
Corollary~\ref{cor:descendant-coadjoint-difference} identifies the coadjoint
action as a different representation. The polynomial boundary model lacks a
deformation-level descendant match.

\subsection{Conventions for the local calculation}

Everything below is over $\C$. The phrase \emph{formal local model} means the
completed Taylor model at the chosen point of $\C^2$; equivalently, one may
work polynomially degree by degree and then complete at the augmentation ideal.
The symbol $\widehat{\mathbf S}$ denotes this completed symmetric algebra, and
all Chevalley-Eilenberg cochains are continuous cochains for the same
filtration.

Shifts follow the BV convention used in the field descriptions: an element of
$V[1]$ has parity reversed and cohomological degree one lower than the
corresponding element of $V$. The notation $\mathbf S(V)$ always means the free
graded-commutative algebra on the graded vector space $V$.

We write $\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}$ for an abstract copy of $\bar A$ whose basis
vector labelled by $z_1^kz_2^l$, with $k+l>0$, is represented on the brane by
the first descendant $\Psi_{k,l}$. On the closed side the cotangent field gives
the continuous Taylor dual $\mathfrak h^\vee$. Choosing the monomial Taylor
basis identifies $\mathfrak h^\vee$ with $\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}$ as a filtered
vector space. The closed module structure remains the coadjoint module
structure.

Boundary terms on $\R$ are discarded by taking compactly supported variations,
or equivalently by working on an interval with fixed boundary values and then
passing to local operators in the interior. The term \emph{stable large-$N$}
means the degreewise stable value of the invariant algebras under block
restriction as $N\to\infty$. Inside a fixed finite matrix algebra all finite-$N$
trace identities remain present. The scalar trace $\mathrm{Tr}(1)=N$ is
treated as a parameter and is removed from the reduced operator algebra.
If $T$ is a trace expression, $\overline T$ denotes its class modulo the scalar
line $\C\cdot\mathrm{Tr}(1)$.

On the open side we compute the scalar-reduced primitive degree-zero
Hamiltonian traces and the nonconstant primitive one-$\psi$ Koszul homology. The
scalar class
$\mathrm{Tr}(\psi)$ is computed by the same finite argument and excluded from
the Hamiltonian comparison, since constants have been removed from $\bar A$.
Higher $\psi$-count homology lies outside the Hamiltonian comparison.

\subsection{The concrete dictionary}

The comparison is the following explicit dictionary. For $k,l\geq0$ and
$k+l>0$, set
$$H_{k,l}=z_1^kz_2^l\in\bar A,\qquad
  E_{k,l}=\delta_{k,l}[1]\in\mathfrak h^\vee[1],$$
where $\delta_{k,l}$ is dual to the Taylor monomial $z_1^kz_2^l$.
On the brane side set
$$O_{k,l}=\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)},$$
and let $\Psi_{k,l}$ be the symmetrised trace obtained by inserting one
$\psi$ into the word with $k$ copies of $\phi_1$ and $l$ copies of $\phi_2$;
the odd matrix $\psi$ is the Koszul variable for the moment-map equation,
introduced in Construction~\ref{constr:reduced-bv-algebra}.
The theorem proves that the assignment
$$H_{k,l}\longmapsto O_{k,l},\qquad E_{k,l}\longmapsto\Psi_{k,l}$$
extends to an isomorphism of the scalar-reduced Hamiltonian descendant algebra
with
$$\mathbf S(\bar A\oplus\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}[1])
  \xrightarrow{\ \sim\ }\mc O_{\mathrm{brane}}^{\mathrm{cl}}$$
in the sector considered here. On the closed side the symbols
$H_{k,l},E_{k,l}$ form the degreewise polynomial PBW generators inside the
special fibre of $U_\hbar(\mathfrak g)$.

Thus every later step has a concrete target: construct the symbols
$O_{k,l},\Psi_{k,l}$ from the open fields, construct $H_{k,l},E_{k,l}$ from
Hamiltonians and cotangent Hamiltonians, prove that commutators of words reduce
to commutative monomials, and check that the bracket coefficient is
$(kn-lm)$.

\subsection{String Field Theory and Koszul Duality}
  We expect Koszul duality for string field theories to be reflected in
  stable trace invariants and cyclic homology. The algebraic template is the
  Loday-Quillen-Tsygan theorem \cites{loday-quillen}{tsygan}:
  \begin{thm}[Loday-Quillen-Tsygan]
    Let $A$ be a unital associative algebra over a field of characteristic
    zero. The stable Chevalley-Eilenberg homology of
    $\lie{gl}_{\infty}(A)$ is the free graded-commutative algebra generated by
    the shifted cyclic homology of $A$:
    $$H_*^{\mathrm{CE}}\bigl(\lie{gl}_{\infty}(A)\bigr)
      \cong \mathbf{S}\bigl(HC_{*-1}(A)\bigr).$$
  \end{thm}

  Thus stable matrix invariants are controlled by cyclic words. In the
  particular reduced model below, BRST exactness of $[\phi_1,\phi_2]$ turns
  cyclic words into ordinary commutative monomials, and the surviving
  single-trace generators are represented by
  $\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)}$ with $k+l>0$.

  The calculation below is the corresponding classical shadow: closed
  Hamiltonian modes match stable single-trace generators, and the first
  nontrivial bracket agrees with the Poisson bracket.

\subsection{Flavours of Topological Strings}
Topological strings manifest in various flavours. Common examples include:

\begin{itemize}
  \item The \textbf{A-model}, where branes are objects of a Fukaya category:
    Lagrangians equipped with the grading, spin or Pin, local-system, and
    unobstructedness data required to define Floer theory.
  \item The \textbf{B-model}, where branes are objects of
    $D^b\mathrm{Coh}(X)$, or of $\mathrm{Perf}(X)$ in the affine local model.
  \item \textbf{Mixed A-B models}, e.g.\ on $X \times X'$ with branes given by
    $L\times Z$ for $L \inj X$ Lagrangian and $Z \inj X'$ holomorphic.
\end{itemize}

\begin{rmk}
  The enumerative content of the A-model includes its world-sheet instanton
  sectors.
\end{rmk}

The construction has two structural inputs:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The open string theory, which is the theory on a brane, and
  \item The closed string field theory, of a gravitational nature.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Open Strings}
If $\mc{C}$ is a cyclic Calabi-Yau $A_\infty$ category and
$\mc{F}\in\mathrm{Ob}\,\mc{C}$, set $A=\RHom(\mc{F},\mc{F})$. The open-string
states are $A$. The BV fields are\footnotemark\footnotetext{If we produce a category, the resulting field
  theory is defined on a point. If instead we produce a sheaf of categories,
  we get a field theory on the support of this sheaf, i.e.\ a field theory on
  the given brane.}
$$A[1].$$
The Calabi-Yau structure supplies a cyclic trace $\mathrm{Tr}:A\to\C[-d]$.
For a cyclic $A_\infty$ algebra with operations $m_n$, the formal
Chern-Simons action is
$$S(a)=\sum_{n\geq1}\frac{1}{n+1}\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(a\,m_n(a,\ldots,a)\bigr),
  \qquad a\in A[1].$$
This is the algebraic core of Witten's open string field theory
\cite{witten-cs} and of the AKSZ packaging \cite{aksz}.

  \section{The Local Model}

\subsection{Setup}
  Let $X = \R^2_A \times \C^2_B$. We use the exact A-brane given by the
  standard line $\R\subset\R^2_A$ with trivial brane data and the B-brane
  $\mc{O}_p\in\mathrm{Perf}(\C^2_B)$. Consider the product brane
  $$\R \times p \inj \R^2_A \times \C^2_B$$
  for some point $p \in \C^2_B$. The A-model lives on $\R^2_A$ (equipped with
  its standard symplectic structure) and the B-model lives on $\C^2_B$
  (equipped with its standard holomorphic-symplectic structure $\omega =
  dz_1\wedge dz_2$).

\subsection{Open String Field States}
  The open string states are the tensor product of A-model and B-model states.

  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{A-model factor.} In the exact self-Floer model for
      $\R\subset T^*\R\cong\R^2$, a small Hamiltonian perturbation identifies
      $CF^*(\R,\R)$ with the Morse complex of $\R$. Hence
      $HF^*(\R,\R)\cong H^*(\R;\C)$, and the de Rham complex $\Omega^*(\R)$ is
      the chain-level model used below.
    \item \textit{B-model factor.} Put affine coordinates at $p=0$ and write
      $R=\C[z_1,z_2]$, $\mathfrak m=(z_1,z_2)$, and $\mc{O}_p=R/\mathfrak m$.
      The skyscraper sheaf has open string states
      $$\Ext^*_R(R/\mathfrak m,R/\mathfrak m),$$
      computed in Proposition~\ref{prop:skyscraper-ext}.
  \end{itemize}

\begin{prop}[Affine skyscraper self-Ext]\label{prop:skyscraper-ext}
  Let $R=\C[z_1,z_2]$, $\mathfrak m=(z_1,z_2)$, and $k=R/\mathfrak m$. Then
  the Yoneda algebra of the skyscraper sheaf at the origin is
  $$\Ext^*_R(k,k)\cong\Lambda_{\C}(\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2),$$
  where $|\varepsilon_i|=1$. In particular this is the free
  graded-commutative algebra on two odd generators.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  The pair $(z_1,z_2)$ is a regular sequence in the polynomial ring $R$, so the
  affine Koszul complex
  $$K=\bigl(0\to R\,e_{12}\xrightarrow{d_2}R\,e_1\oplus R\,e_2
        \xrightarrow{d_1}R\to0\bigr)$$
  is a free resolution of $k$, with
  $$d_1(ae_1+be_2)=az_1+bz_2,\qquad
    d_2(e_{12})=z_2e_1-z_1e_2.$$
  Applying $\Hom_R(-,k)$ gives
  $$0\to k\xrightarrow{0}k\,\varepsilon_1\oplus k\,\varepsilon_2
    \xrightarrow{0}k\,\varepsilon_{12}\to0,$$
  because $z_1$ and $z_2$ act by zero on $k$. Hence the graded vector space of
  Ext groups is $k$ in degree $0$, $k\varepsilon_1\oplus k\varepsilon_2$ in
  degree $1$, and $k\varepsilon_{12}$ in degree $2$.

  The product is the Yoneda product. The Koszul resolution is the exterior
  dg-algebra $R\otimes\Lambda(e_1,e_2)$ with $d(e_i)=z_i$; its diagonal sends
  $e_i$ to $e_i\otimes1+1\otimes e_i$. Dualising this diagonal gives
  $\varepsilon_i^2=0$ and
  $\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2=-\varepsilon_2\varepsilon_1$, while
  $\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2$ spans the top Ext group. Thus the Yoneda
  algebra is $\Lambda_{\C}(\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2)$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Open String Field Theory}

The total space of fields (in the Batalin-Vilkovisky formalism) is
$$\alpha \in \Omega^*(\R)\otimes\Lambda(\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2)
  \otimes \lie{gl}_N[1]$$
with action
$$S(\alpha) = \int_{\R \times \C^{0|2}}\frac{1}{2}\,\mathrm{Tr}(\alpha\, d\alpha) + \frac{1}{3}\,\mathrm{Tr}\,\alpha^3.$$
Here $d = d_{\mathrm{dR}}$; the Ext algebra
$\Lambda(\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2)$ has zero differential. The integral
over $\C^{0|2}$ is the Berezin integral $\int d\varepsilon_1\,d\varepsilon_2$,
which picks out the top component in the odd directions.

\begin{constr}[The open field from the Ext basis]\label{constr:open-field-basis}
  Write $\varepsilon_{12}=\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2$. Before imposing ghost
  number, an open field is a finite sum
  $$\alpha=a_0+a_1\varepsilon_1+a_2\varepsilon_2+a_{12}\varepsilon_{12},$$
  where each $a_I$ is a de Rham form on $\R$ with values in $\lie{gl}_N$,
  shifted by $[1]$. The ghost-number-zero components are exactly
  $$A\in\Omega^1(\R)\otimes\lie{gl}_N,\qquad
    \phi_1,\phi_2\in\Omega^0(\R)\otimes\lie{gl}_N,$$
  and they appear as
  $$\alpha_0=A+\phi_1\varepsilon_1+\phi_2\varepsilon_2.$$
  The Berezin integral kills every monomial except the coefficient of
  $\varepsilon_{12}$, so every term in the ghost-zero action is obtained by
  multiplying the displayed three summands and keeping exactly one real
  one-form and exactly the product $\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2$.
\end{constr}

\begin{rmk}
  The shift $[1]$ acts on the total cohomological degree: a form of de Rham
  degree $p$ and Grassmann degree $q$ has total degree $p+q$, which after the
  shift sits in ghost number $p+q-1$. In particular, the 1-form component of
  $\alpha$ (de Rham degree 1, Grassmann degree 0) sits in ghost number 0 and
  is the bosonic gauge field. The scalar components proportional to
  $\varepsilon_i$ (de Rham degree 0, Grassmann degree 1) also sit in ghost
  number 0 and represent the motion of the brane in the two normal directions.
\end{rmk}

\subsubsection{Ghost-zero open field content}
  The ghost-number-zero part of the BV field is
  $$\alpha_0=A+\phi_1\varepsilon_1+\phi_2\varepsilon_2,$$
  where $A\in\Omega^1(\R)\otimes\lie{gl}_N$ and
  $\phi_i\in\Omega^0(\R)\otimes\lie{gl}_N$.

  \begin{lem}\label{lem:open-action-reduction}
    With the Berezin convention
    $\int_{\C^{0|2}}\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2=1$, the restriction of the
    BV action to $\alpha_0$ is, up to a boundary term on $\R$,
    $$S(\alpha_0)=\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2
      +A[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr).$$
  \end{lem}

  \begin{proof}
    Only monomials containing one real one-form and the factor
    $\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2$ survive the integration over
    $\R\times\C^{0|2}$. In the quadratic term this leaves
    $$\frac12\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2-\phi_2\,d\phi_1\bigr),$$
    where the sign is the Koszul sign from interchanging the two odd
    generators. Since
    $d\,\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\phi_2)=\mathrm{Tr}(d\phi_1\,\phi_2+\phi_1\,d\phi_2)$,
    the second summand differs from $\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\,d\phi_2)$ by an exact
    one-form. In the cubic term the six orderings of
    $A,\phi_1\varepsilon_1,\phi_2\varepsilon_2$ reduce by cyclicity of the trace
    and the odd relation $\varepsilon_2\varepsilon_1=-\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2$
    to $\mathrm{Tr}(A\phi_1\phi_2-A\phi_2\phi_1)$. This is
    $\mathrm{Tr}(A[\phi_1,\phi_2])$.
  \end{proof}

\begin{prop}[Classical BV truncation]\label{prop:open-bv-truncation}
  The subspace of ghost-number-zero fields
  $$\alpha_0=A+\phi_1\varepsilon_1+\phi_2\varepsilon_2$$
  with all nonzero ghost-number components set to zero is a consistent
  classical truncation of the open BV theory. The induced classical action is
  the first-order gauged matrix model of Lemma~\ref{lem:open-action-reduction}.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Decompose the BV field as $\alpha=\sum_r\alpha^{(r)}$ by ghost number. The
  differential $d_{\R}$ and the product on
  $\Lambda(\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2)\otimes\lie{gl}_N$ preserve total degree,
  and the BV action has ghost number zero. Hence every monomial in the
  Euler-Lagrange expression for a component of ghost number $r\neq0$ contains
  at least one field component of nonzero ghost number when evaluated at
  $\alpha=\alpha_0$. Setting all such components to zero therefore makes the
  discarded equations vanish. The remaining equation is exactly the variation
  of $S(\alpha_0)$, computed in Lemma~\ref{lem:open-action-reduction}.

  Concretely, the full Euler-Lagrange expression is the curvature
  $$F(\alpha)=d\alpha+\alpha^2.$$
  For $\alpha_0=A+\phi_1\varepsilon_1+\phi_2\varepsilon_2$ on a line,
  \begin{align*}
  F(\alpha_0)
    &=(d\phi_1+[A,\phi_1])\varepsilon_1
      +(d\phi_2+[A,\phi_2])\varepsilon_2
      +[\phi_1,\phi_2]\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2.
  \end{align*}
  The BV pairing keeps exactly real-form degree one and
  $\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2$. Hence the displayed three components pair exactly
  with the variations of $A,\phi_2,\phi_1$ respectively, while variations of
  nonzero ghost number see complementary curvature components that are zero for
  $\alpha_0$ by real-form degree or $\varepsilon$-degree. This is a physical
  ghost-zero truncation; the residual ordinary $GL_N$ gauge symmetry remains.
\end{proof}

  The ghost-number-zero truncation of the BV master action is closed under the
  classical equations and gives a quantum-mechanical system with a connection
  form
  $$A \in \Omega^1(\R)\otimes \lie{gl}_N,$$
  two adjoint scalars
  $$\phi_1,\phi_2 \in \Omega^0(\R)\otimes\lie{gl}_N,$$
  and action
  $$S = \int_\R \mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2
    + A[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr).$$
  This action is invariant under
  $$\delta_{\varepsilon}\phi_i=[\varepsilon,\phi_i],\qquad
    \delta_{\varepsilon}A=d\varepsilon+[A,\varepsilon],$$
  for $\varepsilon\in\Omega^0(\R)\otimes\lie{gl}_N$. Indeed the variation of
  $\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1d\phi_2)$ containing $d\varepsilon$ is
  $-\mathrm{Tr}(d\varepsilon[\phi_1,\phi_2])$, and this cancels the
  $d\varepsilon$ variation of $\mathrm{Tr}(A[\phi_1,\phi_2])$; the remaining
  terms cancel by invariance of the trace pairing.

\begin{rmk}
  The same open model is obtained inside the Calabi-Yau category by tensoring a
  fixed object with $\C^N$ and passing to endomorphisms. The categorical
  construction is governed by cyclic cohomology. The three-point function is the
  cup product in cyclic cohomology followed by the trace; higher $n$-point
  functions require the corresponding higher operations.
\end{rmk}

\begin{rmk}
  In AKSZ language this is the one-dimensional gauged sigma model with
  Hamiltonian target $\lie{gl}_N\oplus\lie{gl}_N$ and symplectic potential
  $\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\,d\phi_2)$. We use the resulting Poisson bivector
  $$\Pi=\sum_{i,j}\frac{\partial}{\partial(\phi_1)^i{}_j}
    \wedge\frac{\partial}{\partial(\phi_2)^j{}_i},$$
  so $\{(\phi_1)^i{}_j,(\phi_2)^k{}_l\}=\delta^i_l\delta^k_j$. Equivalently,
  with the convention $\iota_{X_f}\omega=-df$ used throughout, the symplectic
  form is $\mathrm{Tr}(d\phi_1\wedge d\phi_2)$. For
  $\epsilon\in\lie{gl}_N$, the Hamiltonian
  $$\mu_\epsilon=-\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\epsilon[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr)$$
  generates $\delta_\epsilon\phi_i=[\epsilon,\phi_i]$. Its zero locus is the
  commuting-pair equation $[\phi_1,\phi_2]=0$, and the classical reduced phase
  space is the stack of commuting pairs of matrices modulo conjugation.
\end{rmk}

\begin{rmk}
  Equivalently, take Chern-Simons theory on $\R\times T^2$ and restrict to the
  harmonic forms $1,\eta_1,\eta_2,\eta_1\eta_2$ on $T^2$. Writing
  $A_{3d}=A_t\,dt+\phi_1\eta_1+\phi_2\eta_2$ and integrating over $T^2$
  gives the same action; the odd variables $\varepsilon_i$ represent the
  degree-one cohomology classes $\eta_i$.
\end{rmk}

\subsection{Closed String States}
Similarly, the closed string state space is of the form
$$\text{A-model closed states}\otimes \text{B-model closed states.}$$
For the A-model on $\R^2$, we take the de Rham cohomology complex
$\Omega^*(\R^2)$.

For the B-model on $\C^2$, we use the local holomorphic-symplectic
Kodaira-Spencer model obtained from Hamiltonian holomorphic vector fields. It
uses polyvectors and the divergence operator, and it is distinct from a
truncation of Calabi-Yau threefold BCOV theory.

\begin{rmk}
  The original BCOV analysis \cite{bcov} is written for Calabi-Yau threefolds.
  On a holomorphic symplectic surface the natural local cotangent theory is the
  BF theory for the dg Lie algebra of Hamiltonian vector fields. The
  holomorphic symplectic form supplies the Poisson bracket, while the
  holomorphic volume form supplies the divergence operator on polyvectors.
\end{rmk}

We consider the space of polyvector fields
$$\mathbf{PV}^{*,*}(\C^2) \cong \Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)[\partial_{z_1},\partial_{z_2}]$$
where $\partial_{z_i}$ are odd generators of poly-vector degree 1 (corresponding
to the two holomorphic vector fields on $\C^2$). The operator
$$\partial: \mathbf{PV}^{i,*} \to \mathbf{PV}^{i-1,*}$$
is the divergence operator determined by the holomorphic volume form
$\Omega=dz_1\wedge dz_2$:
$$\partial_{\Omega}=\iota_{\Omega}^{-1}\circ\partial_{\mathrm{hol}}\circ
  \iota_{\Omega},\qquad
  \iota_{\Omega}:\mathbf{PV}^{i,*}\xrightarrow{\sim}\Omega^{2-i,*}.$$
Thus for $X=a_1\partial_{z_1}+a_2\partial_{z_2}$,
$$\partial_{\Omega}X=(\partial_{z_1}a_1+\partial_{z_2}a_2),$$
while $\partial_{\Omega}$ vanishes on functions.

We work in the formal polynomial local model at $0\in\C^2$, so Dolbeault
cohomology is represented by holomorphic Taylor polynomials. Here
$\partial_{\Omega}$ is the divergence constraint selecting the Hamiltonian BF
sector. The kinematic differential of the closed theory below is
$D=d_{\R^2}+\bar\partial_{\C^2}$. Thus the Poincare lemma identifies
divergence-free vector fields with Hamiltonian potentials. The quotient by
$\partial_{\Omega}$-exact Hamiltonians is absent. Analysing degree by
degree:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\mathbf{PV}^{0,*}(\C^2) = \Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)\subset\Ker\partial$
    trivially.
  \item $\mathbf{PV}^{1,*}(\C^2)\cong\Omega^{1,*}(\C^2)$ via
    $\iota_{(-)}\omega$. Under this identification, with the Hamiltonian
    convention $\iota_{X_f}\omega=-df$ used below,
    $\Ker\partial_{\Omega}$ is the space of closed holomorphic one-forms.
    Lemma~\ref{lem:polynomial-poincare} identifies these with $df$, uniquely
    modulo constants.
  \item The constant Hamiltonians act trivially, since their Hamiltonian vector
    fields vanish. We therefore use the reduced polynomial algebra
    $$\bar A=\C[z_1,z_2]/\C\cdot1$$
    for nontrivial Hamiltonian fields.
\end{itemize}

\begin{lem}[Polynomial Poincare primitive]\label{lem:polynomial-poincare}
  Let
  $$\theta=a(z_1,z_2)\,dz_1+b(z_1,z_2)\,dz_2$$
  be a polynomial one-form on $\C^2$. If
  $\partial_{z_2}a=\partial_{z_1}b$, then there is a polynomial
  $f\in\C[z_1,z_2]$, unique modulo constants, such that $\theta=df$.
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  Define
  $$f(z_1,z_2)=\int_0^1
    \bigl(z_1a(tz_1,tz_2)+z_2b(tz_1,tz_2)\bigr)\,dt.$$
  Since $a$ and $b$ are polynomials, the integral is termwise division of each
  homogeneous term by its degree plus one, hence $f$ is polynomial. Differentiate
  under the integral sign. Closedness says
  $\partial_{z_2}a=\partial_{z_1}b$, so
  \begin{align*}
  \partial_{z_1}f
    &=\int_0^1\bigl(a(tz)+tz_1\partial_{z_1}a(tz)
       +tz_2\partial_{z_1}b(tz)\bigr)\,dt\\
    &=\int_0^1\bigl(a(tz)+t z_1\partial_{z_1}a(tz)
       +t z_2\partial_{z_2}a(tz)\bigr)\,dt\\
    &=\int_0^1\frac{d}{dt}\bigl(t\,a(tz)\bigr)\,dt
      =a(z).
  \end{align*}
  The same calculation, with $a$ and $b$ interchanged, gives
  $\partial_{z_2}f=b(z)$. Therefore $df=\theta$.
  If $df=dg$, then $d(f-g)=0$, hence $f-g$ is constant.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[Hamiltonian polyvector reduction]\label{prop:hamiltonian-polyvector-reduction}
  In the formal polynomial model on $(\C^2,\omega=dz_1\wedge dz_2)$, the
  divergence-closed polyvector-one fields reduce to Hamiltonian vector fields:
  $$\Ker(\partial_{\Omega}:\mathbf{PV}^{1}\to\mathbf{PV}^{0})
    \cong \{df:f\in\C[z_1,z_2]\}
    \cong \bar A.$$
  Under the convention
  $$X_f=(\partial_{z_1}f)\partial_{z_2}
    -(\partial_{z_2}f)\partial_{z_1},\qquad \iota_{X_f}\omega=-df,$$
  the commutator of vector fields is
  $$[X_f,X_g]=X_{\{f,g\}_{\bar A}}.$$
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Let $X=a_1\partial_{z_1}+a_2\partial_{z_2}$. The divergence operator is
  $\partial_{\Omega}X=\partial_{z_1}a_1+\partial_{z_2}a_2$. Contracting with
  $\omega$ gives
  $$\iota_X\omega=a_1\,dz_2-a_2\,dz_1.$$
  The equation $\partial_{\Omega}X=0$ is exactly the closedness of this
  one-form:
  $$d(\iota_X\omega)
    =(\partial_{z_1}a_1+\partial_{z_2}a_2)\,dz_1\wedge dz_2=0.$$
  By Lemma~\ref{lem:polynomial-poincare}, there is a polynomial $f$, unique
  modulo constants, such that $\iota_X\omega=-df$. Therefore
  $$a_1=-\partial_{z_2}f,\qquad a_2=\partial_{z_1}f,$$
  so $X=X_f$. Constants are exactly the kernel of $f\mapsto X_f$, giving
  $\bar A$.

  Finally, for any polynomial $h$,
  \begin{align*}
  [X_f,X_g](h)
    &=\{f,\{g,h\}\}-\{g,\{f,h\}\}\\
    &=\{\{f,g\},h\}
  \end{align*}
  by the Jacobi identity for the constant Poisson bracket. Thus
  $[X_f,X_g]=X_{\{f,g\}}$, and projecting constants gives the bracket on
  $\bar A$.
\end{proof}

\begin{prob}[Analytic graph realization data]\label{prob:analytic-graph-realization}
  A closed-string Feynman-integral theorem on the noncompact space
  $\R^2\times\C^2$ requires the following data:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item a global BV elliptic complex extending the formal jet model;
    \item boundary or asymptotic conditions on $\C^2$;
    \item a gauge fixing $Q^{\mathrm{GF}}$ and the corresponding heat-kernel
      propagator
      $$P_{\epsilon,L}=\int_{\epsilon}^{L}Q^{\mathrm{GF}}K_t\,dt;$$
    \item the treatment of zero modes and the infrared limit $L\to\infty$;
    \item the ultraviolet renormalisation scheme and local counterterms;
    \item a proof of the quantum master equation, or the exact anomaly class;
    \item a bulk-to-boundary kernel for the brane;
    \item trace normalisation and large-$N$ weighting conventions;
    \item a convention for extracting connected single-trace and multi-trace
      coefficients.
  \end{enumerate}
  The computations in these notes use the formal local algebra of polynomial
  Hamiltonians. They are independent of the analytic choices above.
\end{prob}

For the Hamiltonian sector of the closed string field theory we replace
$\Ker\partial$ (on poly-1-vectors) by the Dolbeault-valued Hamiltonian vector
fields:
$$\Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)\text{-valued Hamiltonian vector fields},$$
i.e.\ sections of $\Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)$ identified with Hamiltonian vector
fields via the symplectic form $\omega = dz_1\wedge dz_2$. Concretely, a
section $f\in\Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)$ corresponds to the Hamiltonian vector field
$X_f=\{f,-\}_\omega$, where
$$\{f,g\}_\omega=
  \partial_{z_1}f\,\partial_{z_2}g-\partial_{z_2}f\,\partial_{z_1}g.$$
With this convention
$$X_f=(\partial_{z_1}f)\partial_{z_2}
  -(\partial_{z_2}f)\partial_{z_1},\qquad \iota_{X_f}\omega=-df.$$

\begin{rmk}
  The replacement is the elementary statement
  of Proposition~\ref{prop:hamiltonian-polyvector-reduction}. Constants are
  removed because $X_f=0$ exactly when $f$ is constant.
\end{rmk}

\subsection{Closed String Field Theory}

\subsubsection{The Action Functional}

Let
$$D=d_{\R^2}+\bar\partial_{\C^2}.$$
Write
$$\alpha\in\Omega^*(\R^2)\,\hat{\otimes}\,\Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)\otimes\bar A[1]$$
for the Hamiltonian connection field and
$$\beta\in\Omega^*(\R^2)\,\hat{\otimes}\,\Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)\otimes\bar A^{\vee}[2]$$
for its cotangent field. The bracket on form-valued Hamiltonians is obtained
by wedging the form parts and taking the Poisson bracket of the Hamiltonian
parts with the tensor-product Koszul sign:
$$[\eta\otimes f,\theta\otimes g]
  =(-1)^{|f||\theta|}(\eta\wedge\theta)\otimes\{f,g\},$$
for homogeneous form $\eta,\theta$ and homogeneous Hamiltonian coefficients
$f,g$. In the ghost-zero Hamiltonian calculations used below the coefficients
are even, so this sign is $+1$. The action is
$$S=\int_{\R^2\times\C^2}\Bigl\langle\beta,\,
  D\alpha+\frac12\{\alpha,\alpha\}\Bigr\rangle\,dz_1\,dz_2.$$
The pairing is the evaluation pairing between $\bar A^{\vee}$ and $\bar A$
(or, in a formal residue model, the corresponding continuous residue pairing).
For ghost-number-zero fields, $\alpha$ has total real/Dolbeault form degree
$1$ and $\beta$ has total degree $2$. Hence
$\beta\wedge D\alpha$ and $\beta\wedge\{\alpha,\alpha\}$ have total
real/Dolbeault degree $4$; multiplying by $dz_1\wedge dz_2$ gives a top real
degree $6$ form on $\R^2\times\C^2$.

\begin{rmk}
  The open quantum-mechanical system lives on the brane line. The closed
  Hamiltonian BF theory lives on the full space-time.
\end{rmk}

\subsubsection{Classical field content}

This theory is closely related to a 4-dimensional BF theory: $\beta$ plays the
role of the $B$-field (Lagrange multiplier) and the equations of motion of
$\beta$ enforce $F_\alpha = 0$, where
$$F_\alpha = D\alpha + \tfrac{1}{2}\{\alpha,\alpha\}$$
is the ``curvature'' of $\alpha$ (viewed as a connection on a bundle of
holomorphic-symplectic surfaces over $\R^2$).

Consider the space-time $\R^2_{x_i}\times\C^2_{z_i}$. The ghost-number-zero
components of $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are:
$$\alpha = \alpha_{x_i}\,dx_i + \alpha_{\bar z_j}\,d\bar z_j,\qquad
  \beta = \beta_{x_1 x_2}\,dx_1\,dx_2 + \beta_{x_i\bar z_j}\,dx_i\,d\bar z_j
  + \beta_{\bar z_1\bar z_2}\,d\bar z_1\,d\bar z_2.$$
Here the coefficients of $\alpha$ are Hamiltonian functions modulo constants.
The gauge symmetries are the BF-type transformations
$$\delta_{\varepsilon}\alpha=D\varepsilon+\{\alpha,\varepsilon\},\qquad
  \delta_{\varepsilon}\beta=\operatorname{ad}^{\vee}_{\varepsilon}\beta,$$
and
$$\delta_{\lambda}\alpha=0,\qquad
  \delta_{\lambda}\beta=D_{\alpha}\lambda,$$
where $D_{\alpha}=D+\{\alpha,-\}$ and $\lambda$ is a one-form valued in
$\bar A^{\vee}$. The second symmetry is reducible on shell: if
$\lambda=D_{\alpha}\mu$, then
$D_{\alpha}\lambda=D_{\alpha}^2\mu=\{F_{\alpha},\mu\}$, which vanishes when
$F_{\alpha}=0$.

Analysing $\alpha$ as a Hamiltonian function on $\C^2$: the component
$\alpha_{\bar z_j}d\bar z_j$ is the Hamiltonian potential whose vector field
$X_{\alpha_{\bar z_j}}d\bar z_j$ is the Beltrami differential encoding a
deformation of the complex structure of $\C^2$; the component
$\alpha_{x_i}dx_i$ similarly defines a connection on $\R^2$ valued in
Hamiltonian vector fields on $\C^2$, encoding a bundle of $\C^2$s over
$\R^2$. Together, the
equation of motion $F_\alpha=0$ says this data constitutes an integrable
deformation of complex structure with flat holonomy. The field $\beta$ appears
linearly in the action and enforces exactly this equation.

\begin{rmk}
  This is entirely analogous to BF theory, where $B$ is a Lagrange multiplier
  enforcing $F_A=0$, and the phase space is $T^*\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{flat}}$.
\end{rmk}

\subsubsection{Phase Space}
Formally compactify one real direction and put the perturbative local model on
$\R\times S^1\times X$, where $X$ is holomorphic symplectic and the calculation
is completed at the trivial solution. The equations of motion $F_\alpha=0$ cut
out the Maurer-Cartan moduli problem for Hamiltonian vector fields on
$S^1\times X$. Fix the analytic package of
Problem~\ref{prob:analytic-graph-realization} in this compact or formal
setting: elliptic complex, boundary condition, gauge fixing, propagator,
renormalization scheme, and anomaly cancellation. The perturbative phase space
is the derived cotangent stack
$$T^*_{\mathrm{der}}\mathrm{MC}\bigl(\Omega^*(S^1)\widehat{\otimes}
  \Omega^{0,*}(X,\mathrm{Ham}(X)),D,\{-,-\}\bigr).$$
After passing to the underived tangent space at the trivial solution this
reduces to the ordinary cotangent bundle of the linearized deformation complex.

\subsection{Closed String Fields and Brane Deformations}

The local classical bulk-boundary operation is evaluation of a closed
Hamiltonian on the brane normal fields. If the brane lies along $x_1$ and the
closed string field is $dx_1\,z_1^k z_2^l$, the local boundary functional is
$$\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2+A[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr)
  + \int_\R dx_1\,\mathrm{Tr}\,\phi_1^k\phi_2^l.$$
This is a statement inside the formal local algebraic model: the closed
Hamiltonian is pulled back along the matrix-valued normal coordinate map
$z_i\mapsto\phi_i$ and then traced.
Construction~\ref{constr:boundary-evaluation} is the algebraic form of this
bulk-to-boundary prescription, and Theorem~\ref{thm:bulk-boundary} below proves
that the resulting Hamiltonian evaluation preserves the classical bracket.

\begin{rmk}
  Geometrically, specifying a closed string field on $\C^2$ amounts to
  equipping $\C^2$ with a connection, so that the brane can parallel-transport
  its internal degrees of freedom as it moves along $\R$.
\end{rmk}

\subsection{Operators in the Gauge Theory and the Large N Limit}

The local-operator calculation is performed in the reduced BV model with
fields $\phi_1,\phi_2$ and the antifield $\psi=A^*$ for the Lagrange
multiplier $A$. We take $GL_N$-invariants in characteristic zero, so the
Chevalley part of the gauge BRST differential acts trivially on trace
functions by cyclicity.

\begin{constr}[The reduced BV algebra at the insertion point]\label{constr:reduced-bv-algebra}
  At a fixed point of the brane line, introduce three matrix variables
  $$\phi_1,\phi_2\in\lie{gl}_N,\qquad \psi\in\lie{gl}_N[1].$$
  The reduced local polynomial algebra is
  $$\mc R_N=\C[(\phi_1)^i{}_j,(\phi_2)^i{}_j]
    \otimes\Lambda\bigl((\psi)^i{}_j\bigr).$$
  Its BRST differential is the derivation determined on generators by
  $$Q(\phi_1)^i{}_j=0,\qquad Q(\phi_2)^i{}_j=0,\qquad
    Q(\psi)^i{}_j=([\phi_1,\phi_2])^i{}_j.$$
  This formula is the algebraic shadow of the $A$-equation of motion:
  varying
  $$\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2+A[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr)$$
  with respect to $A$ gives $[\phi_1,\phi_2]=0$, and the antifield $\psi=A^*$
  records that equation as a Koszul differential.
\end{constr}

\begin{lem}[De Rham jets]\label{lem:derivative-jets}
  Positive jets in the topological direction vanish in local
  operator cohomology. Equivalently, every cohomology class has a representative
  determined by the values of the fields and antifields at the insertion point.
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  For a coordinate $t$ on the line, the de Rham part of the BRST operator is
  $d_{\R}$. On the jet algebra one has the contraction homotopy
  $[d_{\R},\iota_{\partial_t}]=\partial_t$. Hence a local expression containing
  a positive $t$-derivative is cohomologous to zero after applying the usual
  Euler homotopy on jet degree. This kills topological-direction jets and leaves
  the holomorphic $z_i$-derivatives used later on the closed side.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[Stable trace invariants]\label{prop:stable-trace-invariants}
  Let $F=\C\langle x_1,x_2\rangle$ and let $\mathrm{Cyc}(F)=F/[F,F]$ be the
  vector space of cyclic words. For each $N$, $GL_N$ acts on
  $\lie{gl}_N^2$ by simultaneous conjugation. The degreewise stable invariant
  algebra
  $$\C[\lie{gl}_\infty^2]^{GL_\infty}_{\mathrm{st}}
    :=\bigoplus_{d\geq0}\varprojlim_N
      \bigl(\C[\lie{gl}_N^2]^{GL_N}\bigr)_d$$
  with respect to block-restriction maps in each word-degree
  is the free commutative algebra generated by the nonempty cyclic words:
  $$\C[\lie{gl}_\infty^2]^{GL_\infty}_{\mathrm{st}}
    \cong\mathbf S\bigl(\mathrm{Cyc}(F)_{\mathrm{nonempty}}\bigr),$$
  where a cyclic word $w$ maps to the trace function
  $(\phi_1,\phi_2)\mapsto\mathrm{Tr}\,w(\phi_1,\phi_2)$.
  More precisely, in total positive word-degree $d$ this map is an isomorphism
  for every $N\geq d$, after removing the scalar empty trace at finite $N$.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Procesi--Razmyslov invariant theory \cites{procesi}{razmyslov} gives two
  inputs. First, $\C[\lie{gl}_N^2]^{GL_N}$ is generated by trace functions of
  words in the two generic matrices. Second, all polynomial relations among
  these trace functions are consequences of the fundamental trace identities
  of matrix size $N$; in particular every homogeneous trace relation has total
  word-degree at least $N+1$. Fix a total word-degree $d$. For every
  $N\geq d$ the degree-$d$ relations are exactly cyclicity of the trace.
  Passing degree by degree to the stable inverse limit removes
  all finite-$N$ trace identities. The surviving identification is
  $\mathrm{Tr}(uv)=\mathrm{Tr}(vu)$. This is exactly the symmetric algebra on
  cyclic words.

  For the one- and two-$\psi$ sectors used below, start with a third even
  generic matrix, take the part linear or quadratic in that third letter, and
  then skew-symmetrise the marked letters. This is the same as declaring the
  marked letter $\psi$ odd. The stable single-trace relation is graded
  cyclicity: moving one $\psi$ through the trace has sign $+1$, while moving
  one $\psi$ past another gives a minus sign. These are exactly the cyclic
  signs used in the finite complex below.
\end{proof}

\begin{rmk}
  The empty trace is the finite-rank scalar $\mathrm{Tr}_N(1)=N$, which changes
  with $N$ and fails to define a stable primitive generator. We remove this
  scalar line before passing to the degreewise stable limit. The class
  $\mathrm{Tr}(\psi)$ is
  a genuine stable odd primitive class. It is excluded from the Hamiltonian
  comparison because constants have been removed from the closed Hamiltonian
  algebra.
\end{rmk}

Varying
$$S=\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2+A[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr)$$
with respect to $A$ gives the moment-map equation
$[\phi_1,\phi_2]=0$. In the reduced BV complex,
$$Q\psi=[\phi_1,\phi_2],\qquad Q\phi_1=Q\phi_2=0$$
on invariant trace functions.

\begin{constr}[Boundary evaluation of a closed Hamiltonian]\label{constr:boundary-evaluation}
  Let $f=\sum_{k,l}c_{k,l}z_1^kz_2^l\in\C[z_1,z_2]$. The brane replaces the
  two normal coordinates $z_1,z_2$ by the two adjoint scalar fields
  $\phi_1,\phi_2$. Choosing the ordered lift with all $\phi_1$'s to the left
  of all $\phi_2$'s gives the invariant function
  $$\widetilde\partial_{\mathrm{bb},N}(f)
    =\sum_{k,l}c_{k,l}\,\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)
    \in\mc R_N^{GL_N}.$$
  Let
  $$H^0_{\mathrm{red}}(\mc R_N^{GL_N},Q)
    :=H^0(\mc R_N^{GL_N},Q)/\C\cdot\mathrm{Tr}(1).$$
  Constants give the scalar $c_{0,0}N$ and therefore vanish in this quotient.
  Thus the construction descends to a linear map
  $$\partial_{\mathrm{bb},N}:\bar A\to
    H^0_{\mathrm{red}}(\mc R_N^{GL_N},Q),\qquad
    [f]\longmapsto \overline{\widetilde\partial_{\mathrm{bb},N}(f)}.$$
  Let $\mathsf W_{k,l}$ be the set of words in two letters containing $k$
  copies of the first letter and $l$ copies of the second. The first descendant
  is obtained by inserting one Koszul variable and summing over all ordered
  lifts:
  For $k+l>0$ the descendant part of the Hamiltonian comparison is
  $$\partial_{\mathrm{bb},N}^{(1)}(\delta_{k,l}[1])=\Psi_{k,l},\qquad
    \Psi_{k,l}:=\sum_{w\in\mathsf W_{k,l}}
      \mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\psi\,w(\phi_1,\phi_2)\bigr).$$
  The same formula at $(k,l)=(0,0)$ gives the decoupled scalar antifield
  $\mathrm{Tr}(\psi)$, which is excluded from the Hamiltonian pairing because
  constants have been removed from $\bar A$.
\end{constr}

\begin{lem}[First descendants are cycles]\label{lem:first-descendant-cycles}
  For every $k,l\geq0$, the symmetrised trace $\Psi_{k,l}$ satisfies
  $Q\Psi_{k,l}=0$.
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  Write $x=\phi_1$ and $y=\phi_2$. Since $x$ and $y$ are even and
  $Q\psi=[x,y]$, one has
  $$Q\Psi_{k,l}
    =\sum_{w\in\mathsf W_{k,l}}\mathrm{Tr}\bigl((xy-yx)w(x,y)\bigr).$$
  Group the resulting traces by cyclic word of multidegree $(k+1,l+1)$. The
  coefficient of a cyclic word in the positive sum is the number of cyclic
  adjacent occurrences of the block $xy$; the coefficient in the negative sum
  is the number of cyclic adjacent occurrences of the block $yx$. On a circle
  the number of transitions from an $x$-run to a $y$-run equals the number of
  transitions from a $y$-run to an $x$-run. The two grouped sums are therefore
  equal, so $Q\Psi_{k,l}=0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[Primitive one-antifield Koszul homology]\label{prop:one-psi-homology}
  Fix $k,l\geq0$. The middle homology of the primitive single-trace complex
  with one displayed $\psi$ and $k,l$ displayed $x,y$ letters is
  one-dimensional, generated by the class of $\Psi_{k,l}$. The nonconstant part
  relevant to the Hamiltonian comparison is the direct sum over $k+l>0$.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Write $x=\phi_1$ and $y=\phi_2$. Let $\mathsf W_{k,l}$ be the set of ordinary
  words with $k$ letters $x$ and $l$ letters $y$, and let
  $\mathsf N_{a,b}$ be the set of cyclic words with $a$ letters $x$ and $b$
  letters $y$. Primitive cyclic words are taken modulo the graded cyclic rule
  $$\mathrm{Tr}(ab)=(-1)^{|a||b|}\mathrm{Tr}(ba),\qquad
  |x|=|y|=0,\quad |\psi|=1.$$
  Graded cyclicity gives a unique representative $\mathrm{Tr}(\psi w)$ for
  every one-$\psi$ cyclic word. Thus the relevant one-$\psi$ chain group is
  $$C_1(k,l)=\C\{\mathsf W_{k,l}\}.$$
  The zero-$\psi$ target is
  $$C_0(k,l)=\C\{\mathsf N_{k+1,l+1}\},$$
  and the differential is the incidence map
  $$\partial_1(w)=[xyw]_{\mathrm{cyc}}-[yxw]_{\mathrm{cyc}}.$$

  The two-$\psi$ source is generated by traces $\mathrm{Tr}(\psi u\psi v)$
  with $u$ and $v$ ordinary words whose total bidegree is $(k-1,l-1)$; if
  $k=0$ or $l=0$ this source is zero. Since $\psi$ is odd, exchanging the two
  displayed $\psi$'s changes sign. Applying $Q\psi=xy-yx$ gives
  \begin{align*}
  \partial_2(u,v)
    &=Q\,\mathrm{Tr}(\psi u\psi v)\\
    &=\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\psi vxyu-\psi vyxu-\psi uxyv+\psi uyxv\bigr),
  \end{align*}
  so, after removing the common front $\psi$,
  $$\partial_2(u,v)=vxyu-vyxu-uxyv+uyxv.$$
  A direct substitution shows $\partial_1\partial_2=0$.

  If $k=0$ or $l=0$, then $C_1(k,l)$ is spanned by $x^k$ or $y^l$,
  $\partial_1=0$, and $C_2(k,l)=0$. Hence the middle homology is already the
  line spanned by $\Psi_{k,l}$. Assume from now on that $k,l>0$.

  We compute the first homology of this finite complex. Form a graph
  $\Gamma_{k,l}$ whose vertices are the necklaces in $\mathsf N_{k+1,l+1}$ and
  whose oriented edges are the words $w\in\mathsf W_{k,l}$, with
  $$w:[yxw]_{\mathrm{cyc}}\longrightarrow[xyw]_{\mathrm{cyc}}.$$
  Then $\partial_1$ is the cellular boundary of this graph. For each pair
  $(u,v)$ as above attach the square whose oriented boundary is
  $vxyu-vyxu-uxyv+uyxv$. Denote the resulting two-complex by $X_{k,l}$.

  Unwrap a necklace at a marked $y$. A vertex is then a tuple
  $$(a_0,\ldots,a_l),\qquad a_i\geq0,\qquad \sum_i a_i=k+1,$$
  where $a_i$ counts the number of $x$'s between the $i$th and $(i+1)$st
  $y$. An edge moves one $x$ across one neighbouring $y$; in these coordinates
  it replaces $a_i,a_{i+1}$ by $a_i-1,a_{i+1}+1$ when $a_i>0$. The attached
  squares say that two moves of two chosen $x$'s commute whenever both orders of
  the moves are defined.

  Lift the cyclic set of $y$-gaps from $\Z/(l+1)$ to $\Z$. The lifted complex
  has vertices
  $$p_0\leq\cdots\leq p_k,\qquad p_k-p_0\leq l,$$
  where $p_i$ records the lifted gap occupied by the $i$th $x$. Edges change one
  $p_i$ by one, preserving the displayed inequalities, and squares commute
  changes of two different coordinates. This is the cubical subdivision of the
  convex polyhedron
  $$\{(t_0,\ldots,t_k)\in\R^{k+1}:t_0\leq\cdots\leq t_k,\ t_k-t_0\leq l\},$$
  hence its first homology vanishes. The original necklace complex is the
  quotient by the free deck transformation
  $$(p_0,\ldots,p_k)\longmapsto(p_0+l+1,\ldots,p_k+l+1),$$
  which moves all $x$'s once around the circle of $y$-gaps. Therefore
  $H_1(X_{k,l};\C)$ is one-dimensional. The cellular one-cycle
  $\sum_{w\in\mathsf W_{k,l}}w$ lifts to a path from a vertex of the lifted
  complex to its deck translate, so it is the generator of this deck-circle
  homology.

  Lemma~\ref{lem:first-descendant-cycles} identifies this representative as a
  cycle in the original trace complex. Its non-boundary property follows from
  the functional
  $$\epsilon\bigl(\mathrm{Tr}(\psi w)\bigr)=1,\qquad w\in\mathsf W_{k,l},$$
  kills every displayed two-$\psi$ boundary because each boundary has two
  positive and two negative summands, while
  $$\epsilon(\Psi_{k,l})=\binom{k+l}{k}\neq0.$$
  Thus the one-$\psi$ homology in bidegree $(k,l)$ is the one-dimensional line
  generated by $[\Psi_{k,l}]$.
\end{proof}

\begin{cor}[Boundary pairing for cotangent generators]\label{cor:cotangent-boundary-pairing}
  Let $\delta_{k,l}\in\mathfrak h^\vee$ be the functional dual to the monomial
  $z_1^kz_2^l\in\bar A$. The assignment
  $$\delta_{k,l}[1]\longmapsto [\Psi_{k,l}]$$
  extends to an isomorphism from the shifted Taylor-dual space
  $\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$ to the nonconstant primitive one-$\psi$ brane homology.
\end{cor}

\begin{proof}
  The monomials $z_1^kz_2^l$ with $k+l>0$ form a topological basis of
  $\bar A$, and the $\delta_{k,l}$ form the continuous dual basis. By
  Proposition~\ref{prop:one-psi-homology}, the primitive one-$\psi$ homology has
  one generator in each bidegree, namely $[\Psi_{k,l}]$. Discarding the scalar
  bidegree $(0,0)$ and sending the dual basis vector in every nonconstant
  bidegree to that generator gives a filtered vector-space isomorphism. This is
  the boundary pairing used in the PBW special fibre; the closed module
  structure remains coadjoint.
\end{proof}

\begin{lem}[Adjacent swaps]\label{lem:adjacent-swaps}
  In the invariant reduced BV cohomology,
  $$\mathrm{Tr}(u\phi_1\phi_2v)-\mathrm{Tr}(u\phi_2\phi_1v)$$
  is $Q$-exact for all words $u,v$.
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  By cyclicity of the trace,
  \begin{align*}
  Q\,\mathrm{Tr}(\psi vu)
    &=\mathrm{Tr}([\phi_1,\phi_2]vu)\\
    &=\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\phi_2vu-\phi_2\phi_1vu)\\
    &=\mathrm{Tr}(u\phi_1\phi_2v)-\mathrm{Tr}(u\phi_2\phi_1v).
  \end{align*}
\end{proof}

\begin{cor}[Matrix evaluation is commutative in cohomology]\label{cor:matrix-evaluation-well-defined}
  If two words in $\phi_1,\phi_2$ have the same numbers of $\phi_1$ and
  $\phi_2$, then their traces define the same class in the reduced BV
  cohomology. Consequently $\partial_{\mathrm{bb},N}(f)$ is determined by the
  commutative polynomial $f\in\C[z_1,z_2]$ and is independent of the chosen
  ordered lift.
\end{cor}

\begin{proof}
  Adjacent transpositions generate all reorderings of a finite word. Each
  adjacent transposition of the adjacent letters $\phi_1,\phi_2$ changes the
  trace by a $Q$-exact term by Lemma~\ref{lem:adjacent-swaps}; cyclicity moves
  the chosen adjacent pair into the displayed position of that lemma. Summing
  the resulting exact differences proves the claim.
\end{proof}

Let $\bar A=\C[z_1,z_2]/\C\cdot1$. The removed constant corresponds on the
gauge side to the scalar $\mathrm{Tr}(1)=N$ and has zero Hamiltonian vector
field.

\begin{prop}[Brane Hamiltonian sector]\label{prop:brane-ops}
  In the stable large-$N$ scalar-reduced BV model, the Hamiltonian descendant
  algebra freely generated by the nonconstant primitive ghost-zero traces and
  the nonconstant primitive one-$\psi$ Koszul homology is
  $$\mc{O}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{brane}}
    \cong \mathbf{S}\bigl(\bar A\oplus \bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}[1]\bigr).$$
  The class of $z_1^kz_2^l$ with $k+l>0$ corresponds to
  $\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)}$. The corresponding descendant
  basis vector corresponds to the symmetrised trace with one $\psi$ and $k,l$
  copies of $\phi_1,\phi_2$.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Proposition~\ref{prop:stable-trace-invariants} gives the free commutative
  algebra on cyclic words.
  Lemma~\ref{lem:adjacent-swaps} identifies any two cyclic words with the same
  numbers of $\phi_1$ and $\phi_2$, because adjacent transpositions generate
  all reorderings. Thus the scalar-reduced ghost-zero cyclic words reduce to
  the monomials $\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)}$ with $k+l>0$.
  Proposition~\ref{prop:one-psi-homology} computes the primitive one-$\psi$
  homology in each multidegree; the nonconstant generators are $\Psi_{k,l}$ with
  $k+l>0$. Products of the scalar-reduced ghost-zero primitive classes and these
  one-$\psi$ primitive classes give the free graded-commutative multi-trace
  algebra in the Hamiltonian sector.
  By definition of the Hamiltonian sector, single traces containing two or
  more occurrences of $\psi$ are excluded from this algebra.
\end{proof}

\begin{rmk}
  The word ``sector'' is literal. The calculation above presents the stable
  algebra generated by the primitive no-$\psi$ Hamiltonians and the nonconstant
  primitive one-$\psi$ homology generated by $\Psi_{k,l}$ for $k+l>0$. The full
  invariant Koszul homology in higher $\psi$-count lies outside this sector, as
  does the decoupled scalar class $\mathrm{Tr}(\psi)$.
\end{rmk}

\begin{prob}[Full primitive Koszul homology]\label{prob:full-psi-homology}
  Let
  $$A_{\psi}=\C\langle x,y,\psi\rangle,\qquad
    |x|=|y|=0,\quad |\psi|=1,$$
  with differential $d\psi=xy-yx$ and $dx=dy=0$. Put
  $$\mathrm{Cyc}(A_{\psi})=A_{\psi}/[A_{\psi},A_{\psi}]_{\mathrm{gr}},\qquad
    [uv]=(-1)^{|u||v|}[vu].$$
  Give $x,y,\psi$ weights
  $$\mathrm{wt}(x)=(1,0),\qquad \mathrm{wt}(y)=(0,1),\qquad
    \mathrm{wt}(\psi)=(1,1).$$
  For fixed $(R,S)$ define
  $$K^p_{R,S}=\C\{[w]_{\mathrm{cyc}}:
    \#\psi(w)=p,\ \#x(w)=R-p,\ \#y(w)=S-p\},$$
  with $0\leq p\leq\min(R,S)$. The differential is
  \begin{align*}
  \partial[w]
    =\sum_{i:a_i=\psi}(-1)^{\#\{\psi\text{ before }i\}}
    [&a_1\cdots a_{i-1}xy\,a_{i+1}\cdots a_n\\
     &-a_1\cdots a_{i-1}yx\,a_{i+1}\cdots a_n]_{\mathrm{cyc}}.
  \end{align*}
  Prove the primitive homology formula
  $$H_p(K_{R,S})\cong
    \begin{cases}
      \C\cdot[x^Ry^S],&p=0,\\
      \C\cdot[\Psi_{R-1,S-1}],&p=1,\ R,S\geq1,\\
      0,&p\geq2.
    \end{cases}$$
  Equivalently, prove that the primitive stable Koszul homology is
  $\C[x,y]\oplus\C[x,y]\theta$ with $|\theta|=1$ and
  $\mathrm{wt}(\theta)=(1,1)$.

  The required new ingredient is the all-$p$ orientation statement identifying this
  graded cyclic complex with the cellular chains of the cubical necklace
  complex whose universal cover is contractible and whose deck quotient has
  homotopy type $S^1$. Proposition~\ref{prop:one-psi-homology} proves exactly
  the $p\leq2$ skeleton needed for the Hamiltonian sector. The complete theorem
  also requires the all-$\psi$ stable trace presentation and an exact
  computational check of the finite complex.
\end{prob}

\subsection{Operators in the Gravitational Theory}\label{sec:grav-ops}

Let $\mathfrak h=\widehat{\mathfrak h}=(z_1,z_2)\C[[z_1,z_2]]$ with Poisson
bracket, and let $\mathfrak h^\vee$ be its continuous Taylor dual. The
cotangent field is a coadjoint module, so the local Hamiltonian Lie algebra is
$$\mathfrak g=\mathfrak h\ltimes\mathfrak h^\vee[1].$$
For $f,g\in\mathfrak h$ and $\lambda,\mu\in\mathfrak h^\vee$, the brackets are
$$[f,g]=\{f,g\},\qquad [f,\eta_\lambda]=\eta_{\operatorname{ad}^{\vee}_f\lambda},
  \qquad [\eta_\lambda,\eta_\mu]=0,$$
where $(\operatorname{ad}^{\vee}_f\lambda)(g)=-\lambda(\{f,g\})$.
On polynomial monomials in the dense subalgebra $\bar A$ this reads
$$[z_1^kz_2^l,z_1^mz_2^n]
  =\overline{(kn-lm)z_1^{k+m-1}z_2^{l+n-1}},$$
where the bar means projection to $\bar A$; in particular the constant bracket
of $z_1$ and $z_2$ is zero in the reduced Hamiltonian Lie algebra.

\begin{lem}[Shifted coadjoint signs]\label{lem:shifted-coadjoint-signs}
  Let $\eta_\lambda$ denote the suspension of
  $\lambda\in\mathfrak h^\vee$ into $\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$. Since the
  Hamiltonians in $\mathfrak h$ are even,
  $$[f,\eta_\lambda]=\eta_{\operatorname{ad}^{\vee}_f\lambda},\qquad
    [\eta_\lambda,\eta_\mu]=0.$$
  Consequently the enveloping algebra relations involving two shifted
  coadjoint generators are odd-commutative:
  $$\eta_\lambda\eta_\mu+\eta_\mu\eta_\lambda=0.$$
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  The semidirect product bracket before shifting is the ordinary coadjoint
  action of the even Lie algebra $\mathfrak h$ on its continuous dual. Suspending
  the module raises the degree of every $\lambda$ by one. The mixed-bracket sign
  is $+1$ because $|f|=0$. The shifted module is
  abelian as a Lie ideal, so its bracket with itself is zero; in the associative
  enveloping algebra this zero Lie bracket gives
  $\eta_\lambda\eta_\mu-(-1)^{1\cdot1}\eta_\mu\eta_\lambda=0$.
\end{proof}

The kinematic BRST operator is
$$Q_0=d_{\R^2}+\bar\partial_{\C^2}.$$
The same jet contraction as in Lemma~\ref{lem:derivative-jets}, together with
the Dolbeault Poincare lemma in the formal local model, leaves precisely the
holomorphic Taylor coefficients. Thus the linear local coordinates are the
functionals
$$\partial_{z_1}^k\partial_{z_2}^l\alpha|_0,\qquad
  \partial_{z_1}^k\partial_{z_2}^l\beta|_0.$$
These are dual to the Taylor monomials $z_1^kz_2^l$ up to the factorial
normalisation fixed by the chosen pairing. Since zero-form components of
$\alpha$ and $\beta$ have negative field ghost numbers, the corresponding
linear functions have positive ghost numbers.

\begin{prop}[Closed local operators are continuous CE cochains]\label{prop:grav-ops}
  The classical local operators of the formal Hamiltonian cotangent BF sector are
  the continuous Chevalley-Eilenberg cochains
  $$\mc{O}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{grav}}
    \cong C^\bullet_{\mathrm{CE},\mathrm{cont}}(\mathfrak g)
    =\widehat{\mathbf{S}}\bigl(\mathfrak g^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}[-1]\bigr),$$
  with CE differential dual to the Lie brackets above.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Work at the formal insertion point. The jet algebra of the fields is the
  completed free graded-commutative algebra on the Taylor coefficients of
  $\alpha$ and $\beta$. The kinematic differential
  $Q_0=d_{\R^2}+\bar\partial_{\C^2}$ contracts all positive real and
  anti-holomorphic jets: in the real directions the homotopy is
  $[d_{\R^2},\iota_{\partial_{x_i}}]=\partial_{x_i}$, and in the
  anti-holomorphic directions it is the formal Dolbeault Poincare homotopy.
  The $Q_0$-cohomology of the jet algebra is therefore generated by the
  holomorphic Taylor coefficients alone.

  The remaining $\alpha$-coefficients are Taylor Hamiltonians modulo constants,
  hence elements of $\mathfrak h=\widehat{\mathfrak h}$; degreewise polynomial
  coefficients form the dense subalgebra $\bar A$. The cotangent field $\beta$
  supplies the odd shifted continuous dual $\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$. Thus the
  linear space of residual local modes is
  $\mathfrak g=\mathfrak h\ltimes\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$.

  The nonlinear BRST differential is determined by the BV Hamiltonian
  $$S=\int\left\langle\beta,D\alpha+\frac12\{\alpha,\alpha\}\right\rangle.$$
  The linear part is exactly $Q_0$, already killed in cohomology. The quadratic
  term sends two Hamiltonian $\alpha$-modes $f,g$ to their reduced Poisson
  bracket $\{f,g\}$, and the action on the cotangent variable is the coadjoint
  action displayed above. The quadratic term has zero component with two
  cotangent inputs, so $[\eta_\lambda,\eta_\mu]=0$. Hence the residual BRST differential on
  functions is precisely the derivation dual to the Lie bracket on
  $\mathfrak g$, which is the Chevalley-Eilenberg differential. Continuity
  records the completion over polynomial degree.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Koszul Duality}

\begin{defn}
  For an augmented complete dg algebra $B$, its Koszul dual is
  $$B^!=\RHom_B(\C,\C).$$
  We regard commutative Chevalley-Eilenberg cochains as $E_1$ algebras by
  restriction. All bar and cobar constructions below are taken in the category
  of complete filtered Tate vector spaces, with continuous maps. The
  completions are by symmetric or tensor length together with the coefficient
  topology coming from Taylor degree.
\end{defn}

\begin{lem}[Tate continuous bar-cobar]\label{lem:continuous-bar-cobar}
  Let $V=\prod_{d\geq1}V_d$ and $W=\bigoplus_{d\geq1}W_d$, with all $V_d,W_d$
  finite dimensional. Then
  $$(V\oplus W)^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}
    =\bigoplus_{d\geq1}V_d^\vee\oplus\prod_{d\geq1}W_d^\vee.$$
  Let $\mathfrak g$ be a Tate graded Lie algebra whose bracket is continuous for
  such a coefficient topology and whose finite coefficient windows are finite
  dimensional. Then
  $$C^\bullet_{\mathrm{CE},\mathrm{cont}}(\mathfrak g)
    =\Hom_{\mathrm{cont}}\bigl(\mathbf S^c(\mathfrak g[1]),\C\bigr)
    =\widehat{\mathbf S}\bigl(\mathfrak g^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}[-1]\bigr).$$
  The continuous bar resolution of the augmentation has dual cobar algebra
  $\Omega C_*^{\mathrm{CE}}(\mathfrak g)$, and the canonical Lie bar-cobar map
  $$\Omega C_*^{\mathrm{CE}}(\mathfrak g)\longrightarrow \widehat U(\mathfrak g)$$
  is a filtered quasi-isomorphism. Its associated graded map is the completed
  PBW isomorphism
  $$\operatorname{gr}\widehat U(\mathfrak g)\cong\widehat{\mathbf S}(\mathfrak g).$$
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  A continuous functional on $\prod V_d$ depends on finitely many homogeneous
  coordinates. A continuous functional on the discrete direct sum
  $\bigoplus W_d$ is an arbitrary compatible product of degreewise functionals.
  This proves the displayed duality formula.

  Filter the CE coalgebra by symmetric coalgebra length and by finite
  coefficient windows. After projecting to a fixed output window and bounding
  tensor length, continuity of the bracket says that finitely many
  homogeneous input coefficients can contribute. The corresponding finite
  diagram is the ordinary finite-dimensional Lie bar-cobar comparison, where
  the bar resolution computes
  $\RHom_{C^\bullet_{\mathrm{CE}}}(\C,\C)$ and the cobar algebra maps to the
  universal enveloping algebra. These finite comparisons are compatible under
  enlargement of the window. Passing to the Tate limit and completing in tensor
  length gives the asserted continuous cobar algebra. The PBW filtration by
  tensor length has associated graded the completed symmetric algebra, so the
  completed comparison is a filtered quasi-isomorphism.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[Koszul dual of continuous CE cochains]\label{prop:ce-koszul}
  Let $\mathfrak g=\mathfrak h\ltimes\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$ be the completed
  graded Lie algebra above, and let
  $$B=C^\bullet_{\mathrm{CE},\mathrm{cont}}(\mathfrak g)
    =\widehat{\mathbf{S}}\bigl(\mathfrak g^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}[-1]\bigr).$$
  Then
  $$B^!\simeq \widehat{U(\mathfrak g)},$$
  the completed graded universal enveloping algebra.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Put
  $$H_d=\operatorname{Span}\{z_1^az_2^b:a+b=d\},\qquad
    \mathfrak h=\prod_{d\geq1}H_d,\qquad
    \mathfrak h^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}=\bigoplus_{d\geq1}H_d^\vee.$$
  Thus
  $$\mathfrak g=\left(\prod_{d\geq1}H_d\right)
    \ltimes\left(\bigoplus_{d\geq1}H_d^\vee\right)[1]$$
  is a Tate Lie algebra. The bracket is continuous: the degree-$r$ coefficient
  of $\{f,g\}$ is determined by Taylor coefficients of $f$ and $g$ of degrees at
  most $r+1$, since $\partial_{z_1}$ and $\partial_{z_2}$ each lower degree by
  one. The coadjoint action is continuous by the same finite-degree test. This
  Taylor topology is independent of the PBW/order filtration used for the Rees
  algebra below.

  Let
  $$C_*^{\mathrm{CE}}(\mathfrak g)=\mathbf S^c(\mathfrak g[1])$$
  be the Chevalley-Eilenberg coalgebra, with coderivation determined by the
  bracket of $\mathfrak g$. By definition
  $$B=\Hom_{\mathrm{cont}}\bigl(C_*^{\mathrm{CE}}(\mathfrak g),\C\bigr).$$
  The coaugmentation filtration on $C_*^{\mathrm{CE}}(\mathfrak g)$ is
  conilpotent in each finite Taylor window, and its completed Tate limit is
  pronilpotent. The augmentation $B\to\C$ has the continuous bar resolution, and
  Lemma~\ref{lem:continuous-bar-cobar} identifies
  $$\RHom_B(\C,\C)\simeq
    \Omega\,C_*^{\mathrm{CE}}(\mathfrak g),$$
  the completed cobar algebra of the CE coalgebra. The completed Lie bar-cobar
  comparison in the lemma identifies this cobar algebra with the completed
  associative algebra generated by $\mathfrak g$ modulo the closed graded Lie
  relations; this is $\widehat{U(\mathfrak g)}$.

  Thus, for the generators $f,g\in\mathfrak h$ and
  $\eta_\lambda,\eta_\mu\in\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$, the completed enveloping
  algebra has relations
  \begin{align*}
    fg-gf&=\{f,g\},\\
    f\eta_\lambda-\eta_\lambda f&=\eta_{\operatorname{ad}^{\vee}_f\lambda},\\
    \eta_\lambda\eta_\mu+\eta_\mu\eta_\lambda&=0.
  \end{align*}
\end{proof}

For comparison with the classical brane algebra introduce the completed
PBW/order Rees enveloping algebra
$$U_{\hbar}(\mathfrak g)
  =\widehat T(\mathfrak g)[[\hbar]]\Big/
  \overline{\left\langle
  xy-(-1)^{|x||y|}yx-\hbar[x,y]\right\rangle},$$
where the closure is for tensor length and the Tate coefficient topology.
This Rees parameter records word length in the enveloping algebra and is
independent of Taylor degree. At $\hbar=0$ one obtains the completed PBW special
fibre
$$U_{\hbar}(\mathfrak g)/(\hbar)\cong
  \widehat{\mathbf{S}}(\mathfrak g)
  =\widehat{\mathbf{S}}\bigl(\widehat{\mathfrak h}\oplus\mathfrak h^\vee[1]\bigr).$$
After choosing the monomial-dual Taylor basis
$\mathfrak h^\vee\cong\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}$, the degreewise polynomial
subalgebra $\mathbf{S}(\bar A\oplus\mathfrak h^\vee[1])$ is the stable classical
brane Hamiltonian descendant algebra of Proposition~\ref{prop:brane-ops}. Thus
the free, abelian closed special fibre is Koszul dual directly to this symmetric
product algebra. The interacting closed Hamiltonian bracket deforms the closed
special fibre to $\widehat{U(\mathfrak g)}$; on the brane side the deformation
checked in these notes is the degree-zero Hamiltonian trace bracket below.

\subsubsection{First-order corrections, order hbar one}

Under the Koszul duality correspondence we have
$$z_1^kz_2^l \leftrightarrow
  \overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)}.$$
The reduced Poisson bracket on the gravity side is:
$$\{z_1^kz_2^l,\, z_1^mz_2^n\}_{\bar A}
  =\overline{(kn - lm)\,z_1^{k+m-1}z_2^{l+n-1}}.$$
If an exponent in the displayed monomial is negative then the coefficient
$kn-lm$ is already zero; if the displayed monomial is constant, its class in
$\bar A$ is zero.
The matching bracket on the open side is the canonical bracket induced by the
first-order kinetic term $\int\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\,d\phi_2)$:
$$\{(\phi_1)^i{}_j,(\phi_2)^k{}_l\}
  =\delta^i_l\delta^k_j,\qquad
  \{(\phi_1)^i{}_j,(\phi_1)^k{}_l\}=0=\{(\phi_2)^i{}_j,(\phi_2)^k{}_l\}.$$

\begin{lem}[Hamiltonian reduction of the trace bracket]\label{lem:trace-bracket-descends}
  The canonical matrix Poisson bracket descends to the scalar-reduced
  invariant quotient used above.
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  For $\epsilon\in\lie{gl}_N$, the component
  $\mu_\epsilon=-\mathrm{Tr}(\epsilon[\phi_1,\phi_2])$ of the moment map
  generates the infinitesimal conjugation action, with the sign fixed by the
  convention for the bracket. Hence $\{F,\mu_\epsilon\}=0$ for every invariant
  function $F$. If $I_\mu$ is the ideal generated by all $\mu_\epsilon$, then
  $\{F,\mu_\epsilon K\}=\mu_\epsilon\{F,K\}$ lies again in $I_\mu$. Therefore
  the bracket of invariant functions descends to
  $(\mc R_N/I_\mu)^{GL_N}$, equivalently to the degree-zero reduced BV
  cohomology. The scalar line $\C\cdot\mathrm{Tr}(1)$ is central, so quotienting
  by it is also compatible with the bracket.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[First-order trace bracket]\label{prop:first-order-bracket}
  In the stable scalar-reduced cohomology,
  $$\{\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)},
      \overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^m\phi_2^n)}\}
    =\overline{(kn-lm)\mathrm{Tr}
      (\phi_1^{k+m-1}\phi_2^{l+n-1})}.$$
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Write $F_{k,l}=\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)$. The matrix Poisson bracket
  induced by
  $$\{(\phi_1)^i{}_j,(\phi_2)^k{}_l\}=\delta^i_l\delta^k_j$$
  is
  $$\{F,G\}=\mathrm{Tr}\left(
      \frac{\partial F}{\partial\phi_1}\frac{\partial G}{\partial\phi_2}
      -\frac{\partial F}{\partial\phi_2}\frac{\partial G}{\partial\phi_1}
    \right),$$
  where the derivatives are cyclic matrix derivatives. Varying one occurrence
  at a time gives
  \begin{align*}
  \frac{\partial F_{k,l}}{\partial\phi_1}
    &=\sum_{a=0}^{k-1}\phi_1^{k-1-a}\phi_2^l\phi_1^a,\\
  \frac{\partial F_{k,l}}{\partial\phi_2}
    &=\sum_{b=0}^{l-1}\phi_2^{l-1-b}\phi_1^k\phi_2^b.
  \end{align*}
  Therefore the first summand in the bracket is a sum of $kn$ traces, each
  obtained by removing one $\phi_1$ from the first word and one $\phi_2$ from
  the second word. The second summand is a sum of $lm$ traces obtained by
  removing one $\phi_2$ from the first word and one $\phi_1$ from the second.
  Every trace appearing in either nonzero sum has multidegree
  $(k+m-1,l+n-1)$. Lemma~\ref{lem:adjacent-swaps} identifies all cyclic words
  with this multidegree in reduced BRST cohomology. The coefficient is
  therefore $kn-lm$, and the common class is
  $\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^{k+m-1}\phi_2^{l+n-1})}$. When the common word
  has multidegree $(0,0)$, this class is the scalar trace and is zero in the
	  scalar-reduced quotient.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[Open action on primitive descendants]\label{prop:open-descendant-action}
  Let
  $$\widetilde\Psi_{a,b}
    =\binom{a+b}{a}^{-1}\Psi_{a,b}$$
  be the averaged primitive one-$\psi$ class. In the stable primitive one-$\psi$
  homology,
  $$\{\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^p\phi_2^q)},\widetilde\Psi_{a,b}\}
    =(pb-qa)\,\widetilde\Psi_{a+p-1,b+q-1},$$
  with the convention that a term with a negative exponent is zero. Thus the
  polynomial descendant classes carry the adjoint polynomial Hamiltonian action.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Put $x=\phi_1$ and $y=\phi_2$. The cyclic derivatives of
  $F_{p,q}=\mathrm{Tr}(x^p y^q)$ are
  $$\frac{\partial F_{p,q}}{\partial x}
    =\sum_{i=0}^{p-1}x^{p-1-i}y^qx^i,\qquad
    \frac{\partial F_{p,q}}{\partial y}
    =\sum_{j=0}^{q-1}y^{q-1-j}x^py^j.$$
  For a one-$\psi$ cyclic word $\mathrm{Tr}(\psi w)$ of bidegree $(a,b)$,
  differentiating with respect to $y$ gives one summand for each of the $b$
  occurrences of $y$ in $w$, and differentiating with respect to $x$ gives one
  summand for each of the $a$ occurrences of $x$. Multiplying by the displayed
  cyclic derivatives of $F_{p,q}$ therefore produces $pb$ positive one-$\psi$
  words and $qa$ negative one-$\psi$ words for each $w\in\mathsf W_{a,b}$, all
  of total bidegree $(a+p-1,b+q-1)$.

  The bracket is a cycle because $F_{p,q}$ is invariant, hence
  $\{F_{p,q},\mu_\epsilon\}=0$ for every moment-map component
  $\mu_\epsilon=-\mathrm{Tr}(\epsilon[x,y])$. Equivalently, the Hamiltonian
  vector field of $F_{p,q}$ preserves the Koszul differential
  $Q\psi=[x,y]$ and acts on Koszul homology. By
  Proposition~\ref{prop:one-psi-homology}, the target primitive one-$\psi$
  homology in that bidegree is one-dimensional. The functional
  $\epsilon(\mathrm{Tr}(\psi w))=1$ detects its coefficient. Since
  $\epsilon(\widetilde\Psi_{a,b})=1$, the averaged input gives
  $$\epsilon\bigl(\{F_{p,q},\widetilde\Psi_{a,b}\}\bigr)=pb-qa.$$
  Also $\epsilon(\widetilde\Psi_{a+p-1,b+q-1})=1$. Hence the displayed formula
  follows.
\end{proof}

\begin{cor}[Descendant action differs from the coadjoint action]\label{cor:descendant-coadjoint-difference}
  Under the boundary pairing
  $\delta_{a,b}[1]\mapsto[\Psi_{a,b}]$, the open Poisson action on polynomial
  one-$\psi$ descendants differs from the closed coadjoint action on
  $\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$.
\end{cor}

\begin{proof}
  Proposition~\ref{prop:open-descendant-action} gives
  $$F_{p,q}:\widetilde\Psi_{a,b}\longmapsto
    (pb-qa)\widetilde\Psi_{a+p-1,b+q-1}.$$
  The closed coadjoint action is dual to the Poisson bracket. For
  $f=z_1^pz_2^q$ and the Taylor-dual basis vector $\delta_{a,b}$,
  $$(\operatorname{ad}^{\vee}_f\delta_{a,b})(z_1^mz_2^n)
    =-\delta_{a,b}\bigl((pn-qm)z_1^{p+m-1}z_2^{q+n-1}\bigr).$$
  Therefore, with the convention that negative indices give zero,
  $$\operatorname{ad}^{\vee}_{z_1^pz_2^q}\delta_{a,b}
    =-(pb-qa+p-q)\delta_{a-p+1,b-q+1}.$$
  For example, $z_1$ acts on open descendants by
  $$\widetilde\Psi_{a,b}\longmapsto b\,\widetilde\Psi_{a,b-1},$$
  whereas it acts coadjointly by
  $$\delta_{a,b}\longmapsto-(b+1)\delta_{a,b+1}.$$
  The scalar edge
  $\{\,\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1),\widetilde\Psi_{0,1}\,\}=\Psi_{0,0}$ is removed with
  the scalar descendant. The theorem uses
  Corollary~\ref{cor:cotangent-boundary-pairing} for the PBW special-fibre
  product identification. A descendant deformation match requires a different
  boundary realization of the continuous dual, such as a Laurent or
  distributional model, together with its own bracket calculation.
\end{proof}

\begin{thm}[Classical bulk-to-boundary Hamiltonian map]\label{thm:bulk-boundary}
  In the stable scalar-reduced Hamiltonian sector, the boundary evaluation maps
  $\partial_{\mathrm{bb},N}$ of Construction~\ref{constr:boundary-evaluation}
  stabilize to a linear map
  $$\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}:\bar A\longrightarrow
    H^0_{\mathrm{red}}(\mc O_{\mathrm{brane}}^{\mathrm{cl}}),\qquad
    f\longmapsto \overline{\mathrm{Tr}\,f(\phi_1,\phi_2)}.$$
  This map is a morphism of Lie algebras from the closed Hamiltonian Poisson
  algebra $\bar A$ to the scalar-reduced open trace Poisson algebra:
  $$\{\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}(f),\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}(g)\}_{\mathrm{open}}
    =\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}\bigl(\{f,g\}_{\bar A}\bigr).$$
\end{thm}

\begin{proof}
  The map is well-defined on $\bar A$ by
  Corollary~\ref{cor:matrix-evaluation-well-defined}; constants map to the
  scalar $N$ and are removed in the scalar-reduced sector. Lemma
  \ref{lem:trace-bracket-descends} gives the open reduced Poisson bracket. For
  $f=z_1^kz_2^l$ and $g=z_1^mz_2^n$, Proposition~\ref{prop:first-order-bracket}
  gives
  $$\{\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}(f),\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}(g)\}_{\mathrm{open}}
    =\overline{(kn-lm)\mathrm{Tr}
      (\phi_1^{k+m-1}\phi_2^{l+n-1})}.$$
  The closed Poisson bracket is
  $$\{f,g\}_{\bar A}=\overline{(kn-lm)z_1^{k+m-1}z_2^{l+n-1}},$$
  and applying $\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}$ gives the same expression. Bilinearity
  proves the identity for all polynomials.
\end{proof}

\begin{cor}[Classical local bulk-boundary coupling]\label{cor:local-bulk-boundary-coupling}
  For scalar compactly supported test functions $a,b$ on the brane line, define
  the smeared boundary Hamiltonian
  $$I_{\partial}(a,f)=\int_\R a(t)\,
    \overline{\mathrm{Tr}\,f(\phi_1(t),\phi_2(t))}\,dt$$
  for $f\in\bar A$. In the local equal-time Poisson bracket convention,
  $$\{I_{\partial}(a,f),I_{\partial}(b,g)\}_{\mathrm{open}}
    =I_{\partial}(ab,\{f,g\}_{\bar A})$$
  for $g\in\bar A$.
\end{cor}

\begin{proof}
  The test functions are scalar smearings of boundary observables. Form parts of
  the closed BV bracket are absent. The test functions have zero Poisson bracket
  with the matrix coordinates. The canonical bracket is local in $t$, so the equal-time
  delta-function multiplies the coefficients to $a(t)b(t)$, and the bracket of
  the inserted Hamiltonians is exactly the bracket computed in
  Theorem~\ref{thm:bulk-boundary}. Compact support removes boundary terms.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:main-local}]
  The open Hamiltonian-sector algebra is obtained from the product of the exact

  $$\mc{O}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{brane}}
    \cong \mathbf{S}(\bar A\oplus\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}[1]).$$

  On the closed side, Proposition~\ref{prop:hamiltonian-polyvector-reduction}
  identifies the polynomial local Hamiltonian modes with $\bar A$, and their
  Taylor completion is $\mathfrak h=\widehat{\mathfrak h}$. The cotangent BF
  field supplies the shifted coadjoint copy, so the local Lie algebra is
  $\mathfrak g=\mathfrak h\ltimes\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$.
  Proposition~\ref{prop:grav-ops} computes local observables as
  $C^\bullet_{\mathrm{CE},\mathrm{cont}}(\mathfrak g)$.
  Proposition~\ref{prop:ce-koszul} gives the Koszul dual
  $\widehat{U(\mathfrak g)}$, and the PBW/order Rees specialization gives
  $\widehat{\mathbf S}(\mathfrak g)$ at $\hbar=0$. Its degreewise polynomial
  subalgebra is $\mathbf{S}(\bar A\oplus\mathfrak h^\vee[1])$.

  coefficient $(kn-lm)$ on both sides after scalar reduction.
\end{proof}

\subsubsection{Formal Weyl/Moyal target, all orders}
We use the Weyl/Moyal Rees convention for the formal quantization of the
constant Poisson tensor:
$$f*g=m\circ\exp\left(\frac{\hbar}{2}
  (\partial_{z_1}\otimes\partial_{z_2}
   -\partial_{z_2}\otimes\partial_{z_1})\right)(f\otimes g).$$
Then the star commutator is odd in $\hbar$:
$$[f,g]_*=\hbar\{f,g\}+\frac{\hbar^3}{24}P^3(f,g)+O(\hbar^5),$$
where $P=\partial_{z_1}\otimes\partial_{z_2}
-\partial_{z_2}\otimes\partial_{z_1}$. Thus the $\hbar^2$ coefficient vanishes
in this quantization convention.

\begin{prop}[Moyal coefficients on monomials]\label{prop:moyal-monomial}
  Let $f=z_1^kz_2^l$ and $g=z_1^mz_2^n$. For every $r\geq0$,
  $$P^r(f,g)=
    \sum_{a=0}^r(-1)^a\binom{r}{a}
    (k)_{r-a}(l)_a(m)_a(n)_{r-a}
    z_1^{k+m-r}z_2^{l+n-r},$$
  where $(s)_q=s(s-1)\cdots(s-q+1)$ and $(s)_0=1$. Terms with a negative
  displayed exponent are understood to be zero; equivalently the corresponding
  falling factorial already vanishes. Consequently
  $$[f,g]_*=\sum_{\substack{r\geq1\\ r\ \mathrm{odd}}}
    \frac{2}{2^r r!}\,\hbar^r P^r(f,g),$$
  so the second-order coefficient vanishes and the third-order coefficient is
  $\frac1{24}P^3(f,g)$.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Since
  $$P=\partial_{z_1}\otimes\partial_{z_2}
    -\partial_{z_2}\otimes\partial_{z_1},$$
  the two summands commute as differential operators on the tensor product.
  The binomial theorem gives
  $$P^r=\sum_{a=0}^r(-1)^a\binom{r}{a}
    \partial_{z_1}^{r-a}\partial_{z_2}^{a}
    \otimes
    \partial_{z_1}^{a}\partial_{z_2}^{r-a}.$$
  Applying this to $z_1^kz_2^l\otimes z_1^mz_2^n$ gives the displayed falling
  factorial formula. Interchanging $f$ and $g$ changes $P^r(f,g)$ by the sign
  $(-1)^r$, so the star commutator keeps exactly odd $r$ and contributes the
  factor $2/(2^r r!)$ in order $r$.
\end{proof}

\subsubsection{Third-order specialization and analytic graph problem}
For monomials $f=z_1^kz_2^l$ and $g=z_1^mz_2^n$, the third-order Moyal
coefficient from Proposition~\ref{prop:moyal-monomial} is
$$P^3(f,g)=
  \sum_{a=0}^3(-1)^a\binom{3}{a}
  (k)_{3-a}(l)_a(m)_a(n)_{3-a}
  z_1^{k+m-3}z_2^{l+n-3},$$
where $(r)_s=r(r-1)\cdots(r-s+1)$ and $(r)_0=1$. Therefore
$$[f,g]_*=\hbar(kn-lm)z_1^{k+m-1}z_2^{l+n-1}
  +\frac{\hbar^3}{24}P^3(f,g)+O(\hbar^5).$$

\begin{rmk}
  In characteristic zero, $GL_N$ is linearly reductive on rational
  representations. Therefore taking $GL_N$-invariants is exact, and
  $$H\bigl((\mc R_N)^{GL_N},Q\bigr)\cong H(\mc R_N,Q)^{GL_N}.$$
  This justifies the invariant calculation used above. A Chevalley BRST model
  for the quotient stack introduces Lie algebra ghosts as presentation data.
  Linearly reductive exactness identifies derived algebraic invariants with
  ordinary invariants in this setting.
\end{rmk}

\begin{rmk}
  The gravitational calculation uses string states, while the gauge-theory
  calculation uses a brane of the form a point cross a Lagrangian. A different
  B-brane replaces the associative algebra by a factorization algebra. With two
  topological directions the open algebra is an $E_2$ algebra, and the duality
  statement is the corresponding $E_2$ Koszul duality.
\end{rmk}
