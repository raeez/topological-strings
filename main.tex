\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Overview}
  The local problem is a constrained canonical system. A brane sitting at a
  point of a holomorphic symplectic surface has two normal coordinates. On a
  stack of $N$ probes those coordinates become two matrices
  $\phi_1,\phi_2$, with symplectic potential
  $\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\,d\phi_2)$. Change of Chan-Paton basis imposes the
  first-class moment-map constraint
  $$[\phi_1,\phi_2]=0,$$
  and the ghost-zero open action is the Dirac first-order action
  $$\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2+A[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr).$$
  The BV/Koszul variable $\psi=A^*$ records the constraint by
  $Q\psi=[\phi_1,\phi_2]$.

  A closed Hamiltonian field $f(z_1,z_2)$ is measured by the brane by
  substituting the matrix-valued normal coordinates:
  $$z_i\mapsto\phi_i,\qquad
    f\longmapsto \mathrm{Tr}\,f(\phi_1,\phi_2).$$
  The degree-zero theorem proves that this measurement is a moment-map shadow:
  after removing the scalar Hamiltonian and stabilizing in $N$, the boundary
  bracket is the Poisson bracket of the normal symplectic plane.

  The closed Hamiltonian BF sector is the shifted cotangent Lie algebra
  $\mathfrak g=\mathfrak h\ltimes\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$ of Hamiltonian vector
  fields on the formal disk. Its local observables are continuous CE cochains.
  The reduced derived Poisson center of the stable degree-zero open
  Hamiltonian algebra is computed below and is canonically
  $C^\bullet_{\mathrm{CE},\mathrm{cont}}(\mathfrak g)$. Separately, the PBW
  special fibre of the Koszul dual $\widehat U(\mathfrak g)$ identifies with
  the connected stable open Hamiltonian trace sector as a filtered
  graded-commutative product. These are different statements.

  \begin{rmk}
    This introduction records the physical source of the local theorem. The
    mathematical assertions are the formal local statements proved below.
  \end{rmk}

  Let $X$ be a holomorphic symplectic manifold. Consider a topological
  twist of M-theory on $\R \times X$ in the presence of a stack of $N$
  one-dimensional topological branes supported on $\R \times p$, for some point
  $p \in X$.

  The theorem below is not a global theorem about this M-theory background. It
  is the formal local model obtained by completing at the brane and separating
  the local directions into one brane direction, one additional topological
  direction, and two holomorphic symplectic normal directions:
  $$\R_{\mathrm{brane}}\times\R_{\mathrm{top}}\times\C^2_{\mathrm{hol}}.$$
  The extra topological direction is the local topological direction normal to
  the brane inside the mixed spacetime, while the formal holomorphic disk is the
  completed normal holomorphic symplectic surface at $p$.

  The local model contains two algebras:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The algebra $\mc{A}_N$ of operators on the stack of $N$ branes
      supported on $\R \times p$.
    \item The algebra $\mc{B}$ of local operators of the closed mixed
      Hamiltonian sector on $\R \times X$. Operator product expansions in the
      direction of $\R$ equip $\mc{B}$ with the structure of an associative
      algebra.
  \end{itemize}

  The physical relation between the two algebras is probe-theoretic. The brane
  algebra records what the closed background does to a finite stack of probes.
  The closed algebra records infinitesimal Hamiltonian deformations of the
  formal symplectic neighbourhood. In the large-$N$ limit single traces retain
  precisely the primitive closed modes; finite-rank trace identities disappear.

  The guiding large-$N$ statement, before the sector restrictions and completions
  are imposed, is:
  \begin{conj}
    As $N \to \infty$, the appropriate connected open brane observables are
    controlled by the Koszul dual of the closed Hamiltonian observable algebra.
  \end{conj}

  \begin{rmk}
    Witten's Chern-Simons model \cite{witten-cs} supplies the open-string
    algebra used for operator-product calculations.
  \end{rmk}

  This paper isolates one local mixed holomorphic-topological example and
  verifies three local statements: the reduced derived Poisson center, the PBW
  special-fibre trace comparison, and the degree-zero Hamiltonian boundary
  bracket. The quantum discussion formulates the natural $\hbar$-adic
  Hamiltonian upgrade, proves the finite-$N$ radial-parts identity for
  Weyl/Capelli generators, and computes the formal odd-power Moyal target. The
  graph-realization problem is the analytic BV/Feynman statement that the
  algebraic boundary bracket and its higher Moyal corrections arise as actual
  renormalized closed-open graph weights, with propagator, counterterms,
  anomaly cancellation, and brane-defect kernel fixed.
  Costello's renormalized BV formalism supplies the general graph/RG package;
  Costello--Li prove the flat odd-dimensional BCOV/HCS case. The remaining
  specialization to this Hamiltonian sector is formulated as
  Problem~\ref{prob:analytic-graph-realization}.

\subsection{Mixed holomorphic-topological strings}

A local string theory in this paper means the part of string field theory
visible to local operators. The closed fields form a BV theory; local
observables form a factorization algebra; a brane contributes an open cyclic
field theory and a bulk-to-boundary map. The words topological and holomorphic
describe how these local operators vary in the spacetime directions. This is a
local string-field-theoretic use of the word ``string'', not a definition of a
global theory of worldsheet moduli, amplitudes on all backgrounds, or a
completed quantum anomaly theory.

Gwilliam--Williams \cite{gwilliam-williams-holomorphic-bosonic-string} are the
precedent for formulating a holomorphic string in the Costello
BV/factorization formalism. Their object is the holomorphic bosonic string on a
Riemann surface, with Dolbeault fields $(\gamma,\beta,c,b)$, a one-loop
quantization in the critical complex dimension, and a factorization algebra
recovering the BRST cohomology. The object here is not that theory and not a
replacement definition. It is a formal local target-space sector: de
Rham/Floer directions, a holomorphic symplectic formal disk, Hamiltonian
cotangent BF fields, and a brane large-$N$ operator algebra.

The topological word refers to locally constant factorization in the de
Rham/Floer directions. The holomorphic word refers to the Dolbeault presentation:
the kinetic operator contains $\bar\partial$, the fields are holomorphic jets
after local formal completion, and anti-holomorphic translations act by
cochain-homotopic zero. A holomorphic theory may acquire further topological
descent after quantization; that extra descent is not part of the definition
used here.

\begin{defn}[Working local topological factorization sector]\label{def:local-topological-string-sector}
  Let $M$ be a smooth manifold. A local topological closed-string sector on
  $M$ is a classical BV theory whose local observables form a locally constant
  factorization algebra on $M$: for inclusions of sufficiently small disks
  $U\subset V$, the factorization structure map
  $$\mathrm{Obs}^{\mathrm{cl}}(U)\longrightarrow
    \mathrm{Obs}^{\mathrm{cl}}(V)$$
  is a quasi-isomorphism. In this paper, attaching branes to such a sector
  means specifying a cyclic open BV theory and the closed-open operations
  actually constructed. In a de Rham model the closed fields carry the
  differential $d_M$ plus the BV linearized differential, and the locally
  constant factorization product records collision of operators up to isotopy.
\end{defn}

\begin{rmk}
  The exact A-model factor used below is a local topological sector in this
  sense: the self-Floer model for $\R\subset T^*\R$ is represented by the
  de Rham complex $\Omega^*(\R)$, and its local observables are insensitive to
  the metric geometry of the interval. This is the topological part of the
  mixed holomorphic-topological example.
\end{rmk}

\begin{defn}[Working local holomorphic factorization sector]\label{def:local-holomorphic-string-sector}
  Let $Y$ be a complex manifold. A local holomorphic sector on $Y$ is a
  classical BV theory whose fields are Dolbeault complexes on $Y$, whose
  differential has the form $\bar\partial$ plus the BV linearized
  differential, whose interactions are holomorphic polydifferential operators,
  and whose local observables form a holomorphic factorization algebra. Here
  holomorphic factorization means that, on coordinate polydisks, the
  factorization products and restriction maps are represented by holomorphic
  polydifferential operations and every anti-holomorphic translation
  $\partial/\partial\bar z_i$ acts null-homotopically on observables. In the
  target-space Kodaira-Spencer examples the fields are Dolbeault-valued
  polyvectors; in the holomorphic symplectic examples below they are
  Hamiltonian polyvectors after removing constants. In this paper branes are
  treated through cyclic open BV theory and the bulk-to-boundary operations
  constructed below.
\end{defn}

\begin{rmk}
  BCOV/Kodaira-Spencer theory is the guiding threefold example
  \cite{bcov}: its fields are Dolbeault-valued polyvectors, and the
  Calabi-Yau volume form supplies the divergence operator. In the holomorphic
  symplectic surface model used here, the analogous divergence-free vector
  fields are represented locally by Hamiltonians modulo constants. The surface
  model is not a truncation of threefold BCOV theory.
\end{rmk}

\begin{rmk}
  The classical local observable theory underlying Gwilliam--Williams is an
  example of a pure holomorphic BV/factorization sector on a Riemann surface,
  with additional worldsheet ghost, BRST, and critical-dimension quantum
  structure. Their critical-dimension and BRST-cohomology theorems are not
  imported here. The model below is a different formal local target-space sector
  whose holomorphic sector is Hamiltonian BF, or the holomorphic symplectic
  Kodaira-Spencer analogue, and whose topological sector is de Rham/Floer. In
  particular, the critical complex
  dimension in Gwilliam--Williams is a worldsheet ghost-anomaly statement for
  the holomorphic bosonic string; it is not a constraint on the local
  $\R^2\times\C^2$ Hamiltonian sector studied here.
\end{rmk}

\begin{defn}[Working classical local mixed holomorphic-topological sector]\label{def:local-th-string}
  Let $M$ be a smooth manifold and let $Y$ be a complex manifold. A classical
  local mixed holomorphic-topological closed sector on $M\times Y$ consists of:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item a sheaf of cochain complexes of fields $\mc E$ which, on product
      coordinate opens $D\times B$, has the form
      $$\mc E(D\times B)
        =\Omega^*(D)\widehat{\otimes}\Omega^{0,*}(B,\mc V)$$
      for a graded holomorphic vector bundle or formal holomorphic vector
      space $\mc V$;
    \item a degree $-1$ local BV pairing on $\mc E$ and a differential
      $$Q=d_M+\bar\partial_Y+Q_0,$$
      where $Q_0$ is holomorphic over $Y$ and is linear over de Rham forms on
      $M$;
    \item a degree-zero local functional
      $I\in\mathcal O_{\mathrm{loc}}(\mc E)$, built from differential
      operators in the $M$ directions and holomorphic polydifferential
      operators in the $Y$ directions, satisfying the classical master equation
      $$QI+\frac12\{I,I\}=0;$$
    \item the associated classical observables
      $$\mathrm{Obs}^{\mathrm{cl}}(U)
        =\left(\widehat{\mathrm{Sym}}\bigl(\mc E^!_c(U)\bigr),
        Q_{\mathrm{obs}}\right),\qquad
        Q_{\mathrm{obs}}=Q^!+\{I,-\},$$
      with the standard factorization product for disjoint supports. Here
      $\mc E^!_c(U)$ denotes compactly supported continuous linear observables,
      i.e.\ the density-dual cosheaf of fields; extension by zero gives the
      covariant structure maps used by the factorization algebra.
  \end{enumerate}
  These data must satisfy the mixed locality condition. For every inclusion of
  small disks $D\subset D'$ in $M$ and every holomorphic polydisk $B\subset Y$,
  the structure map
  $$\mathrm{Obs}^{\mathrm{cl}}(D\times B)\longrightarrow
    \mathrm{Obs}^{\mathrm{cl}}(D'\times B)$$
  is a quasi-isomorphism. In holomorphic coordinates on $B$, each
  anti-holomorphic translation $\partial/\partial\bar z_i$ acts null-homotopically
  on observables: there is a degree $-1$ derivation $\eta_i$ with
  $$[Q_{\mathrm{obs}},\eta_i]=\mathcal L_{\partial/\partial\bar z_i}.$$

  A mixed brane for such a sector is supported on $N\times Z$, where
  $N\subset M$ is a smooth submanifold and $Z\subset Y$ is a holomorphic
  submanifold or a formal holomorphic neighbourhood of one, and is specified by
  an object $\mc F$ of the local holomorphic brane category on $Z$. Its open
  fields have the derived form
  $$\Omega^*(N)\widehat{\otimes}\RHom_Y(\mc F,\mc F)\otimes\End(V)[1],$$
  with a cyclic trace. For a smooth brane this recovers the Dolbeault model
  $\Omega^*(N)\widehat{\otimes}\Omega^{0,*}(Z,\mc W)$; for a skyscraper brane it
  produces the Ext algebra. A full closed-open mixed datum would further
  require a morphism of
  factorization algebras
  $$\mathrm{Obs}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{closed}}\big|_{N\times Z}
    \longrightarrow
    \mathrm Z^{P_0}_{\mathrm{fact}}\bigl(\mathrm{Obs}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{open}}\bigr),$$
  from closed observables to the Poisson/BV factorization center of the open
  brane observables, compatible with the BV brackets and factorization products.
  In the Hamiltonian model below we construct the degree-zero Hamiltonian
  current shadow of this map; the full closed CE/cotangent
  factorization-center lift is Problem~\ref{prob:formal-factorization-center}.
\end{defn}

\begin{rmk}
  A quantum mixed holomorphic-topological string datum would be the
  corresponding $\hbar$-adic factorization algebra obtained from a renormalized
  BV action satisfying the quantum master equation. This paper constructs no
  such quantum datum for the Hamiltonian model. It proves the classical local
  closed-open Hamiltonian theorem and formulates the quantum upgrade in
  Problem~\ref{prob:quantum-hamiltonian-upgrade}.
\end{rmk}

\begin{rmk}[Locality and unitarity]
  Locality in this paper is operational locality of observables, not Lorentzian
  finite propagation. If insertions have disjoint supports, the factorization
  product records that the measurements can be performed independently. In the
  topological directions translations are $Q$-exact, so the position of an
  insertion is invisible until a collision occurs. In the holomorphic normal
  directions the theory is formal at the brane point; principal parts are
  transverse delta-currents, or derivatives of them, supported at the defect.

  The model is not unitary in the ordinary Lorentzian sense. It is complex,
  BV, ghost-valued, and topological-holomorphic; it has no positive Hilbert
  space inner product or reflection-positivity statement. The consistency
  conditions used here are the classical master equation, and in the quantum
  problem the quantum master equation together with factorization/OPE
  associativity. If this local sector comes from a unitary untwisted parent,
  that unitarity belongs to the parent theory, not to the protected BV sector
  by itself.
\end{rmk}

Definition~\ref{def:local-th-string} separates three levels. A pure
topological sector is locally constant in all real directions; in examples this
includes the A-model local/Floer direction and the Chern-Simons-type open field
theory on a topological brane. A pure holomorphic sector is Dolbeault in its
complex directions and produces a holomorphic factorization algebra;
Gwilliam--Williams' holomorphic bosonic string is a worldsheet example, while
BCOV/Kodaira-Spencer gives the guiding target-space closed-string example. The
Hamiltonian BF complex below is the formal local target-space sector used in
this paper. A mixed holomorphic-topological sector lives on
$M\times Y$ with locally constant factorization in $M$ and holomorphic
factorization in $Y$. The A/B terminology used below is one source of such
examples: the A-side supplies the de Rham/Floer direction and the B-side
supplies the Dolbeault or formal holomorphic direction.

\subsection{The local mixed model}

The paper studies the product local model
$$\R^2_{\mathrm{top}}\times\C^2_{\mathrm{hol}}.$$
The theorem is not a global statement about all twisted M-theory backgrounds.
It is the formal local calculation obtained after completing at a brane
$\R\times p$ and taking the holomorphic symplectic normal disk at
$p\in\C^2$, with
$$\omega=dz_1\wedge dz_2.$$
The closed sector is the Hamiltonian Kodaira-Spencer/BF theory on this mixed
space, not a pure A-model or pure B-model. The open sector is the cyclic open
string field theory on the mixed brane: topological along $\R$ and formal
holomorphic in the normal $\C^2$ directions.

\begin{rmk}[Global obstruction]
  The Hamiltonian identification used here is local. On a holomorphic
  symplectic surface $X$, a holomorphic vector field $v$ is symplectic when
  $$\alpha_v=-\iota_v\omega$$
  is a closed holomorphic one-form, and it is Hamiltonian only after choosing
  $f$ with $df=\alpha_v$, in the convention
  $\iota_{X_f}\omega=-df$. Thus globalizing the Hamiltonian sector requires
  either a sheaf of local Hamiltonians or vanishing of the corresponding
  holomorphic de Rham obstruction. The formal disk has no such obstruction by
  the holomorphic Poincare lemma; a general $X$ need not.
\end{rmk}

\begin{prop}[Global local-Hamiltonian restriction]\label{prop:formal-local-global-restriction}
  Let $(X,\omega)$ be a connected holomorphic symplectic surface and let
  $p\in X$. Put
  $$\mc H_X=\mc O_X/\C_X,\qquad
    \widehat{\mathfrak h}_{X,p}=\widehat{\mc O}_{X,p}/\C.$$
  The differential identifies $\mc H_X$ with the sheaf of closed holomorphic
  one-forms, hence with the sheaf of holomorphic symplectic vector fields by
  $\alpha=-\iota_v\omega$. For every $p\in X$ there is a Lie algebra
  restriction
  $$H^0(X,\mc H_X)\longrightarrow\widehat{\mathfrak h}_{X,p}.$$
  The formal neighbourhood $(\widehat X_p,\omega)$ defines the same stable
  Hamiltonian brane sector as the local model of
  Theorem~\ref{thm:main-local}, after choosing a formal Darboux coordinate
  system. The resulting boundary map
  $$\widehat{\mathfrak h}_{X,p}\longrightarrow
    H^0_{\mathrm{Ham}}(\mathrm{Obs}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{brane}})$$
  is independent of this choice up to the natural action of the formal
  symplectomorphism group. The subspace coming from global Hamiltonian
  functions is the image of
  $$H^0(X,\mc O_X)/\C\longrightarrow H^0(X,\mc H_X).$$
  The obstruction for a locally Hamiltonian symplectic vector field to come
  from a global Hamiltonian function is its period class in $H^1(X,\C_X)$.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  The holomorphic Poincare lemma gives an isomorphism
  $$d:\mc O_X/\C_X\xrightarrow{\ \sim\ }\Omega^1_{X,\mathrm{cl}}.$$
  The holomorphic symplectic form identifies a symplectic vector field $v$ with
  the closed one-form $\alpha_v=-\iota_v\omega$. Thus a section of $\mc H_X$ is
  exactly a local Hamiltonian primitive, modulo local constants, and the
  Poisson bracket is independent of the local constants. Taking the germ at
  $p$ and completing gives the displayed restriction to
  $\widehat{\mathfrak h}_{X,p}$.

  The formal holomorphic Darboux theorem identifies the completed local
  symplectic algebra at $p$ with
  $\C[[z_1,z_2]]$ equipped with $dz_1\wedge dz_2$. Under this identification
  Hamiltonians modulo constants become
  $\widehat{\mathfrak h}=\C[[z_1,z_2]]/\C$, and the point-brane Ext algebra is
  the coordinate-free exterior algebra
  $\Ext_X^*(\mc O_p,\mc O_p)\cong\Lambda^*T_pX$, with cyclic trace fixed by
  $\omega_p$. Hence the open BV reduction and the Hamiltonian boundary
  evaluation are exactly the formal local construction after choosing Darboux
  coordinates. A different Darboux coordinate system differs by a formal
  symplectomorphism, which acts on Hamiltonians and on the normal coordinate
  algebra; the substitution map $z_i\mapsto\phi_i$ is equivariant for this
  action.

  The exact sequence
  $$0\to\C_X\to\mc O_X\to\mc H_X\to0$$
  gives
  $$0\to H^0(X,\mc O_X)/\C
    \to H^0(X,\mc H_X)
    \xrightarrow{\delta}H^1(X,\C_X)
    \to H^1(X,\mc O_X).$$
  For a locally Hamiltonian vector field, $\delta$ is the period class of the
  closed one-form $-\iota_v\omega$. Vanishing of this class is exactly the
  existence of a single global holomorphic Hamiltonian primitive, unique modulo
  constants.
\end{proof}

\begin{rmk}
  This is the maximal global statement used in the paper. If
  $H^1(X,\C)=0$, global symplectic vector fields which are locally Hamiltonian
  have global Hamiltonian primitives. If $X$ is Stein, every finite jet needed
  for a finite local calculation can be lifted to a global holomorphic
  Hamiltonian, noncanonically: apply Cartan's theorem B to
  $$0\to\mathfrak m_p^{k+1}\to\mc O_X\to
    \mc O_X/\mathfrak m_p^{k+1}\to0.$$
  This does not imply surjectivity onto
  $\widehat{\mc O}_{X,p}$. If $X$ is compact connected, then
  $H^0(X,\mc O_X)/\C=0$; abelian surfaces still have nonzero locally
  Hamiltonian translations, obstructed by their period classes. A global
  cochain-level Poisson factorization-center map or a global quantum
  deformation-quantization statement requires additional descent, anomaly, and
  background-choice data.
\end{rmk}

The closed Hamiltonian coefficient Lie algebra is the reduced Tate vector space
$$\widehat{\mathfrak h}=\C[[z_1,z_2]]/\C\cdot1.$$
We write a class by its unique zero-constant Taylor representative. The bracket
is the projected Poisson bracket
$$\{f,g\}=\partial_{z_1}f\,\partial_{z_2}g
  -\partial_{z_2}f\,\partial_{z_1}g\quad \bmod \C\cdot1.$$
Thus $\{z_1,z_2\}=0$ in $\widehat{\mathfrak h}$, although the unreduced
Poisson bracket of the chosen representatives is $1$.
The cotangent BF field contributes the continuous dual, so the residual local
closed Lie algebra is
$$\mathfrak g=\widehat{\mathfrak h}\ltimes
  \widehat{\mathfrak h}^{\vee}_{\mathrm{cont}}[1].$$
On product opens in $\R^2\times\C^2$ the closed fields have the mixed form
$$\Omega^*(D)\widehat\otimes\Omega^{0,*}(B)\widehat\otimes
  \bigl(\widehat{\mathfrak h}[1]\oplus
  \widehat{\mathfrak h}^{\vee}_{\mathrm{cont}}[2]\bigr),$$
with differential $d_{\R^2}+\bar\partial_{\C^2}$ and BF interaction
$\frac12\int\langle\beta,\{\alpha,\alpha\}\rangle dz_1dz_2$.
Proposition~\ref{prop:local-model-mixed-definition} checks that this is a local
mixed holomorphic-topological BV sector in the sense above.

The open brane is the product of the exact local A-brane
$\R\subset T^*\R$ and the B-brane $\mc O_p$ at the formal holomorphic point.
For a cyclic Calabi-Yau $A_\infty$ category, the open field of an object
$\mc F$ is $\RHom(\mc F,\mc F)[1]$ and the formal Chern-Simons action is
$$S(a)=\sum_{n\geq1}\frac{1}{n+1}
  \mathrm{Tr}\bigl(a\,m_n(a,\ldots,a)\bigr),\qquad a\in\RHom(\mc F,\mc F)[1].$$
This is the algebraic core of Witten's open string field theory
\cite{witten-cs} and of the AKSZ packaging \cite{aksz}. In the present local
model
$$\RHom_{\C[z_1,z_2]}(\mc O_p,\mc O_p)
  \cong\Lambda(\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2),$$
and the ghost-zero open field is
$$\alpha_0=A+\phi_1\varepsilon_1+\phi_2\varepsilon_2.$$
The ghost-zero action is the first-order gauged matrix model
$$\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2+A[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr),$$
where $\phi_1,\phi_2$ are adjoint scalar normal coordinates and $A$ imposes the
moment-map equation $[\phi_1,\phi_2]=0$.

\subsection{Hamiltonian sector and stable large N}

Everything below is over $\C$. The phrase \emph{formal local model} means the
completed Taylor model at the chosen point of $\C^2$; equivalently, one may
work polynomially degree by degree and then complete at the augmentation ideal.
The symbol $\widehat{\mathbf S}$ denotes this completed symmetric algebra, and
all Chevalley-Eilenberg cochains are continuous cochains for the same
filtration.

Shifts follow the BV convention used in the field descriptions: an element of
$V[1]$ has parity reversed and cohomological degree one lower than the
corresponding element of $V$. The notation $\mathbf S(V)$ always means the free
graded-commutative algebra on the graded vector space $V$.

Let
$$\bar A=\C[z_1,z_2]/\C\cdot1,\qquad
  \mathfrak h=\widehat{\mathfrak h}=\C[[z_1,z_2]]/\C\cdot1,\qquad
  \mathfrak h^\vee=\widehat{\mathfrak h}^{\vee}_{\mathrm{cont}}.$$
Constants are removed because a constant Hamiltonian has zero Hamiltonian vector
field. We use zero-constant Taylor representatives for calculations, but the
Lie bracket is always the Poisson bracket followed by projection modulo
constants. On the brane side $\mathrm{Tr}_N(1)=N$ is the empty trace, a central
scalar depending on the rank. We do not quotient the associative operator
algebra by its unit. The Hamiltonian comparison uses the connected stable trace
sector: the unit is retained in the symmetric algebra, but the empty trace is
not a primitive generator and constants project to zero in the Hamiltonian
Lie/vector quotient. If $T$ is a positive word-degree trace expression,
$\overline T$ denotes its connected trace class; constants have zero connected
class.

\begin{rmk}[Scalar Hamiltonian and center-of-mass term]\label{rmk:scalar-hamiltonian-center-of-mass}
  The quotient by constants is load-bearing. In the reduced Hamiltonian algebra
  of the formal disk,
  $$\{z_1,z_2\}=1\equiv0\quad\text{in }\bar A.$$
  On the unreduced brane phase space, however,
  $$\{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1),\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_2)\}=N.$$
  This is the center-of-mass symplectic pair of the stack. Every bracket
  statement below involving linear Hamiltonians is therefore a statement after
  projection to the Hamiltonian quotient by scalar trace classes. The same
  term reappears quantum mechanically as the Capelli shift in
  Remark~\ref{rmk:normal-ordering-obstruction}.
\end{rmk}

At a fixed point of the brane line the reduced BV algebra is generated by
two even matrices $\phi_1,\phi_2$ and the odd antifield $\psi=A^*$, with
$$Q\phi_1=Q\phi_2=0,\qquad Q\psi=[\phi_1,\phi_2].$$
Thus the moment-map equation is imposed homologically. Boundary terms on $\R$
are discarded by taking compactly supported variations, or equivalently by
working on an interval with fixed boundary values and passing to local operators
in the interior.

The term \emph{stable large-$N$} means the degreewise stable value of the
invariant algebras under block restriction as $N\to\infty$. Inside a fixed
finite matrix algebra all finite-$N$ trace identities remain present. In the
stable limit, Procesi--Razmyslov invariant theory
\cites{procesi,razmyslov} says that invariant functions of two matrices are
freely generated by cyclic trace words. The differential
$Q\psi=[\phi_1,\phi_2]$ then makes adjacent swaps exact, so ghost-zero cyclic
words reduce to ordinary commutative monomials
$$\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)},\qquad k+l>0.$$
The primitive one-$\psi$ Koszul homology supplies a second nonconstant family
$\Psi_{k,l}$, obtained by inserting one $\psi$ and symmetrising over all words
with $k$ copies of $\phi_1$ and $l$ copies of $\phi_2$. The scalar class
$\mathrm{Tr}(\psi)$ is excluded from the Hamiltonian comparison because
constants have been removed from $\bar A$; higher $\psi$-count homology lies
outside this Hamiltonian sector.

The large-$N$ principle is the string-field-theoretic form of the
Loday-Quillen-Tsygan principle \cites{loday-quillen,tsygan}. Open strings on a
stack produce matrix algebras; closed modes appear in the primitive stable
part. The theorem below verifies this principle directly in the present
connected Hamiltonian trace sector.

\begin{thm}[Loday-Quillen-Tsygan]
  Let $A$ be a unital associative algebra over a field of characteristic zero.
  The stable Chevalley-Eilenberg homology of $\lie{gl}_{\infty}(A)$ is the free
  graded-commutative algebra generated by shifted cyclic homology:
  $$H_*^{\mathrm{CE}}\bigl(\lie{gl}_{\infty}(A)\bigr)
    \cong \mathbf S\bigl(HC_{*-1}(A)\bigr).$$
\end{thm}
Here this theorem is a template rather than a direct input: stable matrix
constructions are controlled by cyclic words, while the particular reduced
model is computed by Procesi--Razmyslov invariant theory and the explicit
Koszul differential above. No global $HC_{*-1}$ convention is imported into the
main theorem; cyclic homology supplies the guiding stable-trace analogy, not the
proof of the Hamiltonian sector.

\subsection{The concrete dictionary}

We write $\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}$ for an abstract copy of $\bar A$ whose basis
vector labelled by $z_1^kz_2^l$, with $k+l>0$, is represented on the brane by
the first descendant $\Psi_{k,l}$. On the closed side the cotangent field gives
the continuous Taylor dual $\mathfrak h^\vee$. Choosing the monomial Taylor
basis identifies $\mathfrak h^\vee$ with $\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}$ as a filtered
vector space. The closed module structure remains the coadjoint module
structure.

For $k,l\geq0$ and $k+l>0$, set
$$H_{k,l}=z_1^kz_2^l\in\bar A,\qquad
  E_{k,l}=\delta_{k,l}[1]\in\mathfrak h^\vee[1],$$
where $\delta_{k,l}$ is the coefficient functional dual to the Taylor monomial
$z_1^kz_2^l$:
$$\delta_{k,l}(z_1^az_2^b)=\delta_{k,a}\delta_{l,b}.$$
On the brane side set
$$O_{k,l}=\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)},$$
and let $\Psi_{k,l}$ be the symmetrised trace with one $\psi$, $k$ copies of
$\phi_1$, and $l$ copies of $\phi_2$. The theorem proves that
$$H_{k,l}\longmapsto O_{k,l},\qquad E_{k,l}\longmapsto\Psi_{k,l}$$
extends to an isomorphism of the connected stable Hamiltonian descendant sector
with
$$\mathbf S(\bar A\oplus\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}[1])
  \xrightarrow{\ \sim\ }\mc O_{\mathrm{brane},H}^{\mathrm{cl}}$$
in the sector considered here. On the closed side the symbols
$H_{k,l},E_{k,l}$ form the degreewise polynomial PBW generators inside the
special fibre of $U_\hbar(\mathfrak g)$.

Thus every later step has a concrete target: construct the symbols
$O_{k,l},\Psi_{k,l}$ from the open fields, construct $H_{k,l},E_{k,l}$ from
Hamiltonians and cotangent Hamiltonians, prove that commutators of words reduce
to commutative monomials, and check that the bracket coefficient is
$(kn-lm)$.

\subsection{The local theorem}

The theorem has three layers. First, the open calculation says
that the ghost-zero brane theory is the algebra of invariant functions on
commuting matrix pairs, together with the first Koszul descendants of the
commuting-pair equation. Second, the closed calculation says that the same
generators are the PBW symbols of the Hamiltonian cotangent Lie algebra. Third,
the boundary map is not an abstract isomorphism: it is the physical operation
of evaluating a Hamiltonian on the matrix-valued normal coordinates of the
brane.

\begin{thm}[PBW special fibre for the stable Hamiltonian trace sector]\label{thm:main-local}
  In the formal Hamiltonian sector of the local mixed
  holomorphic-topological BV string-field model on $\R^2\times\C^2$ with brane
  $\R\times p$ constructed in
  Proposition~\ref{prop:local-model-mixed-definition}, the stable
  large-$N$ connected open Hamiltonian trace sector freely generated by the
  nonempty primitive degree-zero Hamiltonian traces and the nonconstant
  primitive one-antifield Koszul classes is
  $$\mc{O}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{brane},H}
    \cong \mathbf{S}\bigl(\bar A\oplus\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}[1]\bigr).$$
  The classical local operators in the formal Hamiltonian cotangent BF sector
  are the continuous Chevalley-Eilenberg algebra
  $$\mc{O}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{grav}}
    \cong C^\bullet_{\mathrm{CE},\mathrm{cont}}(\mathfrak g)
    =\widehat{\mathbf{S}}\bigl(\mathfrak g^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}[-1]\bigr).$$
  Its Koszul dual is the completed graded enveloping algebra
  $$\bigl(\mc{O}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{grav}}\bigr)^!
    \simeq \widehat{U(\mathfrak g)}.$$
  The PBW/order Rees algebra $U_\hbar(\mathfrak g)$ has completed special fibre
  $$U_\hbar(\mathfrak g)/(\hbar)\cong
    \widehat{\mathbf{S}}\bigl(\widehat{\mathfrak h}\oplus\mathfrak h^\vee[1]\bigr).$$
  Its degreewise polynomial subalgebra
  $\mathbf{S}(\bar A\oplus\mathfrak h^\vee[1])$ identifies with the open stable
  Hamiltonian-sector algebra after choosing the monomial-dual basis
  $\mathfrak h^\vee\cong\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}$. This is a filtered
  graded-commutative PBW special-fibre product identification, not a
  deformation-level identification of the descendant module actions. The
  open-side deformation is checked below on degree-zero Hamiltonian generators.
  On those generators the identification is induced by the connected
  bulk-to-boundary evaluation map
  $$\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}(f)=\overline{\mathrm{Tr}\,f(\phi_1,\phi_2)},$$
  which sends a Hamiltonian Lie/PBW mode, not a closed CE cochain representative,
  to the corresponding brane Hamiltonian and intertwines Poisson brackets. The
  shifted PBW special-fibre symbols are labelled by the
  nonconstant first Koszul descendants $\Psi_{k,l}$; this is not an
  $\mathfrak h$-module identification. On monomials,
  $$z_1^kz_2^l\longleftrightarrow
    \overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)},\qquad k+l>0,$$
  the first bracket is
  $$\{\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)},
      \overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^m\phi_2^n)}\}
    =\overline{(kn-lm)\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^{k+m-1}\phi_2^{l+n-1})},$$
  matching the Poisson bracket on $\bar A$. If a displayed exponent is
  negative, the coefficient is already zero; if the displayed monomial is
  constant, its connected trace class is zero.
\end{thm}

The proof uses the formal local model alone. A complete closed-open mixed datum
would refine the bracket-compatible Hamiltonian boundary evaluation to a
Poisson factorization-center morphism
$$\mathrm{Obs}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{closed}}\big|_{\R\times p}
  \longrightarrow
  \mathrm Z^{P_0}_{\mathrm{fact}}\bigl(\mathrm{Obs}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{open}}\bigr).$$
Proposition~\ref{thm:hamiltonian-current-center-lift} constructs the
degree-zero Hamiltonian current shadow of this map. The full closed
CE/cotangent lift is Problem~\ref{prob:formal-factorization-center}. The
quantum calculation later fixes the formal Weyl/Moyal target; the analytic
problem is to identify Costello's renormalized BV graph package with this
Hamiltonian BF sector and to recover the first and third graph normalizations.

\section{The Local Model}

\subsection{Setup}
Let
$$M=\R^2,\qquad Y=\C^2,\qquad X=M\times Y.$$
The real factor $M$ is the topological direction and the complex factor $Y$
is the holomorphic direction; all Hamiltonian calculations are completed at
the formal neighbourhood of $p=0\in Y$. We use the exact A-brane given by the
standard line $\R\subset M$ with trivial brane data and the B-brane
  $\mc{O}_p\in\mathrm{Perf}(Y)$. Consider the mixed brane
  $$\R \times p \inj \R^2 \times \C^2$$
  for some point $p \in Y$. We take $p=0$ in coordinates. The holomorphic
  factor carries the standard holomorphic symplectic form
  $$\omega=dz_1\wedge dz_2.$$
  Thus the model is topological along $\R^2$ and holomorphic Hamiltonian along
  $\C^2$.

\begin{prop}[Local mixed model]\label{prop:local-model-mixed-definition}
  The formal local Hamiltonian data used here define a closed mixed
  holomorphic-topological BV sector and a compatible cyclic open brane BV
  theory. The constructed closed-open operation is the degree-zero Hamiltonian
  boundary evaluation. The full Poisson factorization-center morphism is
  Problem~\ref{prob:formal-factorization-center}, not part of this proposition.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  On product opens $D\times B$ in the completed formal neighbourhood of
  $\R^2\times\{p\}\subset\R^2\times\C^2$, the closed fields are
  $$\mc E_{\mathrm{cl}}(D\times B)=
    \Omega^*(D)\widehat{\otimes}\Omega^{0,*}(B)\widehat{\otimes}
    \bigl(\widehat{\mathfrak h}[1]\oplus
    \widehat{\mathfrak h}^{\vee}_{\mathrm{cont}}[2]\bigr).$$
  Degreewise polynomial calculations use the dense subspace
  $\bar A=\C[z_1,z_2]/\C\cdot1$. The differential is
  $D=d_{\R^2}+\bar\partial_{\C^2}$, so $Q_0=0$ in
  Definition~\ref{def:local-th-string}. The BV pairing is the local pairing on
  compactly supported fields, obtained by multiplying the form components,
  integrating over $D\times B$ against $dz_1\,dz_2$, and using the continuous
  evaluation pairing
  $\widehat{\mathfrak h}^{\vee}_{\mathrm{cont}}\otimes
  \widehat{\mathfrak h}\to\C$. If
  $\alpha\in\Omega^p\widehat{\otimes}\widehat{\mathfrak h}[1]$ and
  $\beta\in\Omega^q\widehat{\otimes}
  \widehat{\mathfrak h}^{\vee}_{\mathrm{cont}}[2]$, then
  $|\alpha|=p-1$ and $|\beta|=q-2$; the top-form condition $p+q=4$ makes the
  pairing degree $-1$.

  The free quadratic term
  $$S_{\mathrm{free}}=\int_{D\times B}
    \left\langle\beta,D\alpha\right\rangle dz_1\,dz_2$$
  is the BV Hamiltonian for the differential $D$. The interaction in
  Definition~\ref{def:local-th-string} is only
  $$I_{\mathrm{BF}}=\frac12\int_{D\times B}
    \left\langle\beta,\{\alpha,\alpha\}\right\rangle dz_1\,dz_2.$$
  The total BF action is $S_{\mathrm{free}}+I_{\mathrm{BF}}$.
  The Poisson bracket on $\bar A$ is the holomorphic bracket determined by
  $dz_1\wedge dz_2$. Since $D$ is a derivation of this bracket and the bracket
  satisfies Jacobi,
  $$D I_{\mathrm{BF}}+\frac12\{I_{\mathrm{BF}},I_{\mathrm{BF}}\}=0,$$
  so the total BF action satisfies the classical master equation without
  double-counting the free differential.

  Mixed locality is the Cartan homotopy calculation on the interacting
  observables. For a constant vector
  field $v$ in the $\R^2$ direction, $[D,\iota_v]=\mathcal L_v$; for an
  anti-holomorphic coordinate vector field $\partial/\partial\bar z_i$,
  $[D,\iota_{\partial/\partial\bar z_i}]
  =\mathcal L_{\partial/\partial\bar z_i}$. The interaction is built from the
  de Rham wedge product and holomorphic polydifferential operators, so the
  contractions are derivations of the local observable algebra and commute with
  $\{I_{\mathrm{BF}},-\}$ up to the displayed Lie derivative. Thus
  $[Q_{\mathrm{obs}},\iota_v]=\mathcal L_v$ and
  $[Q_{\mathrm{obs}},\iota_{\partial/\partial\bar z_i}]
  =\mathcal L_{\partial/\partial\bar z_i}$ on local observables. The de Rham
  Poincare lemma in the $D$ directions gives the quasi-isomorphism for small
  disk inclusions, and the Dolbeault Poincare lemma gives the holomorphic
  dependence in the $B$ direction.

  The brane is $N\times Z=\R\times\{p\}$, with $Z$ understood as the formal
  holomorphic neighbourhood at $p$. Its open fields are
  $$\Omega^*(\R)\otimes
    \Ext^*_{\C[z_1,z_2]}(\mc O_p,\mc O_p)\otimes\lie{gl}_N[1],$$
  and Proposition~\ref{prop:skyscraper-ext} identifies the Ext algebra with
  $\Lambda(\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2)$. This is the derived
  $\RHom_Y(\mc O_p,\mc O_p)$ allowed in Definition~\ref{def:local-th-string},
  not an ordinary Dolbeault algebra on the point. The trace on $\lie{gl}_N$ and
  the Berezin trace on this exterior algebra give the cyclic open string field
  theory. Construction~\ref{constr:boundary-evaluation} and
  Theorem~\ref{thm:bulk-boundary} construct the degree-zero Hamiltonian
  boundary component and prove its bracket compatibility. The full
  Poisson factorization-center closed-open map is Problem~\ref{prob:formal-factorization-center},
  not a theorem proved here.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Open mixed brane states}
  The open string states are the tensor product of the topological brane
  direction and the formal holomorphic normal direction.

  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Topological factor.} In the exact self-Floer model for
      the graded, spin zero-section $\R\subset T^*\R\cong\R^2$, we use the
      local exact Fukaya model near a compact interval in the brane. A small
      compactly supported Hamiltonian perturbation identifies $CF^*(\R,\R)$
      with the Morse complex on that interval, and passage to local operators
      in the interior is represented by the de Rham complex $\Omega^*(\R)$
      with compactly supported variations. No wrapped generators at infinity
      are included in this local model.
    \item \textit{Holomorphic factor.} Put affine coordinates at $p=0$ and write
      $R=\C[z_1,z_2]$, $\mathfrak m=(z_1,z_2)$, and $\mc{O}_p=R/\mathfrak m$.
      The skyscraper sheaf has open string states
      $$\Ext^*_R(R/\mathfrak m,R/\mathfrak m),$$
      computed in Proposition~\ref{prop:skyscraper-ext}.
  \end{itemize}

\begin{prop}[Affine skyscraper self-Ext]\label{prop:skyscraper-ext}
  Let $R=\C[z_1,z_2]$, $\mathfrak m=(z_1,z_2)$, and $k=R/\mathfrak m$. Then
  the Yoneda algebra of the skyscraper sheaf at the origin is
  $$\Ext^*_R(k,k)\cong\Lambda_{\C}(\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2),$$
  where $|\varepsilon_i|=1$. In particular this is the free
  graded-commutative algebra on two odd generators.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  The pair $(z_1,z_2)$ is a regular sequence in the polynomial ring $R$, so the
  affine Koszul complex
  $$K=\bigl(0\to R\,e_{12}\xrightarrow{d_2}R\,e_1\oplus R\,e_2
        \xrightarrow{d_1}R\to0\bigr)$$
  is a free resolution of $k$, with
  $$d_1(ae_1+be_2)=az_1+bz_2,\qquad
    d_2(e_{12})=z_2e_1-z_1e_2.$$
  Applying $\Hom_R(-,k)$ gives
  $$0\to k\xrightarrow{0}k\,\varepsilon_1\oplus k\,\varepsilon_2
    \xrightarrow{0}k\,\varepsilon_{12}\to0,$$
  because $z_1$ and $z_2$ act by zero on $k$. Hence the graded vector space of
  Ext groups is $k$ in degree $0$, $k\varepsilon_1\oplus k\varepsilon_2$ in
  degree $1$, and $k\varepsilon_{12}$ in degree $2$.

  The product is the Yoneda product. The Koszul resolution is the exterior
  dg-algebra $R\otimes\Lambda(e_1,e_2)$ with $d(e_i)=z_i$; its diagonal sends
  $e_i$ to $e_i\otimes1+1\otimes e_i$. Dualising this diagonal gives
  $\varepsilon_i^2=0$ and
  $\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2=-\varepsilon_2\varepsilon_1$, while
  $\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2$ spans the top Ext group. Thus the Yoneda
  algebra is $\Lambda_{\C}(\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2)$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Open mixed brane field theory}

The open theory is the minimal local field theory which remembers the brane's two
normal holomorphic directions. The two Ext generators are odd because they are
derived normal directions of a point brane; after the BV shift their ghost-zero
coefficients become the two ordinary matrix coordinates $\phi_1,\phi_2$. The
one-form coefficient is the gauge field $A$ along the brane. Thus the formal
Chern-Simons action is the mechanism which turns the Ext algebra into
first-order matrix mechanics.

The total space of fields (in the Batalin-Vilkovisky formalism) is
$$\alpha \in \Omega^*(\R)\otimes\Lambda(\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2)
  \otimes \lie{gl}_N[1]$$
with action
$$S(\alpha)=\int_{\R \times \C^{0|2}}
  \frac{1}{2}\,\mathrm{Tr}(\alpha\, d\alpha)
  + \frac{1}{3}\,\mathrm{Tr}\,\alpha^3.$$
Here $d = d_{\mathrm{dR}}$; the Ext algebra
$\Lambda(\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2)$ has zero differential. The integral
over $\C^{0|2}$ is the Berezin integral $\int d\varepsilon_1\,d\varepsilon_2$,
which picks out the top component in the odd directions.

The cyclic trace is
$$\operatorname{Tr}_{\mathrm{open}}(\eta\otimes u\otimes M)
  =\int_\R \eta\,[\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2]u\,
    \operatorname{Tr}_N(M),$$
where $[\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2]u$ denotes the coefficient of the top Ext
class. The top Ext class is the residue orientation dual to
$dz_1\wedge dz_2$. The de Rham integral has degree $-1$ and the Ext residue has
degree $-2$; after the field shift by $[1]$, the induced BV pairing has degree
$-1$.

\begin{constr}[The open field from the Ext basis]\label{constr:open-field-basis}
  Write $\varepsilon_{12}=\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2$. Before imposing ghost
  number, an open field is a finite sum
  $$\alpha=a_0+a_1\varepsilon_1+a_2\varepsilon_2+a_{12}\varepsilon_{12},$$
  where each $a_I$ is a de Rham form on $\R$ with values in $\lie{gl}_N$,
  shifted by $[1]$. The ghost-number-zero components are exactly
  $$A\in\Omega^1(\R)\otimes\lie{gl}_N,\qquad
    \phi_1,\phi_2\in\Omega^0(\R)\otimes\lie{gl}_N,$$
  and they appear as
  $$\alpha_0=A+\phi_1\varepsilon_1+\phi_2\varepsilon_2.$$
  The Berezin integral kills every monomial except the coefficient of
  $\varepsilon_{12}$, so every term in the ghost-zero action is obtained by
  multiplying the displayed three summands and keeping exactly one real
  one-form and exactly the product $\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2$.
\end{constr}

\begin{rmk}
  The shift $[1]$ acts on the total cohomological degree: a form of de Rham
  degree $p$ and Grassmann degree $q$ has total degree $p+q$, which after the
  shift sits in ghost number $p+q-1$. In particular, the 1-form component of
  $\alpha$ (de Rham degree 1, Grassmann degree 0) sits in ghost number 0 and
  is the bosonic gauge field. The scalar components proportional to
  $\varepsilon_i$ (de Rham degree 0, Grassmann degree 1) also sit in ghost
  number 0 and represent the motion of the brane in the two normal directions.
\end{rmk}

\subsubsection{Ghost-zero open field content}
  The ghost-number-zero part of the BV field is
  $$\alpha_0=A+\phi_1\varepsilon_1+\phi_2\varepsilon_2,$$
  where $A\in\Omega^1(\R)\otimes\lie{gl}_N$ and
  $\phi_i\in\Omega^0(\R)\otimes\lie{gl}_N$.

  \begin{lem}\label{lem:open-action-reduction}
    With the Berezin convention
    $\int_{\C^{0|2}}\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2=1$, the restriction of the
    BV action to $\alpha_0$ is, up to a boundary term on $\R$,
    $$S(\alpha_0)=\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2
      +A[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr).$$
  \end{lem}

  \begin{proof}
    Only monomials containing one real one-form and the factor
    $\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2$ survive the integration over
    $\R\times\C^{0|2}$. In the quadratic term this leaves
    $$\frac12\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2-\phi_2\,d\phi_1\bigr),$$
    where the sign is the Koszul sign from interchanging the two odd
    generators. Since
    $d\,\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\phi_2)=\mathrm{Tr}(d\phi_1\,\phi_2+\phi_1\,d\phi_2)$,
    the second summand differs from $\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\,d\phi_2)$ by an exact
    one-form. In the cubic term the six orderings of
    $A,\phi_1\varepsilon_1,\phi_2\varepsilon_2$ reduce by cyclicity of the trace
    and the odd relation $\varepsilon_2\varepsilon_1=-\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2$
    to
    $$3\,\mathrm{Tr}(A\phi_1\phi_2-A\phi_2\phi_1).$$
    The coefficient $\frac13$ in the Chern-Simons action therefore gives exactly
    $\mathrm{Tr}(A[\phi_1,\phi_2])$.
  \end{proof}

\begin{lem}[Dirac reduction of the probe phase space]\label{lem:dirac-probe-reduction}
  Let
  $$\mathcal P_N=\lie{gl}_N\oplus\lie{gl}_N$$
  with coordinates $(\phi_1,\phi_2)$ and symplectic form
  $$\Omega_N=\mathrm{Tr}(d\phi_1\wedge d\phi_2).$$
  With the convention $\iota_{X_f}\Omega_N=-df$, simultaneous conjugation by
  $\lie{gl}_N$ is Hamiltonian with moment map components
  $$\mu_\epsilon=-\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\epsilon[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr),
    \qquad \epsilon\in\lie{gl}_N.$$
  They satisfy the first-class identity
  $$\{\mu_\epsilon,\mu_\eta\}=\mu_{[\epsilon,\eta]}.$$
  The action of Lemma~\ref{lem:open-action-reduction} is the corresponding
  Dirac first-order action
  $$\int_\R \mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\,d\phi_2)-\mu_A,$$
  and varying $A$ imposes the constrained phase space
  $$\mu^{-1}(0)/GL_N=\{[\phi_1,\phi_2]=0\}/GL_N.$$
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  The symplectic form gives
  $$\{(\phi_1)^i{}_j,(\phi_2)^k{}_l\}=\delta^i_l\delta^k_j.$$
  For $v_\epsilon(\phi_i)=[\epsilon,\phi_i]$, cyclicity of the trace gives
  $$d\mu_\epsilon(\delta\phi_1,\delta\phi_2)
    =-\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\epsilon([\delta\phi_1,\phi_2]
      +[\phi_1,\delta\phi_2])\bigr)
    =-\Omega_N(v_\epsilon,(\delta\phi_1,\delta\phi_2)).$$
  Thus $X_{\mu_\epsilon}=v_\epsilon$. The moment-map bracket is therefore the
  infinitesimal equivariance identity
  $\{\mu_\epsilon,\mu_\eta\}=\mu_{[\epsilon,\eta]}$. Substituting
  $\epsilon=A(t)$ gives
  $$\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\,d\phi_2)-\mu_A
    =\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2+A[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr),$$
  and the $A$-equation is $[\phi_1,\phi_2]=0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[Classical BV truncation]\label{prop:open-bv-truncation}
  The subspace of ghost-number-zero fields
  $$\alpha_0=A+\phi_1\varepsilon_1+\phi_2\varepsilon_2$$
  with all nonzero ghost-number components set to zero is a consistent
  classical truncation of the open BV theory. The induced classical action is
  the first-order gauged matrix model of Lemma~\ref{lem:open-action-reduction}.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Decompose the BV field as $\alpha=\sum_r\alpha^{(r)}$ by ghost number. The
  differential $d_{\R}$ and the product on
  $\Lambda(\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2)\otimes\lie{gl}_N$ preserve total degree,
  and the BV action has ghost number zero. Hence every monomial in the
  Euler-Lagrange expression for a component of ghost number $r\neq0$ contains
  at least one field component of nonzero ghost number when evaluated at
  $\alpha=\alpha_0$. Setting all such components to zero therefore makes the
  discarded equations vanish. The remaining equation is exactly the variation
  of $S(\alpha_0)$, computed in Lemma~\ref{lem:open-action-reduction}.

  Concretely, the full Euler-Lagrange expression is the curvature
  $$F(\alpha)=d\alpha+\alpha^2.$$
  For $\alpha_0=A+\phi_1\varepsilon_1+\phi_2\varepsilon_2$ on a line,
  \begin{align*}
  F(\alpha_0)
    &=(d\phi_1+[A,\phi_1])\varepsilon_1
      +(d\phi_2+[A,\phi_2])\varepsilon_2
      +[\phi_1,\phi_2]\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2.
  \end{align*}
  The BV pairing keeps exactly real-form degree one and
  $\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2$. Hence the displayed three components pair exactly
  with the variations of $A,\phi_2,\phi_1$ respectively, while variations of
  nonzero ghost number see complementary curvature components that are zero for
  $\alpha_0$ by real-form degree or $\varepsilon$-degree. This is a physical
  ghost-zero truncation; the residual ordinary $GL_N$ gauge symmetry remains.
\end{proof}

  The ghost-number-zero truncation of the BV master action is closed under the
  classical equations and gives a quantum-mechanical system with a connection
  form
  $$A \in \Omega^1(\R)\otimes \lie{gl}_N,$$
  two adjoint scalars
  $$\phi_1,\phi_2 \in \Omega^0(\R)\otimes\lie{gl}_N,$$
  and action
  $$S = \int_\R \mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2
    + A[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr).$$
  This action is invariant under
  $$\delta_{\varepsilon}\phi_i=[\varepsilon,\phi_i],\qquad
    \delta_{\varepsilon}A=d\varepsilon+[A,\varepsilon],$$
  for $\varepsilon\in\Omega^0(\R)\otimes\lie{gl}_N$. Indeed the variation of
  $\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1d\phi_2)$ containing $d\varepsilon$ is
  $-\mathrm{Tr}(d\varepsilon[\phi_1,\phi_2])$, and this cancels the
  $d\varepsilon$ variation of $\mathrm{Tr}(A[\phi_1,\phi_2])$; the remaining
  terms cancel by invariance of the trace pairing.

\begin{rmk}
  The same local open model is obtained from the Calabi-Yau $2$ point-brane
  residue calculation by tensoring the skyscraper object with $\C^N$ and
  passing to endomorphisms. This is a local CY$_2$ pairing statement, not a
  global CY-to-chiral input. Global transfer would require the corresponding
  negative cyclic Calabi-Yau class, framing data, and descent; none of that is
  used in the proof below.
\end{rmk}

\begin{rmk}
  In AKSZ language this is the one-dimensional gauged sigma model with
  Hamiltonian target $\lie{gl}_N\oplus\lie{gl}_N$ and symplectic potential
  $\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\,d\phi_2)$. We use the resulting Poisson bivector
  $$\Pi=\sum_{i,j}\frac{\partial}{\partial(\phi_1)^i{}_j}
    \wedge\frac{\partial}{\partial(\phi_2)^j{}_i},$$
  so $\{(\phi_1)^i{}_j,(\phi_2)^k{}_l\}=\delta^i_l\delta^k_j$. Equivalently,
  with the convention $\iota_{X_f}\omega=-df$ used throughout, the symplectic
  form is $\mathrm{Tr}(d\phi_1\wedge d\phi_2)$. For
  $\epsilon\in\lie{gl}_N$, the Hamiltonian
  $$\mu_\epsilon=-\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\epsilon[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr)$$
  generates $\delta_\epsilon\phi_i=[\epsilon,\phi_i]$. Its zero locus is the
  commuting-pair equation $[\phi_1,\phi_2]=0$, and the classical reduced phase
  space is the stack of commuting pairs of matrices modulo conjugation.
\end{rmk}

\begin{rmk}
  Equivalently, take Chern-Simons theory on $\R\times T^2$ and restrict to the
  harmonic forms $1,\eta_1,\eta_2,\eta_1\eta_2$ on $T^2$. Writing
  $A_{3d}=A_t\,dt+\phi_1\eta_1+\phi_2\eta_2$ and integrating over $T^2$
  gives the same action; the odd variables $\varepsilon_i$ represent the
  degree-one cohomology classes $\eta_i$.
\end{rmk}

\subsection{Closed mixed Hamiltonian sector}

The closed field is the infinitesimal geometry of the formal normal surface.
Because the surface is holomorphic symplectic, an infinitesimal canonical
transformation is a Hamiltonian function modulo constants. The BF partner is
the cotangent variable which makes the theory local and BV. This is the closed
counterpart of the open first-order form: both sides are governed by the same
symplectic normal plane.

The closed sector used here is a mixed field theory, not a tensor product of
two independent closed-string state spaces. Its fields have a topological de
Rham factor along $\R^2$ and a holomorphic Dolbeault factor along $\C^2$:
$$\Omega^*(\R^2)\widehat{\otimes}\Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)
  \widehat{\otimes}\bigl(\widehat{\mathfrak h}[1]\oplus
  \widehat{\mathfrak h}^{\vee}_{\mathrm{cont}}[2]\bigr).$$
The coefficient $\widehat{\mathfrak h}$ is the completed Lie algebra of
nonconstant Hamiltonian functions on the formal holomorphic symplectic disk.
The continuous dual copy
$\widehat{\mathfrak h}^{\vee}_{\mathrm{cont}}[2]$ is the cotangent/BF field.
Thus the closed theory is the Hamiltonian Kodaira-Spencer/BF sector of the mixed
holomorphic-topological string.

\begin{rmk}
  The original BCOV analysis \cite{bcov} is written for Calabi-Yau threefolds.
  On a holomorphic symplectic surface the natural local cotangent theory is the
  BF theory for the dg Lie algebra of Hamiltonian vector fields. The
  holomorphic symplectic form supplies the Poisson bracket, while the
  holomorphic volume form supplies the divergence operator on polyvectors.
\end{rmk}

We consider the space of polyvector fields
$$\mathbf{PV}^{*,*}(\C^2) \cong \Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)[\partial_{z_1},\partial_{z_2}]$$
where $\partial_{z_i}$ are odd generators of poly-vector degree 1 (corresponding
to the two holomorphic vector fields on $\C^2$). The operator
$$\partial: \mathbf{PV}^{i,*} \to \mathbf{PV}^{i-1,*}$$
is the divergence operator determined by the holomorphic volume form
$\Omega=dz_1\wedge dz_2$:
$$\partial_{\Omega}=\iota_{\Omega}^{-1}\circ\partial_{\mathrm{hol}}\circ
  \iota_{\Omega},\qquad
  \iota_{\Omega}:\mathbf{PV}^{i,*}\xrightarrow{\sim}\Omega^{2-i,*}.$$
Thus for $X=a_1\partial_{z_1}+a_2\partial_{z_2}$,
$$\partial_{\Omega}X=(\partial_{z_1}a_1+\partial_{z_2}a_2),$$
while $\partial_{\Omega}$ vanishes on functions.

We work in the formal polynomial local model at $0\in\C^2$, so Dolbeault
cohomology is represented by holomorphic Taylor polynomials. Here
$\partial_{\Omega}$ is the divergence constraint selecting the Hamiltonian BF
sector. The kinematic differential of the closed theory below is
$D=d_{\R^2}+\bar\partial_{\C^2}$. Thus the Poincare lemma identifies
divergence-free vector fields with Hamiltonian potentials. The quotient by
$\partial_{\Omega}$-exact Hamiltonians is absent. Analysing degree by
degree:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\mathbf{PV}^{0,*}(\C^2) = \Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)\subset\Ker\partial$
    trivially.
  \item $\mathbf{PV}^{1,*}(\C^2)\cong\Omega^{1,*}(\C^2)$ via
    $\iota_{(-)}\omega$. Under this identification, with the Hamiltonian
    convention $\iota_{X_f}\omega=-df$ used below,
    $\Ker\partial_{\Omega}$ is the space of closed holomorphic one-forms.
    Lemma~\ref{lem:polynomial-poincare} identifies these with $df$, uniquely
    modulo constants.
  \item The constant Hamiltonians act trivially, since their Hamiltonian vector
    fields vanish. We therefore use the reduced polynomial algebra
    $$\bar A=\C[z_1,z_2]/\C\cdot1$$
    for nontrivial Hamiltonian fields.
\end{itemize}

\begin{lem}[Polynomial Poincare primitive]\label{lem:polynomial-poincare}
  Let
  $$\theta=a(z_1,z_2)\,dz_1+b(z_1,z_2)\,dz_2$$
  be a polynomial one-form on $\C^2$. If
  $\partial_{z_2}a=\partial_{z_1}b$, then there is a polynomial
  $f\in\C[z_1,z_2]$, unique modulo constants, such that $\theta=df$.
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  Define
  $$f(z_1,z_2)=\int_0^1
    \bigl(z_1a(tz_1,tz_2)+z_2b(tz_1,tz_2)\bigr)\,dt.$$
  Since $a$ and $b$ are polynomials, the integral is termwise division of each
  homogeneous term by its degree plus one, hence $f$ is polynomial. Differentiate
  under the integral sign. Closedness says
  $\partial_{z_2}a=\partial_{z_1}b$, so
  \begin{align*}
  \partial_{z_1}f
    &=\int_0^1\bigl(a(tz)+tz_1\partial_{z_1}a(tz)
       +tz_2\partial_{z_1}b(tz)\bigr)\,dt\\
    &=\int_0^1\bigl(a(tz)+t z_1\partial_{z_1}a(tz)
       +t z_2\partial_{z_2}a(tz)\bigr)\,dt\\
    &=\int_0^1\frac{d}{dt}\bigl(t\,a(tz)\bigr)\,dt
      =a(z).
  \end{align*}
  The same calculation, with $a$ and $b$ interchanged, gives
  $\partial_{z_2}f=b(z)$. Therefore $df=\theta$.
  If $df=dg$, then $d(f-g)=0$, hence $f-g$ is constant.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[Hamiltonian polyvector reduction]\label{prop:hamiltonian-polyvector-reduction}
  In the formal polynomial model on $(\C^2,\omega=dz_1\wedge dz_2)$, the
  divergence-closed polyvector-one fields reduce to Hamiltonian vector fields:
  $$\Ker(\partial_{\Omega}:\mathbf{PV}^{1}\to\mathbf{PV}^{0})
    \cong \{df:f\in\C[z_1,z_2]\}
    \cong \bar A.$$
  Under the convention
  $$X_f=(\partial_{z_1}f)\partial_{z_2}
    -(\partial_{z_2}f)\partial_{z_1},\qquad \iota_{X_f}\omega=-df,$$
  the commutator of vector fields is
  $$[X_f,X_g]=X_{\{f,g\}_{\bar A}}.$$
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Let $X=a_1\partial_{z_1}+a_2\partial_{z_2}$. The divergence operator is
  $\partial_{\Omega}X=\partial_{z_1}a_1+\partial_{z_2}a_2$. Contracting with
  $\omega$ gives
  $$\iota_X\omega=a_1\,dz_2-a_2\,dz_1.$$
  The equation $\partial_{\Omega}X=0$ is exactly the closedness of this
  one-form:
  $$d(\iota_X\omega)
    =(\partial_{z_1}a_1+\partial_{z_2}a_2)\,dz_1\wedge dz_2=0.$$
  By Lemma~\ref{lem:polynomial-poincare}, there is a polynomial $f$, unique
  modulo constants, such that $\iota_X\omega=-df$. Therefore
  $$a_1=-\partial_{z_2}f,\qquad a_2=\partial_{z_1}f,$$
  so $X=X_f$. Constants are exactly the kernel of $f\mapsto X_f$, giving
  $\bar A$.

  Finally, for any polynomial $h$,
  \begin{align*}
  [X_f,X_g](h)
    &=\{f,\{g,h\}\}-\{g,\{f,h\}\}\\
    &=\{\{f,g\},h\}
  \end{align*}
  by the Jacobi identity for the constant Poisson bracket. Thus
  $[X_f,X_g]=X_{\{f,g\}}$, and projecting constants gives the bracket on
  $\bar A$.
\end{proof}

For the Hamiltonian sector of the closed mixed field theory we replace
$\Ker\partial$ (on poly-1-vectors) by the Dolbeault-valued Hamiltonian vector
fields:
$$\Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)\text{-valued Hamiltonian vector fields},$$
i.e.\ sections of $\Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)$ identified with Hamiltonian vector
fields via the symplectic form $\omega = dz_1\wedge dz_2$. Concretely, a
section $f\in\Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)$ corresponds to the Hamiltonian vector field
$X_f=\{f,-\}_\omega$, where
$$\{f,g\}_\omega=
  \partial_{z_1}f\,\partial_{z_2}g-\partial_{z_2}f\,\partial_{z_1}g.$$
With this convention
$$X_f=(\partial_{z_1}f)\partial_{z_2}
  -(\partial_{z_2}f)\partial_{z_1},\qquad \iota_{X_f}\omega=-df.$$

\begin{rmk}
  The replacement is the elementary statement
  of Proposition~\ref{prop:hamiltonian-polyvector-reduction}. Constants are
  removed because $X_f=0$ exactly when $f$ is constant.
\end{rmk}

\subsection{Hamiltonian BF closed sector}

\subsubsection{The Action Functional}

Let
$$D=d_{\R^2}+\bar\partial_{\C^2}.$$
Write
$$\alpha\in\Omega^*(\R^2)\,\hat{\otimes}\,\Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)
  \widehat{\otimes}\widehat{\mathfrak h}[1]$$
for the Hamiltonian connection field and
$$\beta\in\Omega^*(\R^2)\,\hat{\otimes}\,\Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)
  \widehat{\otimes}\widehat{\mathfrak h}^{\vee}_{\mathrm{cont}}[2]$$
for its cotangent field. The bracket on form-valued Hamiltonians is obtained
by wedging the form parts and taking the Poisson bracket of the Hamiltonian
parts with the tensor-product Koszul sign:
$$[\eta\otimes f,\theta\otimes g]
  =(-1)^{|f||\theta|}(\eta\wedge\theta)\otimes\{f,g\},$$
for homogeneous form $\eta,\theta$ and homogeneous Hamiltonian coefficients
$f,g$. In the ghost-zero Hamiltonian calculations used below the coefficients
are even, so this sign is $+1$. The action is
$$S=\int_{\R^2\times\C^2}\Bigl\langle\beta,\,
  D\alpha+\frac12\{\alpha,\alpha\}\Bigr\rangle\,dz_1\,dz_2.$$
The pairing is the continuous evaluation pairing between
$\widehat{\mathfrak h}^{\vee}_{\mathrm{cont}}$ and $\widehat{\mathfrak h}$.
The dense polynomial submodel replaces these by $\bar A^\vee$ and $\bar A$ in
each finite degree.
For ghost-number-zero fields, $\alpha$ has total real/Dolbeault form degree
$1$ and $\beta$ has total degree $2$. Hence
$\beta\wedge D\alpha$ and $\beta\wedge\{\alpha,\alpha\}$ have total
real/Dolbeault degree $4$; multiplying by $dz_1\wedge dz_2$ gives a top real
degree $6$ form on $\R^2\times\C^2$.

\begin{rmk}
  The open mixed brane system lives on the brane line. The closed Hamiltonian
  BF theory lives on the full mixed space-time $\R^2\times\C^2$.
\end{rmk}

\subsubsection{Classical field content}

This theory is closely related to a 4-dimensional BF theory: $\beta$ plays the
role of the $B$-field (Lagrange multiplier) and the equations of motion of
$\beta$ enforce $F_\alpha = 0$, where
$$F_\alpha = D\alpha + \tfrac{1}{2}\{\alpha,\alpha\}$$
is the ``curvature'' of $\alpha$ (viewed as a connection on a bundle of
holomorphic-symplectic surfaces over $\R^2$).

Consider the space-time $\R^2_{x_i}\times\C^2_{z_i}$. The ghost-number-zero
components of $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are:
$$\alpha = \alpha_{x_i}\,dx_i + \alpha_{\bar z_j}\,d\bar z_j,\qquad
  \beta = \beta_{x_1 x_2}\,dx_1\,dx_2 + \beta_{x_i\bar z_j}\,dx_i\,d\bar z_j
  + \beta_{\bar z_1\bar z_2}\,d\bar z_1\,d\bar z_2.$$
Here the coefficients of $\alpha$ are Hamiltonian functions modulo constants.
The gauge symmetries are the BF-type transformations
$$\delta_{\varepsilon}\alpha=D\varepsilon+\{\alpha,\varepsilon\},\qquad
  \delta_{\varepsilon}\beta=\operatorname{ad}^{\vee}_{\varepsilon}\beta,$$
and
$$\delta_{\lambda}\alpha=0,\qquad
  \delta_{\lambda}\beta=D_{\alpha}\lambda,$$
where $D_{\alpha}=D+\{\alpha,-\}$ and $\lambda$ is a one-form valued in
$\bar A^{\vee}$. The second symmetry is reducible on shell: if
$\lambda=D_{\alpha}\mu$, then
$D_{\alpha}\lambda=D_{\alpha}^2\mu=\{F_{\alpha},\mu\}$, which vanishes when
$F_{\alpha}=0$.

Analysing $\alpha$ as a Hamiltonian function on $\C^2$: the component
$\alpha_{\bar z_j}d\bar z_j$ is the Hamiltonian potential whose vector field
$X_{\alpha_{\bar z_j}}d\bar z_j$ is the Beltrami differential encoding a
deformation of the complex structure of $\C^2$; the component
$\alpha_{x_i}dx_i$ similarly defines a connection on $\R^2$ valued in
Hamiltonian vector fields on $\C^2$, encoding a bundle of $\C^2$s over
$\R^2$. Together, the
equation of motion $F_\alpha=0$ says this data constitutes an integrable
deformation of complex structure with flat holonomy. The field $\beta$ appears
linearly in the action and enforces exactly this equation.

\begin{rmk}
  This is entirely analogous to BF theory, where $B$ is a Lagrange multiplier
  enforcing $F_A=0$, and the phase space is $T^*\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{flat}}$.
\end{rmk}

\subsubsection{Phase Space}
As an optional analytic model for propagators, formally compactify one real
direction and put the perturbative local model on
$\R\times S^1\times Y_{\mathrm{hol}}$, where $Y_{\mathrm{hol}}$ is holomorphic
symplectic and the calculation is completed at the trivial solution. This is a
compactified avatar of the formal local model, not a change of notation for the
product $X=M\times Y$ used above. The equations of motion $F_\alpha=0$ cut
out the Maurer-Cartan moduli problem for Hamiltonian vector fields on
$S^1\times Y_{\mathrm{hol}}$. Fix the analytic package of
Problem~\ref{prob:analytic-graph-realization} in this compact or formal
setting: elliptic complex, brane-defect or boundary condition, gauge fixing,
propagator, renormalization scheme, and anomaly cancellation. The perturbative
phase space
is the derived cotangent stack
$$T^*_{\mathrm{der}}\mathrm{MC}\bigl(\Omega^*(S^1)\widehat{\otimes}
  \Omega^{0,*}(Y_{\mathrm{hol}},\mathrm{Ham}(Y_{\mathrm{hol}})),D,\{-,-\}\bigr).$$
After passing to the underived tangent space at the trivial solution this
reduces to the ordinary cotangent bundle of the linearized deformation complex.

\subsection{Closed-open brane coupling}

This is the point at which the physical picture becomes a formula. A closed
Hamiltonian is not observed by the brane as an abstract vector field; it is
observed by evaluating it on the brane's matrix-valued normal coordinates and
taking the trace over Chan-Paton indices.

The local classical bulk-boundary operation is the Hamiltonian shadow of the
desired closed-open morphism: evaluate a closed Hamiltonian on the brane normal
fields. If the brane lies along $x_1$ and the closed field is
$dx_1\,z_1^k z_2^l$, the local boundary functional is
$$\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2+A[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr)
  + \int_\R dx_1\,\mathrm{Tr}\,\phi_1^k\phi_2^l.$$
This is a statement inside the formal local algebraic model: the closed
Hamiltonian is pulled back along the matrix-valued normal coordinate map
$z_i\mapsto\phi_i$ and then traced.
Construction~\ref{constr:boundary-evaluation} is the algebraic form of this
bulk-to-boundary prescription. Theorem~\ref{thm:bulk-boundary} below proves
that the resulting Hamiltonian evaluation preserves the classical bracket. This
is the degree-zero Hamiltonian component expected from a full closed-open map;
the full Poisson factorization-center morphism is not constructed here.

\begin{constr}[Reduced line-defect current kernel]\label{constr:reduced-line-defect-current-kernel}
  Let
  $$X=\R^2_{\mathrm{top}}\times\C^2_{\mathrm{hol}},\qquad
    L=\R_{x_1}\times\{x_2=0\}\times\{p\}\subset X,$$
  and let $i:L\hookrightarrow X$ be the inclusion. This is a line defect, not
  a spacetime boundary. In the reduced formal local model, a test density
  $a(t)\,dt$ on $L$ defines the defect-supported current $i_*(a(t)\,dt)$,
  locally written as $a(x_1)\delta(x_2)\delta_p\,dx_1dx_2$ together with the
  formal holomorphic delta-current at $p$.

  The degree-zero Hamiltonian part of the reduced PBW/Rees defect-current
  vertex is
  $$K_L^{\mathrm{cl}}(a\otimes f)
    =\int_L a(t)\,
      \overline{\mathrm{Tr}\,f(\phi_1(t),\phi_2(t))}\,dt,
    \qquad f\in\bar A.$$
  For the cotangent principal-part source, the reduced vertex sends
  $$B\otimes\rho[1]\longmapsto\Theta_\rho(B),\qquad
    B\in\mathcal D'_c(L),\quad \rho\in\mc P,$$
  using Proposition~\ref{prop:reduced-principal-part-boundary-current}. These
  formulas define a reduced PBW/Rees defect-current kernel. They are not a
  cochain-level map from closed CE observables, and they are not yet the
  quantum Costello brane-defect kernel: the Ward identity, anomaly cancellation,
  finite-scale propagator, and quantum moment-map compatibility remain separate
  obligations.
\end{constr}

\begin{rmk}
  Geometrically, specifying a closed Hamiltonian field on $\C^2$ amounts to
  equipping $\C^2$ with a connection, so that the brane can parallel-transport
  its internal degrees of freedom as it moves along $\R$.
\end{rmk}

For a factorization algebra $\mc A$ on the line, write
$\mathrm Z_{\mathrm{fact}}(\mc A)$ for the factorization algebra of $E_1$
central operations. In the locally constant one-dimensional case its stalk is
the Hochschild cochain complex of the corresponding $E_1$ algebra. The notation
$\mathrm Z^{P_0}_{\mathrm{fact}}(\mc A)$ means the same central operations with
the additional compatibility homotopies for the classical Poisson/BV bracket.
The proposition below constructs the ordinary central operations and the induced
$P_0$ bracket identity on stable connected local cohomology. It does not
construct all higher $P_0$ center homotopies.

\begin{prop}[Degree-zero Hamiltonian current shadow]\label{thm:hamiltonian-current-center-lift}
  Let $\mathrm{Obs}^{\mathrm{cl,red}}_{\mathrm{open},N}$ be the locally
  constant replacement of the classical open brane factorization algebra
  obtained after the de Rham jet contraction of Lemma~\ref{lem:derivative-jets}.
  Its stalk is the reduced BV algebra
  $A_N^{\mathrm{pt}}=(\mc R_N^{GL_N},Q)$.
  For an interval $I\subset\R$, put
  $$\mathfrak J_H(I)=C_c^\infty(I)\otimes\bar A,$$
  with extension by zero for inclusions and with current bracket
  $$[a\otimes f,b\otimes g]=ab\otimes\{f,g\}_{\bar A}.$$
  For $a\in C_c^\infty(I)$ and $f\in\bar A$, define the central operation
  $$\Phi_{I,N}(a\otimes f)(u)
    =\left(\int_I a(t)\,
      \overline{\mathrm{Tr}\,f(\phi_1(t),\phi_2(t))}\,dt\right)u.$$
  In the locally constant reduced model this is multiplication by the
  compactly supported Hamiltonian boundary observable. The assignments
  $\Phi_{I,N}$ are compatible with inclusions of intervals. Extending
  multiplicatively from generators gives a prefactorization map from the
  commutative special fibre
  $\mathbf S(\mathfrak J_H)$ to ordinary multiplication operations on
  $\mathrm{Obs}^{\mathrm{cl,red}}_{\mathrm{open},N}$.
  After projection to the stable connected Hamiltonian trace quotient, the
  generator-level local bracket satisfies the $P_0$ identity
  $$\{\Phi(a\otimes f),\Phi(b\otimes g)\}_{\mathrm{open}}
    =\Phi(ab\otimes\{f,g\}_{\bar A}).$$
  Its stalk at a point is the boundary map of
  Theorem~\ref{thm:bulk-boundary}.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  In the reduced open model $Q\phi_1=Q\phi_2=0$. Hence
  $\overline{\mathrm{Tr}\,f(\phi_1,\phi_2)}$ is $Q$-closed for every
  Hamiltonian $f$, and the compactly supported integral is $Q$-closed. Since the
  reduced open observable algebra is graded-commutative, multiplication by this
  closed element is a Hochschild $0$-cocycle; this is the interval-wise version
  of Proposition~\ref{prop:stalk-central-multiplication}. Extension by zero of
  the test function is compatible with restriction to a larger interval.
  For disjoint intervals the factorization product of two such operations is
  multiplication by the product
  $I_\partial(a,f)I_\partial(b,g)$; this is the multiplicative extension from
  $\mathfrak J_H$ to $\mathbf S(\mathfrak J_H)$, not the linear current bracket.

  The Poisson statement is local on the line. For compactly supported test
  functions $a,b$, Corollary~\ref{cor:local-bulk-boundary-coupling} gives
  $$\{I_\partial(a,f),I_\partial(b,g)\}_{\mathrm{open}}
    =I_\partial(ab,\{f,g\}_{\bar A}).$$
  Passing to the connected stable Hamiltonian quotient removes only the scalar
  Hamiltonian line, which is central for the bracket. Thus the current bracket
  is preserved. Taking $a$ and $b$ supported in a small coordinate interval and
  then passing to the locally constant stalk recovers
  Theorem~\ref{thm:bulk-boundary}.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[CE-source obstruction]\label{prop:ce-source-obstruction}
  Let $\bar A=\C[z_1,z_2]/\C\cdot1$ with the reduced Poisson bracket. Then
  $\bar A$ is a perfect Lie algebra:
  $$\bar A=\{\bar A,\bar A\}.$$
  If $\xi_{a,b}$ is the CE length-one coordinate dual to the Hamiltonian mode
  $z_1^az_2^b$, with $a+b>0$, then $\xi_{a,b}$ is not closed, and no CE
  cocycle has CE-length-one part $\xi_{a,b}$.
  Consequently the boundary assignment
  $$f\longmapsto \overline{\mathrm{Tr}\,f(\phi_1,\phi_2)}$$
  is a Hamiltonian-mode, or PBW/Koszul-dual, construction. It is not a
  cochain-level map out of
  $C^\bullet_{\mathrm{CE},\mathrm{cont}}(\mathfrak g)$ obtained by sending CE
  linear coordinates dual to Hamiltonian modes to the boundary observables.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  For every monomial $z_1^az_2^b$ one has, before quotienting constants,
  $$z_1^az_2^b=
    \left\{z_1,\frac{1}{b+1}z_1^az_2^{b+1}\right\}.$$
  Hence every nonconstant class in $\bar A$ is a Poisson bracket. If
  $\xi_{a,b}$ denotes the CE linear coordinate dual to the class of
  $z_1^az_2^b$, then
  $$d_{\mathrm{CE}}\xi_{a,b}\left(
    z_1,\frac{1}{b+1}z_1^az_2^{b+1}\right)=-1$$
  in the CE convention used here. Thus $\xi_{a,b}$ is not closed. The CE
  differential raises cochain length by one: if
  $O=\xi_{a,b}+O_{\geq2}$, then the CE-length-two part of
  $d_{\mathrm{CE}}O$ is $d_{\mathrm{CE}}\xi_{a,b}$, and no higher-length term
  can cancel it. Hence no closed CE cochain has linear Hamiltonian part
  $\xi_{a,b}$. A cochain-level map from closed CE observables must send closed
  source cochains to closed central operations. The Hamiltonian boundary
  observable is closed on the open side by
  Proposition~\ref{prop:stalk-central-multiplication}, but its source is the
  Hamiltonian mode $f$, not a closed CE generator. This is the source
  obstruction.
\end{proof}

\begin{thm}[Reduced derived Poisson center]\label{thm:reduced-derived-poisson-center}
  Let
  $$\mathfrak h=\prod_{d\geq1}H_d,\qquad
    \mathfrak h^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}=\bigoplus_{d\geq1}H_d^\vee$$
  be the product/direct-sum Tate Hamiltonian Lie algebra of the formal
  symplectic disk and its continuous dual. Put
  $$\mathfrak g=\mathfrak h\ltimes\mathfrak h^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}[1].$$
  Let $A_\partial=\widehat{\mathbf S}(\mathfrak h)$ be the completed stable
  degree-zero Hamiltonian trace algebra, with generators $O_i$ corresponding to
  a homogeneous topological basis $H_i$ of $\mathfrak h$. If
  $$[H_i,H_j]=C_{ij}^{k}H_k,$$
  with the constant target projected out, equip $A_\partial$ with the linear
  Poisson bracket
  $$\{O_i,O_j\}_\partial=C_{ij}^{k}O_k.$$
  Let
  $$\operatorname{PV}_{\mathrm{cont}}(A_\partial)
    =
    A_\partial\widehat\otimes
    \widehat\Lambda(\mathfrak h^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}})$$
  be the completed algebra of continuous polyvectors, with odd generators
  $\theta_i$ dual to the coordinates $O_i$. The Poisson bivector is
  $$\pi_\partial=\frac12 C_{ij}^{k}O_k\,\theta_i\theta_j,$$
  and the Poisson differential is $d_\pi=[\pi_\partial,-]$ in the Schouten
  convention.

  If $c_i$ is the CE coordinate dual to $H_i\in\mathfrak h$ and $u_i$ is the CE
  coordinate dual to the shifted cotangent generator $\delta_i[1]\in
  \mathfrak h^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}[1]$, then the assignment
  $$c_i\longmapsto\theta_i,\qquad u_i\longmapsto O_i$$
  extends to an isomorphism of completed graded-commutative dg algebras
  $$C^\bullet_{\mathrm{CE},\mathrm{cont}}(\mathfrak g)
    \xrightarrow{\ \sim\ }
    \bigl(\operatorname{PV}_{\mathrm{cont}}(A_\partial),d_\pi\bigr).$$
\end{thm}

\begin{proof}
  By the product/direct-sum convention already used in
  Proposition~\ref{prop:ce-koszul}, the continuous CE algebra of
  $\mathfrak g$ is freely generated, after completion, by the odd coordinates
  $c_i$ and the even coordinates $u_i$. The CE differential is dual to the
  brackets of $\mathfrak g$:
  $$d_{\mathrm{CE}}c_k=-\frac12 C_{ij}^{k}c_ic_j,\qquad
    d_{\mathrm{CE}}u_k=C_{kj}^{\ell}u_\ell c_j.$$
  The second formula is the coadjoint action of $\mathfrak h$ on
  $\mathfrak h^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}[1]$ written on the dual coordinates.

  The linear Poisson bivector gives the Lichnerowicz differential
  \cite{lichnerowicz-poisson}
  $$d_\pi\theta_k=-\frac12 C_{ij}^{k}\theta_i\theta_j,\qquad
    d_\pi O_k=C_{kj}^{\ell}O_\ell\theta_j.$$
  Hence $c_i\mapsto\theta_i$ and $u_i\mapsto O_i$ intertwine the two
  differentials. The underlying completed graded-commutative algebras are
  freely generated by corresponding variables, so this is an isomorphism.

  Continuity is the same coefficient-finiteness used in
  Lemma~\ref{lem:continuous-bar-cobar}: for each fixed output coefficient in a
  bracket of Hamiltonians only finitely many homogeneous input-degree pairs
  contribute, and the coadjoint input has finite support in
  $\mathfrak h^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}$. Thus the displayed sums define
  continuous derivations of the completed CE and polyvector algebras.
\end{proof}

\begin{rmk}
  The theorem is the reduced algebraic center calculation. It does not say that
  the scalar boundary function $O_f=\overline{\mathrm{Tr}\,f(\phi_1,\phi_2)}$
  is the image of the nonclosed CE coordinate $c_f$. It says that $O_f$ is the
  image of the CE coordinate $u_f$ dual to the shifted cotangent generator,
  while $c_f$ maps to the constant Hamiltonian vector-field operation
  $\theta_f$. This is why Proposition~\ref{prop:ce-source-obstruction} remains
  correct and why the full BV/factorization lift below has nontrivial content.
\end{rmk}

\begin{constr}[Compact current PBW/Rees source]\label{constr:compact-current-pbw-rees-source}
  Let $\mc P$ denote the principal-part module
  $$\mc P=\bigoplus_{\substack{a,b\geq0\\ a+b>0}}\C\,\rho_{a,b},\qquad
    \rho_{a,b}=z_1^{-a-1}z_2^{-b-1}\,dz_1dz_2,$$
  identified with $\mathfrak h^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}$ by the residue pairing
  of Proposition~\ref{prop:principal-part-coadjoint}. Put
  $$\mathfrak g_{\mathrm{pp}}=\mathfrak h\ltimes\mc P[1],$$
  with zero differential and bracket
  $$[f,g]=\{f,g\},\qquad [f,\rho[1]]=(f\cdot\rho)[1],\qquad
    [\rho[1],\sigma[1]]=0,$$
  where $f\cdot\rho=\pi_{\mathrm{pp}}\{f,\rho\}$. On monomials this action is
  $$z_1^pz_2^q\cdot\rho_{a,b}
    =-(pb-qa+p-q)\rho_{a-p+1,b-q+1},$$
  with negative-index targets zero.

  For an interval $I\subset\R$, define the compactly supported current Lie
  algebra
  $$\mathfrak J_{\mathfrak g}(I)

\end{constr}

\begin{prob}[PBW-source $P_0$ center action and CE comparison]\label{prob:pbw-p0-center-action}
  Construct a filtered factorization-center action of the compactly

\subsection{Operators in the open brane gauge theory and the large N limit}

The local-operator calculation is performed in the reduced BV model with
fields $\phi_1,\phi_2$ and the antifield $\psi=A^*$ for the Lagrange
multiplier $A$. We take $GL_N$-invariants in characteristic zero, so the
Chevalley part of the gauge BRST differential acts trivially on trace
functions by cyclicity.

The gauge field has one algebraic job: it imposes the moment-map equation
$[\phi_1,\phi_2]=0$. In BV language this equation is imposed by the Koszul
differential $Q\psi=[\phi_1,\phi_2]$. This is why the stable trace algebra
starts noncommutative, as cyclic words in two matrices, and then collapses in
cohomology to commutative monomials on the formal symplectic plane.

\begin{constr}[The reduced BV algebra at the insertion point]\label{constr:reduced-bv-algebra}
  At a fixed point of the brane line, introduce three matrix variables
  $$\phi_1,\phi_2\in\lie{gl}_N,\qquad \psi\in\lie{gl}_N[1].$$
  The reduced local polynomial algebra is
  $$\mc R_N=\C[(\phi_1)^i{}_j,(\phi_2)^i{}_j]
    \otimes\Lambda\bigl((\psi)^i{}_j\bigr).$$
  Its BRST differential is the derivation determined on generators by
  $$Q(\phi_1)^i{}_j=0,\qquad Q(\phi_2)^i{}_j=0,\qquad
    Q(\psi)^i{}_j=([\phi_1,\phi_2])^i{}_j.$$
  This formula is the algebraic shadow of the $A$-equation of motion:
  varying
  $$\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2+A[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr)$$
  with respect to $A$ gives $[\phi_1,\phi_2]=0$, and the antifield $\psi=A^*$
  records that equation as a Koszul differential.
\end{constr}

\begin{lem}[De Rham jets]\label{lem:derivative-jets}
  Positive jets in the topological direction vanish in local
  operator cohomology. Equivalently, every cohomology class has a representative
  determined by the values of the fields and antifields at the insertion point.
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  For a coordinate $t$ on the line, the de Rham part of the BRST operator is
  $d_{\R}$. On the jet algebra one has the contraction homotopy
  $[d_{\R},\iota_{\partial_t}]=\partial_t$. Hence a local expression containing
  a positive $t$-derivative is cohomologous to zero after applying the usual
  Euler homotopy on jet degree. This kills topological-direction jets and leaves
  the holomorphic $z_i$-derivatives used later on the closed side.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[Stable trace invariants]\label{prop:stable-trace-invariants}
  Let $F=\C\langle x_1,x_2\rangle$ and let $\mathrm{Cyc}(F)=F/[F,F]$ be the
  vector space of cyclic words. For each $N$, $GL_N$ acts on
  $\lie{gl}_N^2$ by simultaneous conjugation. The degreewise stable invariant
  algebra
  $$\C[\lie{gl}_\infty^2]^{GL_\infty}_{\mathrm{st}}
    :=\bigoplus_{d\geq0}\varprojlim_N
      \bigl(\C[\lie{gl}_N^2]^{GL_N}\bigr)_d$$
  with respect to block-restriction maps in each word-degree
  is the free commutative algebra generated by the nonempty cyclic words:
  $$\C[\lie{gl}_\infty^2]^{GL_\infty}_{\mathrm{st}}
    \cong\mathbf S\bigl(\mathrm{Cyc}(F)_{\mathrm{nonempty}}\bigr),$$
  where a cyclic word $w$ maps to the trace function
  $(\phi_1,\phi_2)\mapsto\mathrm{Tr}\,w(\phi_1,\phi_2)$.
  More precisely, in total positive word-degree $d$ this map is an isomorphism
  for every $N\geq d$, after omitting the scalar empty trace as a primitive
  generator.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Procesi--Razmyslov invariant theory \cites{procesi,razmyslov} gives two
  inputs. First, $\C[\lie{gl}_N^2]^{GL_N}$ is generated by trace functions of
  words in the two generic matrices. Second, all polynomial relations among
  these trace functions are consequences of the fundamental trace identities
  of matrix size $N$; in particular every homogeneous trace relation has total
  word-degree at least $N+1$. Fix a total word-degree $d$. For every
  $N\geq d$ the degree-$d$ relations are exactly cyclicity of the trace.
  Passing degree by degree to the stable inverse limit removes
  all finite-$N$ trace identities. The surviving identification is
  $\mathrm{Tr}(uv)=\mathrm{Tr}(vu)$. This is exactly the symmetric algebra on
  cyclic words.

\end{proof}

\begin{lem}[Stable marked trace invariants]\label{lem:stable-marked-traces}
  In the stable invariant theory used below, the primitive part linear or
  quadratic in an odd marked matrix $\psi$ is obtained from the corresponding
  even three-matrix trace invariant by polarization and skew-symmetrisation in
  the marked letters. The only stable single-trace relation is graded cyclicity:
  moving one $\psi$ through the trace has sign $+1$, while moving one $\psi$
  past another $\psi$ gives a minus sign.
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  Apply Proposition~\ref{prop:stable-trace-invariants} to three even generic
  matrices and take the homogeneous summand linear, respectively quadratic, in
  the third matrix. The Procesi--Razmyslov trace identities have no stable
  relations in the fixed total word-degree once $N$ is large enough. Declaring
  the marked matrix odd is the usual super-polarization: the linear part is
  unchanged, and the quadratic part is projected to the alternating component
  under exchange of the two marked occurrences. The trace relation therefore
  becomes exactly graded cyclicity. These are the signs used in the finite
  complex for one- and two-$\psi$ words.
\end{proof}

\begin{rmk}
  The empty trace is the finite-rank scalar $\mathrm{Tr}_N(1)=N$, which changes
  with $N$ and fails to define a stable primitive generator. The stable
  symmetric algebra still has its ordinary unit; what is omitted is the empty
  trace as a generator of the connected primitive part. The class
  $\mathrm{Tr}(\psi)$ is a genuine stable odd primitive class. It is excluded
  from the Hamiltonian comparison because constants have been removed from the
  closed Hamiltonian algebra.
\end{rmk}

Varying
$$S=\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2+A[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr)$$
with respect to $A$ gives the moment-map equation
$[\phi_1,\phi_2]=0$. In the reduced BV complex,
$$Q\psi=[\phi_1,\phi_2],\qquad Q\phi_1=Q\phi_2=0$$
on invariant trace functions.

\begin{constr}[Boundary evaluation of a closed Hamiltonian]\label{constr:boundary-evaluation}
  Let $f=\sum_{k,l}c_{k,l}z_1^kz_2^l\in\C[z_1,z_2]$. The brane replaces the
  two normal coordinates $z_1,z_2$ by the two adjoint scalar fields
  $\phi_1,\phi_2$. Choosing the ordered lift with all $\phi_1$'s to the left
  of all $\phi_2$'s gives the invariant function
  $$\widetilde\partial_{\mathrm{bb},N}(f)
    =\sum_{k,l}c_{k,l}\,\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)
    \in\mc R_N^{GL_N}.$$
  Let
  $$H^0_{\mathrm{Ham}}(\mc R_N^{GL_N},Q)
    :=H^0(\mc R_N^{GL_N},Q)/\C\cdot\mathrm{Tr}(1)$$
  as a vector space, and as a Lie algebra for the Poisson bracket. This is not
  an associative quotient of the operator algebra by its unit. Constants give
  the scalar $c_{0,0}N$ and therefore vanish in this Hamiltonian quotient.
  Thus the construction descends to a linear map
  $$\partial_{\mathrm{bb},N}:\bar A\to
    H^0_{\mathrm{Ham}}(\mc R_N^{GL_N},Q),\qquad
    [f]\longmapsto \overline{\widetilde\partial_{\mathrm{bb},N}(f)}.$$
  Let $\mathsf W_{k,l}$ be the set of words in two letters containing $k$
  copies of the first letter and $l$ copies of the second. The first descendant
  is obtained by inserting one Koszul variable and summing over all ordered
  lifts:
  For $k+l>0$ the descendant part of the Hamiltonian comparison is
  $$\partial_{\mathrm{bb},N}^{(1)}(\delta_{k,l}[1])=\Psi_{k,l},\qquad
    \Psi_{k,l}:=\sum_{w\in\mathsf W_{k,l}}
      \mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\psi\,w(\phi_1,\phi_2)\bigr).$$
  The same formula at $(k,l)=(0,0)$ gives the decoupled scalar antifield
  $\mathrm{Tr}(\psi)$, which is excluded from the Hamiltonian pairing because
  constants have been removed from $\bar A$.
\end{constr}

\begin{lem}[First descendants are cycles]\label{lem:first-descendant-cycles}
  For every $k,l\geq0$, the symmetrised trace $\Psi_{k,l}$ satisfies
  $Q\Psi_{k,l}=0$.
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  Write $x=\phi_1$ and $y=\phi_2$. Since $x$ and $y$ are even and
  $Q\psi=[x,y]$, one has
  $$Q\Psi_{k,l}
    =\sum_{w\in\mathsf W_{k,l}}\mathrm{Tr}\bigl((xy-yx)w(x,y)\bigr).$$
  Group the resulting traces by cyclic word of multidegree $(k+1,l+1)$. The
  coefficient of a cyclic word in the positive sum is the number of cyclic
  adjacent occurrences of the block $xy$; the coefficient in the negative sum
  is the number of cyclic adjacent occurrences of the block $yx$. On a circle
  the number of transitions from an $x$-run to a $y$-run equals the number of
  transitions from a $y$-run to an $x$-run. The two grouped sums are therefore
  equal, so $Q\Psi_{k,l}=0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[Primitive one-antifield Koszul homology]\label{prop:one-psi-homology}
  Fix $k,l\geq0$. The middle homology of the primitive single-trace complex
  with one displayed $\psi$ and $k,l$ displayed $x,y$ letters is
  one-dimensional, generated by the class of $\Psi_{k,l}$. The nonconstant part
  relevant to the Hamiltonian comparison is the direct sum over $k+l>0$.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Write $x=\phi_1$ and $y=\phi_2$. Let $\mathsf W_{k,l}$ be the set of ordinary
  words with $k$ letters $x$ and $l$ letters $y$, and let
  $\mathsf N_{a,b}$ be the set of cyclic words with $a$ letters $x$ and $b$
  letters $y$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:stable-marked-traces}, primitive cyclic words
  are taken modulo the graded cyclic rule
  $$\mathrm{Tr}(ab)=(-1)^{|a||b|}\mathrm{Tr}(ba),\qquad
  |x|=|y|=0,\quad |\psi|=1.$$
  Graded cyclicity gives a unique representative $\mathrm{Tr}(\psi w)$ for
  every one-$\psi$ cyclic word. Thus the relevant one-$\psi$ chain group is
  $$C_1(k,l)=\C\{\mathsf W_{k,l}\}.$$
  The zero-$\psi$ target is
  $$C_0(k,l)=\C\{\mathsf N_{k+1,l+1}\},$$
  and the differential is the incidence map
  $$\partial_1(w)=[xyw]_{\mathrm{cyc}}-[yxw]_{\mathrm{cyc}}.$$

  The two-$\psi$ source is generated by traces $\mathrm{Tr}(\psi u\psi v)$
  with $u$ and $v$ ordinary words whose total bidegree is $(k-1,l-1)$; if
  $k=0$ or $l=0$ this source is zero. Since $\psi$ is odd, exchanging the two
  displayed $\psi$'s changes sign. Applying $Q\psi=xy-yx$ gives
  \begin{align*}
  \partial_2(u,v)
    &=Q\,\mathrm{Tr}(\psi u\psi v)\\
    &=\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\psi vxyu-\psi vyxu-\psi uxyv+\psi uyxv\bigr),
  \end{align*}
  so, after removing the common front $\psi$,
  $$\partial_2(u,v)=vxyu-vyxu-uxyv+uyxv.$$
  A direct substitution shows $\partial_1\partial_2=0$.

  If $k=0$ or $l=0$, then $C_1(k,l)$ is spanned by $x^k$ or $y^l$,
  $\partial_1=0$, and $C_2(k,l)=0$. Hence the middle homology is already the
  line spanned by $\Psi_{k,l}$. Assume from now on that $k,l>0$.

  We compute the first homology of this finite complex. Form a graph
  $\Gamma_{k,l}$ whose vertices are the necklaces in $\mathsf N_{k+1,l+1}$ and
  whose oriented edges are the words $w\in\mathsf W_{k,l}$, with
  $$w:[yxw]_{\mathrm{cyc}}\longrightarrow[xyw]_{\mathrm{cyc}}.$$
  Then $\partial_1$ is the cellular boundary of this graph. For each pair
  $(u,v)$ as above attach the square whose oriented boundary is
  $vxyu-vyxu-uxyv+uyxv$. Denote the resulting two-complex by $X_{k,l}$.

  Equivalently, $X_{k,l}$ is the two-skeleton of the chip-moving state complex
  $Y_{k,l}$. The full complex $Y_{k,l}$ has an $r$-cell for $r$ mutually chosen
  chip moves on a cycle with $l+1$ oriented edges and $k+1$ indistinguishable
  chips, modulo cyclic relabelling of the edge labels. A vertex is a cyclic
  distribution of the chips among the vertices of this cycle, hence a necklace
  in $\mathsf N_{k+1,l+1}$. A one-cell moves one chosen chip across one oriented
  edge; cutting the cycle at this moving edge records exactly a word
  $w\in\mathsf W_{k,l}$, and its two endpoints are
  $[yxw]_{\mathrm{cyc}}$ and $[xyw]_{\mathrm{cyc}}$. A two-cell records two chip
  moves performed independently, and its oriented boundary is precisely
  $vxyu-vyxu-uxyv+uyxv$. Therefore the cellular chain complex of $Y_{k,l}$
  through degree two is exactly
  $$C_2(k,l)\longrightarrow C_1(k,l)\longrightarrow C_0(k,l),$$
  and the inclusion $X_{k,l}=Y_{k,l}^{(2)}\hookrightarrow Y_{k,l}$ induces an
  isomorphism on $H_1$, since cells of dimension at least three do not affect
  first homology.

  The homology of $Y_{k,l}$ is the homology of the cycle. For completeness we
  recall the elementary Abel-map argument. Before quotienting by cyclic
  relabelling, send a chip configuration to its Abel coordinate, the sum of the
  chip positions in $\R/(l+1)\Z$, and extend linearly over each cell. In the
  universal cover of the target circle, a fibre is described by weakly ordered
  lifted chip positions with fixed total sum; it is a convex polytope, subdivided
  by the same chip-crossing cells. Hence the Abel map is a cellular homotopy
  equivalence to $S^1$. The finite cyclic relabelling of the $y$-gaps is
  equivariant for this Abel map: it rotates the target circle, and every
  stabilizer acts affinely on a convex fibre, whose quotient is again
  contractible. Thus the quotient target is still a circle and the quotient
  complex $Y_{k,l}$ has the homology of $S^1$. Consequently
  $$H_1(X_{k,l};\C)\cong H_1(S^1;\C)\cong\C.$$

  Lemma~\ref{lem:first-descendant-cycles} identifies this representative as a
  cycle in the original trace complex. Its non-boundary property follows from
  the functional
  $$\epsilon\bigl(\mathrm{Tr}(\psi w)\bigr)=1,\qquad w\in\mathsf W_{k,l},$$
  kills every displayed two-$\psi$ boundary because each boundary has two
  positive and two negative summands, while
  $$\epsilon(\Psi_{k,l})=\binom{k+l}{k}\neq0.$$
  Thus the one-$\psi$ homology in bidegree $(k,l)$ is the one-dimensional line
  generated by $[\Psi_{k,l}]$.
\end{proof}

\begin{cor}[Boundary pairing for cotangent generators]\label{cor:cotangent-boundary-pairing}
  Let $\delta_{k,l}\in\mathfrak h^\vee$ be the functional dual to the monomial
  $z_1^kz_2^l\in\bar A$. The assignment
  $$\delta_{k,l}[1]\longmapsto [\Psi_{k,l}]$$
  extends to an isomorphism from the shifted Taylor-dual space
  $\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$ to the nonconstant primitive one-$\psi$ brane homology.
\end{cor}

\begin{proof}
  The monomials $z_1^kz_2^l$ with $k+l>0$ form the polynomial basis of
  $\bar A$ and the topological basis of $\widehat{\mathfrak h}$; the
  $\delta_{k,l}$ form the continuous coefficient-dual basis. By
  Proposition~\ref{prop:one-psi-homology}, the primitive one-$\psi$ homology has
  one generator in each bidegree, namely $[\Psi_{k,l}]$. Discarding the scalar
  bidegree $(0,0)$ and sending the dual basis vector in every nonconstant
  bidegree to that generator gives a filtered vector-space isomorphism. This is
  the boundary pairing used in the PBW special fibre; the closed module
  structure remains coadjoint.
\end{proof}

\begin{lem}[Adjacent swaps]\label{lem:adjacent-swaps}
  In the invariant reduced BV cohomology,
  $$\mathrm{Tr}(u\phi_1\phi_2v)-\mathrm{Tr}(u\phi_2\phi_1v)$$
  is $Q$-exact for all words $u,v$.
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  By cyclicity of the trace,
  \begin{align*}
  Q\,\mathrm{Tr}(\psi vu)
    &=\mathrm{Tr}([\phi_1,\phi_2]vu)\\
    &=\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\phi_2vu-\phi_2\phi_1vu)\\
    &=\mathrm{Tr}(u\phi_1\phi_2v)-\mathrm{Tr}(u\phi_2\phi_1v).
  \end{align*}
\end{proof}

\begin{cor}[Matrix evaluation is commutative in cohomology]\label{cor:matrix-evaluation-well-defined}
  If two words in $\phi_1,\phi_2$ have the same numbers of $\phi_1$ and
  $\phi_2$, then their traces define the same class in the reduced BV
  cohomology. Consequently $\partial_{\mathrm{bb},N}(f)$ is determined by the
  commutative polynomial $f\in\C[z_1,z_2]$ and is independent of the chosen
  ordered lift.
\end{cor}

\begin{proof}
  Adjacent transpositions generate all reorderings of a finite word. Each
  adjacent transposition of the adjacent letters $\phi_1,\phi_2$ changes the
  trace by a $Q$-exact term by Lemma~\ref{lem:adjacent-swaps}; cyclicity moves
  the chosen adjacent pair into the displayed position of that lemma. Summing
  the resulting exact differences proves the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[Stalk central multiplication]\label{prop:stalk-central-multiplication}
  Let
  $$A_N^{\mathrm{pt}}=(\mc R_N^{GL_N},Q)$$
  be the invariant reduced open BV algebra at a brane insertion point. For
  $f\in\C[z_1,z_2]$, set
  $$B_f=\widetilde\partial_{\mathrm{bb},N}(f)
    =\sum_{k,l}c_{k,l}\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l).$$
  Then $QB_f=0$. Regard $B_f$ as a Hochschild $0$-cochain; its associated
  central operation is multiplication
  $$L_{B_f}:A_N^{\mathrm{pt}}\to A_N^{\mathrm{pt}},\qquad
    a\longmapsto B_fa.$$
  This Hochschild $0$-cochain is closed. The same statement holds for
  multiplication by a closed one-antifield class $\Psi_{k,l}$. After projection to the
  Hamiltonian quotient and stabilization in $N$, the degree-zero classes satisfy
  $$\{B_f,B_g\}_{\mathrm{open}}=B_{\{f,g\}_{\bar A}}.$$
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Since $Q\phi_1=Q\phi_2=0$, every $B_f$ is $Q$-closed. The algebra
  $A_N^{\mathrm{pt}}$ is graded-commutative, so the Hochschild differential of
  a $0$-cochain $b$ is
  $$d_{\mathrm{Hoch}}b(a)=Qb\,a+
    ba-(-1)^{|b||a|}ab.$$
  This vanishes for every closed graded-central element $b$. The classes
  $\Psi_{k,l}$ are closed by Lemma~\ref{lem:first-descendant-cycles} and are
  central for the same graded-commutative reason. Ordered-lift independence for
  $B_f$ is cohomological and is exactly
  Corollary~\ref{cor:matrix-evaluation-well-defined}. The Poisson identity is
  Theorem~\ref{thm:bulk-boundary}.
\end{proof}

Let $\bar A=\C[z_1,z_2]/\C\cdot1$. The removed constant corresponds on the
gauge side to the scalar $\mathrm{Tr}(1)=N$ and has zero Hamiltonian vector
field.

\begin{prop}[Connected brane Hamiltonian sector]\label{prop:brane-ops}
  In the stable large-$N$ open BV model, the connected Hamiltonian descendant
  sector freely generated by the nonempty primitive ghost-zero traces and the
  nonconstant primitive one-$\psi$ Koszul homology is
  $$\mc{O}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{brane},H}
    \cong \mathbf{S}\bigl(\bar A\oplus \bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}[1]\bigr).$$
  The class of $z_1^kz_2^l$ with $k+l>0$ corresponds to
  $\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)}$. The corresponding descendant
  basis vector corresponds to the symmetrised trace with one $\psi$ and $k,l$
  copies of $\phi_1,\phi_2$.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Proposition~\ref{prop:stable-trace-invariants} gives the free commutative
  algebra on cyclic words.
  Lemma~\ref{lem:adjacent-swaps} identifies any two cyclic words with the same
  numbers of $\phi_1$ and $\phi_2$, because adjacent transpositions generate
  all reorderings. Thus the connected ghost-zero cyclic words reduce to the
  monomials $\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)}$ with $k+l>0$.
  Proposition~\ref{prop:one-psi-homology} computes the primitive one-$\psi$
  homology in each multidegree; the nonconstant generators are $\Psi_{k,l}$ with
  $k+l>0$. Products of the connected ghost-zero primitive classes and these
  one-$\psi$ primitive classes give the free graded-commutative multi-trace
  algebra in the Hamiltonian sector.
  By definition of the Hamiltonian sector, single traces containing two or
  more occurrences of $\psi$ are excluded from this algebra.
\end{proof}

\begin{rmk}
  The word ``sector'' is literal. The calculation above presents the stable
  algebra generated by the primitive no-$\psi$ Hamiltonians and the nonconstant
  primitive one-$\psi$ homology generated by $\Psi_{k,l}$ for $k+l>0$. The full
  invariant Koszul homology in higher $\psi$-count lies outside this sector, as
  does the decoupled scalar class $\mathrm{Tr}(\psi)$.
\end{rmk}

\begin{prob}[Full primitive Koszul homology]\label{prob:full-psi-homology}
  Let
  $$A_{\psi}=\C\langle x,y,\psi\rangle,\qquad
    |x|=|y|=0,\quad |\psi|=1,$$
  with differential $d\psi=xy-yx$ and $dx=dy=0$. Put
  $$\mathrm{Cyc}(A_{\psi})=A_{\psi}/[A_{\psi},A_{\psi}]_{\mathrm{gr}},\qquad
    [uv]=(-1)^{|u||v|}[vu].$$
  Give $x,y,\psi$ weights
  $$\mathrm{wt}(x)=(1,0),\qquad \mathrm{wt}(y)=(0,1),\qquad
    \mathrm{wt}(\psi)=(1,1).$$
  For fixed $(R,S)$ define
  $$K^p_{R,S}=\C\{[w]_{\mathrm{cyc}}:
    \#\psi(w)=p,\ \#x(w)=R-p,\ \#y(w)=S-p\},$$
  with $0\leq p\leq\min(R,S)$. The differential is
  \begin{align*}
  \partial[w]
    =\sum_{i:a_i=\psi}(-1)^{\#\{\psi\text{ before }i\}}
    [&a_1\cdots a_{i-1}xy\,a_{i+1}\cdots a_n\\
     &-a_1\cdots a_{i-1}yx\,a_{i+1}\cdots a_n]_{\mathrm{cyc}}.
  \end{align*}
  Prove the primitive homology formula
  $$H_p(K_{R,S})\cong
    \begin{cases}
      \C\cdot[x^Ry^S],&p=0,\\
      \C\cdot[\Psi_{R-1,S-1}],&p=1,\ R,S\geq1,\\
      0,&p\geq2.
    \end{cases}$$
  Equivalently, prove that the primitive stable Koszul homology is
  $\C[x,y]\oplus\C[x,y]\theta$ with $|\theta|=1$ and
  $\mathrm{wt}(\theta)=(1,1)$.

  The required new ingredient is the all-$p$ orientation statement identifying
  this graded cyclic complex with the cellular chains of the chip-moving
  necklace state complex, together with the Abel-map proof that the relevant
  state complex has the homology of $S^1$. Proposition~\ref{prop:one-psi-homology}
  proves exactly the $p\leq2$ skeleton needed for the Hamiltonian sector. The
  complete theorem also requires the all-$\psi$ stable trace presentation and an
  exact computational check of the finite complex.
\end{prob}

\subsection{Operators in the closed Hamiltonian sector}\label{sec:grav-ops}

Let $\mathfrak h=\widehat{\mathfrak h}=\C[[z_1,z_2]]/\C\cdot1$ with the
projected Poisson bracket, and let $\mathfrak h^\vee$ be its continuous Taylor
dual. The
cotangent field is a coadjoint module, so the local Hamiltonian Lie algebra is
$$\mathfrak g=\mathfrak h\ltimes\mathfrak h^\vee[1].$$
For $f,g\in\mathfrak h$ and $\lambda,\mu\in\mathfrak h^\vee$, the brackets are
$$[f,g]=\{f,g\},\qquad [f,\eta_\lambda]=\eta_{\operatorname{ad}^{\vee}_f\lambda},
  \qquad [\eta_\lambda,\eta_\mu]=0,$$
where $(\operatorname{ad}^{\vee}_f\lambda)(g)=-\lambda(\{f,g\})$.
On polynomial monomials in the dense subalgebra $\bar A$ this reads
$$[z_1^kz_2^l,z_1^mz_2^n]
  =\overline{(kn-lm)z_1^{k+m-1}z_2^{l+n-1}},$$
where the bar means projection to $\bar A$; in particular the constant bracket
of $z_1$ and $z_2$ is zero in the reduced Hamiltonian Lie algebra.

\begin{lem}[Shifted coadjoint signs]\label{lem:shifted-coadjoint-signs}
  Let $\eta_\lambda$ denote the suspension of
  $\lambda\in\mathfrak h^\vee$ into $\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$. Since the
  Hamiltonians in $\mathfrak h$ are even,
  $$[f,\eta_\lambda]=\eta_{\operatorname{ad}^{\vee}_f\lambda},\qquad
    [\eta_\lambda,\eta_\mu]=0.$$
  Consequently the enveloping algebra relations involving two shifted
  coadjoint generators are odd-commutative:
  $$\eta_\lambda\eta_\mu+\eta_\mu\eta_\lambda=0.$$
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  The semidirect product bracket before shifting is the ordinary coadjoint
  action of the even Lie algebra $\mathfrak h$ on its continuous dual. Suspending
  the module raises the degree of every $\lambda$ by one. The mixed-bracket sign
  is $+1$ because $|f|=0$. The shifted module is
  abelian as a Lie ideal, so its bracket with itself is zero; in the associative
  enveloping algebra this zero Lie bracket gives
  $\eta_\lambda\eta_\mu-(-1)^{1\cdot1}\eta_\mu\eta_\lambda=0$.
\end{proof}

The kinematic BRST operator is
$$Q_0=d_{\R^2}+\bar\partial_{\C^2}.$$
The same jet contraction as in Lemma~\ref{lem:derivative-jets}, together with
the Dolbeault Poincare lemma in the formal local model, leaves precisely the
holomorphic Taylor coefficients. Thus the linear local coordinates are the
functionals
$$\partial_{z_1}^k\partial_{z_2}^l\alpha|_0,\qquad
  \partial_{z_1}^k\partial_{z_2}^l\beta|_0.$$
With the coefficient-dual convention fixed above, these are represented by
$(k!l!)^{-1}\partial_{z_1}^k\partial_{z_2}^l|_0$. Since zero-form components of
$\alpha$ and $\beta$ have negative field ghost numbers, the corresponding
linear functions have positive ghost numbers.

\begin{prop}[Closed local operators are continuous CE cochains]\label{prop:grav-ops}
  The classical local operators of the formal Hamiltonian cotangent BF sector are
  the continuous Chevalley-Eilenberg cochains
  $$\mc{O}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{grav}}
    \cong C^\bullet_{\mathrm{CE},\mathrm{cont}}(\mathfrak g)
    =\widehat{\mathbf{S}}\bigl(\mathfrak g^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}[-1]\bigr),$$
  with CE differential dual to the Lie brackets above.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Work at the formal insertion point. The jet algebra of the fields is the
  completed free graded-commutative algebra on the Taylor coefficients of
  $\alpha$ and $\beta$. The kinematic differential
  $Q_0=d_{\R^2}+\bar\partial_{\C^2}$ contracts all positive real and
  anti-holomorphic jets: in the real directions the homotopy is
  $[d_{\R^2},\iota_{\partial_{x_i}}]=\partial_{x_i}$, and in the
  anti-holomorphic directions it is the formal Dolbeault Poincare homotopy.
  The $Q_0$-cohomology of the jet algebra is therefore generated by the
  holomorphic Taylor coefficients alone.

  The remaining $\alpha$-coefficients are Taylor Hamiltonians modulo constants,
  hence elements of $\mathfrak h=\widehat{\mathfrak h}$; degreewise polynomial
  coefficients form the dense subalgebra $\bar A$. The cotangent field $\beta$
  supplies the odd shifted continuous dual $\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$. Thus the
  linear space of residual local modes is
  $\mathfrak g=\mathfrak h\ltimes\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$.

  The nonlinear BRST differential is determined by the BV Hamiltonian
  $$S=\int\left\langle\beta,D\alpha+\frac12\{\alpha,\alpha\}\right\rangle.$$
  The linear part is exactly $Q_0$, already killed in cohomology. The quadratic
  term sends two Hamiltonian $\alpha$-modes $f,g$ to their reduced Poisson
  bracket $\{f,g\}$, and the action on the cotangent variable is the coadjoint
  action displayed above. The quadratic term has zero component with two
  cotangent inputs, so $[\eta_\lambda,\eta_\mu]=0$. Hence the residual BRST differential on
  functions is precisely the derivation dual to the Lie bracket on
  $\mathfrak g$, which is the Chevalley-Eilenberg differential. Continuity
  records the completion over polynomial degree.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Koszul Duality}

\begin{defn}
  For an augmented complete dg algebra $B$, its Koszul dual is
  $$B^!=\RHom_B(\C,\C).$$
  We regard commutative Chevalley-Eilenberg cochains as $E_1$ algebras by
  restriction. All bar and cobar constructions below are taken in the category
  of complete filtered Tate vector spaces, with continuous maps. The
  completions are by symmetric or tensor length together with the coefficient
  topology coming from Taylor degree.
  For a Tate space $V$, the notation $V^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}$ means the
  continuous dual with its dual Tate topology, $\widehat\otimes$ means the
  completed Tate tensor product, and
  $$\widehat{\mathbf S}^n(V)=(V^{\widehat\otimes n})_{\mathbb S_n}.$$
  Chevalley-Eilenberg chains below are direct sums in CE length with completed
  coefficients in each length; CE cochains, completed cobar algebras, and
  completed enveloping algebras are products in the corresponding length
  filtration.
\end{defn}

\begin{lem}[Tate CE chains, completed PBW, and Koszul duality]\label{lem:continuous-bar-cobar}
  Let
  $$L=\left(\prod_{d\geq1}P_d\right)\oplus
    \left(\bigoplus_{d\geq1}Q_d\right)$$
  be a graded Tate vector space over $\C$, with all $P_d,Q_d$ finite
  dimensional and with chosen topological coefficient bases. Give $\prod P_d$
  the product topology and $\bigoplus Q_d$ the discrete direct-sum topology.
  Assume that $L$ is a graded Lie algebra whose bracket is continuous and
  coefficient-finite for this product/direct-sum topology:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item for the product-product part, every fixed output coefficient receives
      contributions from only finitely many input degree pairs;
    \item for every finite direct-sum support $S\subset\Z_{\geq1}$ in a $Q$
      input, the bracket with an arbitrary product input depends on only
      finitely many product degrees and lands in a finite direct sum of $Q$
      degrees determined by $S$;
    \item the same finite-window condition holds after restricting both inputs
      to finite sets of degrees.
  \end{enumerate}
  Equivalently, every coefficient of the bracket on completed tensors is a
  finite sum once the direct-sum inputs have their prescribed finite support.
  Then
  $$L^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}
    =\bigoplus_{d\geq1}P_d^\vee\oplus\prod_{d\geq1}Q_d^\vee,$$
  the natural map $L\to L^{\vee\vee}_{\mathrm{cont}}$ is an isomorphism in this
  Tate category. Define
  $$C_*^{\mathrm{CE}}(L)=
    \bigoplus_{n\geq0}\widehat{\mathbf S}^n(L[1]),$$
  where only the coefficient topology inside each symmetric power is completed.
  Its continuous dual is
  $$C^\bullet_{\mathrm{CE},\mathrm{cont}}(L)
    =\Hom_{\mathrm{cont}}\bigl(C_*^{\mathrm{CE}}(L),\C\bigr)
    =\prod_{n\geq0}
      \Hom_{\mathrm{cont}}\bigl(\widehat{\mathbf S}^n(L[1]),\C\bigr)
    =\widehat{\mathbf S}\bigl(L^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}[-1]\bigr).$$
  With completed tensor and symmetric algebras defined by tensor length and the
  coefficient topology, the canonical twisting map induces a filtered
  quasi-isomorphism
  $$\widehat{\Omega}\,C_*^{\mathrm{CE}}(L)
    \xrightarrow{\ \sim\ }\widehat U(L),$$
  and
  $$\operatorname{gr}_{\mathrm{PBW}}\widehat U(L)
    \cong\widehat{\mathbf S}(L).$$
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  A continuous functional on $\prod P_d$ depends on finitely many homogeneous
  coordinates. A continuous functional on the discrete direct sum
  $\bigoplus Q_d$ is an arbitrary product of degreewise functionals. This proves
  the displayed continuous-dual formula and the corresponding Tate reflexivity;
  it is the product/direct-sum Tate convention of
  Beilinson--Feigin--Mazur \cite{beilinson-feigin-mazur}.

  The coefficient topology is not implemented by truncating Taylor degree as a
  Lie quotient. In the Hamiltonian application the Poisson bracket lowers
  Hamiltonian degree, and the coadjoint action can raise dual degree as a
  formal degree rule. The relevant finiteness is instead the support condition
  in the direct-sum dual input: every actual dual input has finite Taylor
  support. The Chevalley-Eilenberg coalgebra is therefore not completed in
  symmetric length. It is the conilpotent coalgebra
  $$C_*^{\mathrm{CE}}(L)=
    \bigoplus_{n\geq0}\widehat{\mathbf S}^n(L[1]),$$
  with completed coefficients in each length. The bracket defines the usual CE
  coderivation, which lowers symmetric length by one. The coefficient-finiteness
  hypothesis makes each output coefficient a finite sum, hence the coderivation
  is continuous. Dualizing the direct sum over $n$ gives the completed product
  over cochain length, namely
  $\widehat{\mathbf S}(L^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}[-1])$.

  Filter $T(L)$ by word length and complete. The closed enveloping algebra is
  the quotient of $\widehat T(L)$ by the closed ideal generated by
  $$xy-(-1)^{|x||y|}yx-[x,y].$$
  Choose an ordered topological coefficient basis compatible with finite degree
  windows. PBW straightening replaces one inversion by the ordered word plus one
  bracket term. Coefficient-finiteness implies that every coefficient of every
  straightened completed word is computed by finitely many reductions. Thus
  ordinary graded PBW, applied on finite-dimensional degree windows and then
  passed to the completion, gives
  $$\operatorname{gr}_{\mathrm{PBW}}\widehat U(L)
    \cong\widehat{\mathbf S}(L)$$
  \cites{priddy,loday-vallette}.

  The cobar comparison is checked with the same PBW/order filtration. The
  canonical Lie twisting cochain
  $$C_*^{\mathrm{CE}}(L)\longrightarrow\widehat U(L)$$
  sends a suspended cogenerator to the corresponding enveloping generator. Its
  associated graded map is the completed Koszul complex of
  $\widehat{\mathbf S}(L)$. The usual Euler contracting homotopy on the Koszul
  complex is $h=\iota_E/N$ in positive total Koszul degree, where $E$ is the
  Euler vector field and $N$ is total polynomial plus exterior degree. This
  homotopy is coefficientwise finite, preserves each finite coefficient window,
  and therefore extends to the coefficient and length completions. Since the
  filtrations are complete and separated, the filtered map
  $\widehat{\Omega}\,C_*^{\mathrm{CE}}(L)\to\widehat U(L)$ is a
  quasi-isomorphism. Finally, Tate reflexivity identifies the continuous bar
  dual of $C^\bullet_{\mathrm{CE},\mathrm{cont}}(L)$ with this completed cobar
  algebra.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[Koszul dual of continuous CE cochains]\label{prop:ce-koszul}
  Let $\mathfrak g=\mathfrak h\ltimes\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$ be the completed
  graded Lie algebra above, and let
  $$B=C^\bullet_{\mathrm{CE},\mathrm{cont}}(\mathfrak g)
    =\widehat{\mathbf{S}}\bigl(\mathfrak g^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}[-1]\bigr).$$
  Then
  $$B^!\simeq \widehat{U(\mathfrak g)},$$
  the completed graded universal enveloping algebra.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Put
  $$H_d=\operatorname{Span}\{z_1^az_2^b:a+b=d\},\qquad
    \mathfrak h=\prod_{d\geq1}H_d,\qquad
    \mathfrak h^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}=\bigoplus_{d\geq1}H_d^\vee.$$
  Thus
  $$\mathfrak g=\left(\prod_{d\geq1}H_d\right)
    \ltimes\left(\bigoplus_{d\geq1}H_d^\vee\right)[1]$$
  is a Tate Lie algebra. The bracket is degreewise finite:
  $$\{H_p,H_q\}\subset H_{p+q-2},$$
  with the degree-zero summand projected to zero. Thus the degree-$r$
  coefficient of $\{f,g\}$ depends only on the finitely many pairs
  $p+q=r+2$. For the coadjoint action, if $f\in H_p$ and
  $\lambda\in H_q^\vee$, then
  $$\operatorname{ad}^{\vee}_f\lambda\in H_{q-p+2}^\vee$$
  when $q-p+2\geq1$, and it is zero otherwise. Finite-support Taylor duals
  remain finite-support: for fixed $\lambda\in H_q^\vee$ only the Hamiltonian
  degrees $p\leq q+1$ can act nontrivially, and for a finite direct-sum support
  in $\mathfrak h^\vee$ the union of those contributing $p$ is finite. Thus the
  product/direct-sum coefficient-finiteness hypotheses of
  Lemma~\ref{lem:continuous-bar-cobar} hold. The abelian bracket on
  $\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$ is trivial. The Taylor coefficient topology is
  independent of the PBW/order filtration used for the Rees algebra below.

  Let
  $$C_*^{\mathrm{CE}}(\mathfrak g)
    =\bigoplus_{n\geq0}\widehat{\mathbf S}^n(\mathfrak g[1])$$
  be the coefficient-completed, length-direct-sum Chevalley-Eilenberg
  coalgebra, with coderivation determined by the bracket of $\mathfrak g$. By
  definition
  $$B=\Hom_{\mathrm{cont}}\bigl(C_*^{\mathrm{CE}}(\mathfrak g),\C\bigr).$$
  The Chevalley-Eilenberg coalgebra is conilpotent for symmetric length. The
  augmentation $B\to\C$ has the continuous bar resolution in the category of
  complete filtered Tate spaces, and
  Lemma~\ref{lem:continuous-bar-cobar} identifies
  $$\RHom_B(\C,\C)\simeq
    \widehat{\Omega}\,C_*^{\mathrm{CE}}(\mathfrak g),$$
  the completed cobar algebra of the CE coalgebra. The completed Lie bar-cobar
  comparison in the lemma identifies this cobar algebra with the completed
  associative algebra generated by $\mathfrak g$ modulo the closed graded Lie
  relations; this is $\widehat{U(\mathfrak g)}$.

  Thus, for the generators $f,g\in\mathfrak h$ and
  $\eta_\lambda,\eta_\mu\in\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$, the completed enveloping
  algebra has relations
  \begin{align*}
    fg-gf&=\{f,g\},\\
    f\eta_\lambda-\eta_\lambda f&=\eta_{\operatorname{ad}^{\vee}_f\lambda},\\
    \eta_\lambda\eta_\mu+\eta_\mu\eta_\lambda&=0.
  \end{align*}
\end{proof}

For comparison with the classical brane algebra introduce the completed
PBW/order Rees enveloping algebra
$$U_{\hbar}(\mathfrak g)
  =\widehat T(\mathfrak g)[[\hbar]]\Big/
  \overline{\left\langle
  xy-(-1)^{|x||y|}yx-\hbar[x,y]\right\rangle},$$
where the closure is for tensor length and the Tate coefficient topology.
This Rees parameter records word length in the enveloping algebra and is
independent of Taylor degree. At $\hbar=0$ one obtains the completed PBW special
fibre
$$U_{\hbar}(\mathfrak g)/(\hbar)\cong
  \widehat{\mathbf{S}}(\mathfrak g)
  =\widehat{\mathbf{S}}\bigl(\widehat{\mathfrak h}\oplus\mathfrak h^\vee[1]\bigr).$$
After choosing the monomial-dual Taylor basis
$\mathfrak h^\vee\cong\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}$, the degreewise polynomial
subalgebra $\mathbf{S}(\bar A\oplus\mathfrak h^\vee[1])$ is the stable classical
brane Hamiltonian descendant algebra of Proposition~\ref{prop:brane-ops}. Thus
the degreewise polynomial part of the PBW associated graded of the closed
Koszul dual is the stable classical brane Hamiltonian descendant algebra. The
interacting closed Hamiltonian bracket deforms the closed special fibre to
$\widehat{U(\mathfrak g)}$; on the brane side the deformation checked here is
the degree-zero Hamiltonian trace bracket below.

\subsubsection{First-order corrections, order hbar one}

Under the Koszul duality correspondence we have
$$z_1^kz_2^l \leftrightarrow
  \overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)}.$$
The reduced Poisson bracket on the gravity side is:
$$\{z_1^kz_2^l,\, z_1^mz_2^n\}_{\bar A}
  =\overline{(kn - lm)\,z_1^{k+m-1}z_2^{l+n-1}}.$$
If an exponent in the displayed monomial is negative then the coefficient
$kn-lm$ is already zero; if the displayed monomial is constant, its class in
$\bar A$ is zero.
The matching bracket on the open side is the canonical bracket induced by the
first-order kinetic term $\int\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\,d\phi_2)$, with the sign
convention $\iota_{X_f}\omega=-df$ fixed in the AKSZ remark above:
$$\{(\phi_1)^i{}_j,(\phi_2)^k{}_l\}
  =\delta^i_l\delta^k_j,\qquad
  \{(\phi_1)^i{}_j,(\phi_1)^k{}_l\}=0=\{(\phi_2)^i{}_j,(\phi_2)^k{}_l\}.$$

\begin{lem}[Hamiltonian trace bracket]\label{lem:trace-bracket-descends}
  The canonical matrix Poisson bracket descends to the invariant reduced BV
  cohomology and then to the Hamiltonian Lie quotient by constant trace
  classes.
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  For $\epsilon\in\lie{gl}_N$, the component
  $\mu_\epsilon=-\mathrm{Tr}(\epsilon[\phi_1,\phi_2])$ of the moment map
  generates the infinitesimal conjugation action, with the sign fixed by the
  convention for the bracket. Hence $\{F,\mu_\epsilon\}=0$ for every invariant
  function $F$. If $I_\mu$ is the ideal generated by all $\mu_\epsilon$, then
  $\{F,\mu_\epsilon K\}=\mu_\epsilon\{F,K\}$ lies again in $I_\mu$. Therefore
  the bracket of invariant functions descends to
  $(\mc R_N/I_\mu)^{GL_N}$, equivalently to the degree-zero reduced BV
  cohomology. The central line $\C\cdot\mathrm{Tr}(1)$ is central for this Lie
  bracket, so the Hamiltonian vector-space quotient by constants is compatible
  with the bracket. No associative quotient by the unit is taken.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[First-order trace bracket]\label{prop:first-order-bracket}
  In the stable connected Hamiltonian trace cohomology,
  $$\{\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)},
      \overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^m\phi_2^n)}\}
    =\overline{(kn-lm)\mathrm{Tr}
      (\phi_1^{k+m-1}\phi_2^{l+n-1})}.$$
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Write $F_{k,l}=\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)$. The matrix Poisson bracket
  induced by
  $$\{(\phi_1)^i{}_j,(\phi_2)^k{}_l\}=\delta^i_l\delta^k_j$$
  is
  $$\{F,G\}=\mathrm{Tr}\left(
      \frac{\partial F}{\partial\phi_1}\frac{\partial G}{\partial\phi_2}
      -\frac{\partial F}{\partial\phi_2}\frac{\partial G}{\partial\phi_1}
    \right),$$
  where the derivatives are cyclic matrix derivatives. Varying one occurrence
  at a time gives
  \begin{align*}
  \frac{\partial F_{k,l}}{\partial\phi_1}
    &=\sum_{a=0}^{k-1}\phi_1^{k-1-a}\phi_2^l\phi_1^a,\\
  \frac{\partial F_{k,l}}{\partial\phi_2}
    &=\sum_{b=0}^{l-1}\phi_2^{l-1-b}\phi_1^k\phi_2^b.
  \end{align*}
  Therefore the first summand in the bracket is a sum of $kn$ traces, each
  obtained by removing one $\phi_1$ from the first word and one $\phi_2$ from
  the second word. The second summand is a sum of $lm$ traces obtained by
  removing one $\phi_2$ from the first word and one $\phi_1$ from the second.
  Every trace appearing in either nonzero sum has multidegree
  $(k+m-1,l+n-1)$. Lemma~\ref{lem:adjacent-swaps} identifies all cyclic words
  with this multidegree in reduced BRST cohomology. The coefficient is
  therefore $kn-lm$, and the common class is
  $\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^{k+m-1}\phi_2^{l+n-1})}$. When the common word
  has multidegree $(0,0)$, this class is the scalar trace and is zero in the
  Hamiltonian quotient by constants.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[Open action on primitive descendants]\label{prop:open-descendant-action}
  Let
  $$\widetilde\Psi_{a,b}
    =\binom{a+b}{a}^{-1}\Psi_{a,b}$$
  be the averaged primitive one-$\psi$ class. In the stable primitive one-$\psi$
  homology,
  $$\{\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^p\phi_2^q)},\widetilde\Psi_{a,b}\}
    =(pb-qa)\,\widetilde\Psi_{a+p-1,b+q-1},$$
  with the convention that a term with a negative exponent is zero. Thus the
  unreduced polynomial descendant classes carry the adjoint polynomial
  Hamiltonian action. The Hamiltonian descendant sector is the quotient by the
  scalar line $\C\widetilde\Psi_{0,0}$, so the displayed action is projected
  further by killing the scalar target.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Put $x=\phi_1$ and $y=\phi_2$. The cyclic derivatives of
  $F_{p,q}=\mathrm{Tr}(x^p y^q)$ are
  $$\frac{\partial F_{p,q}}{\partial x}
    =\sum_{i=0}^{p-1}x^{p-1-i}y^qx^i,\qquad
    \frac{\partial F_{p,q}}{\partial y}
    =\sum_{j=0}^{q-1}y^{q-1-j}x^py^j.$$
  For a one-$\psi$ cyclic word $\mathrm{Tr}(\psi w)$ of bidegree $(a,b)$,
  differentiating with respect to $y$ gives one summand for each of the $b$
  occurrences of $y$ in $w$, and differentiating with respect to $x$ gives one
  summand for each of the $a$ occurrences of $x$. Multiplying by the displayed
  cyclic derivatives of $F_{p,q}$ therefore produces $pb$ positive one-$\psi$
  words and $qa$ negative one-$\psi$ words for each $w\in\mathsf W_{a,b}$, all
  of total bidegree $(a+p-1,b+q-1)$.

  The bracket is a cycle because $F_{p,q}$ is invariant, hence
  $\{F_{p,q},\mu_\epsilon\}=0$ for every moment-map component
  $\mu_\epsilon=-\mathrm{Tr}(\epsilon[x,y])$. Equivalently, the Hamiltonian
  vector field of $F_{p,q}$ preserves the Koszul differential
  $Q\psi=[x,y]$ and acts on Koszul homology. By
  Proposition~\ref{prop:one-psi-homology}, the target primitive one-$\psi$
  homology in that bidegree is one-dimensional. The functional
  $\epsilon(\mathrm{Tr}(\psi w))=1$ detects its coefficient. Since
  $\epsilon(\widetilde\Psi_{a,b})=1$, the averaged input gives
  $$\epsilon\bigl(\{F_{p,q},\widetilde\Psi_{a,b}\}\bigr)=pb-qa.$$
  Also $\epsilon(\widetilde\Psi_{a+p-1,b+q-1})=1$. Hence the displayed formula
  follows. If the target bidegree is $(0,0)$, the same formula is still the
  unreduced primitive one-$\psi$ action; the Hamiltonian descendant quotient
  sends that scalar class to zero.
\end{proof}

\begin{cor}[Descendant action differs from the coadjoint action]\label{cor:descendant-coadjoint-difference}
  Under the boundary pairing
  $\delta_{a,b}[1]\mapsto[\Psi_{a,b}]$, the open Poisson action on polynomial
  one-$\psi$ descendants differs from the closed coadjoint action on
  $\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$.
\end{cor}

\begin{proof}
  Proposition~\ref{prop:open-descendant-action} gives
  $$F_{p,q}:\widetilde\Psi_{a,b}\longmapsto
    (pb-qa)\widetilde\Psi_{a+p-1,b+q-1}.$$
  The closed coadjoint action is dual to the Poisson bracket. For
  $f=z_1^pz_2^q$ and the Taylor-dual basis vector $\delta_{a,b}$,
  $$(\operatorname{ad}^{\vee}_f\delta_{a,b})(z_1^mz_2^n)
    =-\delta_{a,b}\bigl((pn-qm)z_1^{p+m-1}z_2^{q+n-1}\bigr).$$
  Therefore, with the convention that negative indices give zero,
  $$\operatorname{ad}^{\vee}_{z_1^pz_2^q}\delta_{a,b}
    =-(pb-qa+p-q)\delta_{a-p+1,b-q+1}.$$
  For example, $z_1$ acts on open descendants by
  $$\widetilde\Psi_{a,b}\longmapsto b\,\widetilde\Psi_{a,b-1},$$
  whereas it acts coadjointly by
  $$\delta_{a,b}\longmapsto-(b+1)\delta_{a,b+1}.$$
  The scalar edge
  $\{\,\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1),\widetilde\Psi_{0,1}\,\}=\Psi_{0,0}$ is removed with
  the scalar descendant. The theorem uses
  Corollary~\ref{cor:cotangent-boundary-pairing} for the PBW special-fibre
  product identification. A descendant deformation match requires a different
  boundary realization of the continuous dual, such as a Laurent or
  distributional model, together with its own bracket calculation.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[Principal-part realization of the coadjoint module]\label{prop:principal-part-coadjoint}
  Let
  $$\mc P=\bigoplus_{\substack{a,b\geq0\\a+b>0}}
    \C\,\rho_{a,b},\qquad
    \rho_{a,b}=z_1^{-a-1}z_2^{-b-1}\,dz_1dz_2,$$
  with residue pairing
  $$\langle \rho_{a,b},z_1^mz_2^n\rangle
    =\operatorname{Res}_{z_1=z_2=0}
      z_1^{m-a-1}z_2^{n-b-1}\,dz_1dz_2
    =\delta_{a,m}\delta_{b,n}.$$
  Let $\pi_{\mathrm{pp}}$ denote projection of Laurent two-forms to the span of
  the principal parts $\rho_{a,b}$. The action
  $$f\cdot\rho=\pi_{\mathrm{pp}}\bigl(\{f,\rho\}\bigr)$$
  identifies $\mc P$ with the continuous coadjoint module
  $\mathfrak h^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}$. On monomials,
  $$z_1^pz_2^q\cdot\rho_{a,b}
    =-(pb-qa+p-q)\rho_{a-p+1,b-q+1},$$
  with the convention that a term with a negative index is zero.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  The residue pairing identifies $\rho_{a,b}$ with the Taylor-dual functional
  $\delta_{a,b}$. Hamiltonian vector fields preserve the holomorphic volume
  form $dz_1dz_2$, so integration by parts for the residue pairing gives
  $$\langle \{f,\rho\},g\rangle=-\langle\rho,\{f,g\}\rangle.$$
  Hence the Laurent principal-part action realizes the coadjoint action.
  Directly, for $f=z_1^pz_2^q$ and
  $\rho_{a,b}=z_1^{-a-1}z_2^{-b-1}dz_1dz_2$,
  \begin{align*}
  \{f,z_1^{-a-1}z_2^{-b-1}\}
    &=p z_1^{p-1}z_2^q\,(-(b+1))z_1^{-a-1}z_2^{-b-2}\\
    &\quad
      -q z_1^pz_2^{q-1}\,(-(a+1))z_1^{-a-2}z_2^{-b-1}\\
    &=-(pb-qa+p-q)z_1^{p-a-2}z_2^{q-b-2}.
  \end{align*}
  Since
  $z_1^{p-a-2}z_2^{q-b-2}\,dz_1dz_2
    =\rho_{a-p+1,b-q+1}$ when both indices are nonnegative, principal-part
  projection gives the displayed formula.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[No polynomial boundary realization of the principal parts]\label{prop:no-polynomial-principal-part-boundary}
  In the present polynomial open BV/Koszul matrix model, no family of ordinary
  polynomial boundary local cohomology classes
  $$\Theta_{a,b},\qquad a,b\geq0,\quad a+b>0,$$
  can realize the principal-part coadjoint module of
  Proposition~\ref{prop:principal-part-coadjoint}.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  The linear Hamiltonians
  $$O_{1,0}=\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1)},\qquad
    O_{0,1}=\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_2)}$$
  act on polynomial boundary observables by the constant Hamiltonian vector
  fields $\partial_{\phi_2}$ and $-\partial_{\phi_1}$, respectively. On every
  ordinary polynomial observable these operators are locally nilpotent: after
  enough iterations the polynomial degree in the differentiated variable is
  exhausted.

  On the principal-part coadjoint module, however,
  Proposition~\ref{prop:principal-part-coadjoint} gives
  $$O_{1,0}\cdot\rho_{a,b}=-(b+1)\rho_{a,b+1},\qquad
    O_{0,1}\cdot\rho_{a,b}=(a+1)\rho_{a+1,b}.$$
  Neither operator is locally nilpotent on any nonzero principal-part
  generator. Local nilpotence is preserved under isomorphism of modules.
  Therefore the desired $\Theta_{a,b}$ cannot be ordinary polynomial boundary
  observables in the current open BV/Koszul model.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[Reduced principal-part boundary current object]\label{prop:reduced-principal-part-boundary-current}
  After the de Rham contraction to the reduced local model on the brane line,

  Construction~\ref{constr:boundary-local-dual-principal-parts} extends to a

  compactly supported densities give the smooth smearing formulas below. Put
  also
  $$\mathfrak J_{\partial}^{\mathrm{Ham}}(I)
    =C_c^\infty(I)\widehat\otimes\bar A$$
  and define extension by zero for inclusions of intervals. Let
  $$\mathrm{Obs}_{\partial,N}^{\mathrm{cl,pp}}(I)

      \widehat\otimes
      \widehat{\mathbf S}\bigl(\mathfrak J_{\partial}^{\mathrm{pp}}(I)\bigr).$$
  The differential is $Q_{\mathrm{open}}$ on the ordinary open factor and zero
  on $\mathfrak J_{\partial}^{\mathrm{pp}}(I)$. The product for disjoint
  intervals is the graded-commutative product after extension by zero.

  For $a,b\in C_c^\infty(I)$, define the Hamiltonian smearings
  $$B_f(a)=\int_I a(t)B_f(t)\,dt,\qquad
    O_f(a)=a\otimes f.$$
  For $B\in\mathcal D'_c(I)$, write
  $$\Theta_\rho(B)=B\otimes\rho[1].$$
  Pair $\Theta_\rho(B)$ only with the primitive connected Hamiltonian trace
  projection by
  $$\langle\Theta_\rho(B),B_g(b)\rangle
    =B(b)\rho(g).$$
  Descendants, constants, and decomposable multi-traces are outside this
  pairing unless an additional coalgebra projection is specified. The
  $P_0$ bracket on the reduced current object is
  $$\{B_f(a),B_g(b)\}=B_{\{f,g\}}(ab),\qquad
    \{B_f(a),\Theta_\rho(B)\}=\Theta_{f\cdot\rho}(aB),\qquad
    \{\Theta_\rho(B),\Theta_\sigma(C)\}=0,$$

  regular densities $B=b(t)\,dt$ and on the basis $\Theta_{i,j}$,
  $$\{O_{p,q}(a),\Theta_{i,j}(b)\}
    =-(pj-qi+p-q)\Theta_{i-p+1,j-q+1}(ab),$$
  again with negative-index targets zero.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Extension by zero makes both $I\mapsto C_c^\infty(I)$ and
  $I\mapsto\mathcal D'_c(I)$ cosheaves on the line. Multiplication of a compactly
  supported distribution by a smooth compactly supported function is compatible
  with extension by zero. Tensoring with the fixed discrete principal-part
  module $\mc P[1]$ preserves this reduced compact-support functoriality. The
  factorization product on
  disjoint intervals is ordinary multiplication after pushing both compactly
  supported sections into the union; the odd generators
  $\Theta_\rho$ therefore generate the completed graded-symmetric, equivalently
  exterior, principal-part factor.

  The differential is compatible with these structure maps because
  $Q_{\mathrm{open}}B_f=0$ on the Hamiltonian trace classes and
  $Q\Theta_\rho=0$ by definition in the added local-dual factor. The first
  bracket identity is Theorem~\ref{thm:bulk-boundary} with test functions
  multiplied on the line. The second is the negative transpose of that bracket
  under the displayed primitive trace pairing, and reduces exactly to the
  principal-part coadjoint formula of
  Proposition~\ref{prop:principal-part-coadjoint}. The third says that the
  shifted cotangent local-dual ideal is abelian. Jacobi is then the Jacobi
  identity in the semidirect Lie algebra
  $\bar A\ltimes\mc P[1]$: equivalently,
  $[\operatorname{ad}_f^\vee,\operatorname{ad}_g^\vee]
    =\operatorname{ad}_{\{f,g\}}^\vee$ on the continuous dual.
\end{proof}

\begin{thm}[Classical bulk-to-boundary Hamiltonian map]\label{thm:bulk-boundary}
  In the stable connected Hamiltonian trace sector of the closed-open mixed
  holomorphic-topological theory, the boundary evaluation maps
  $\partial_{\mathrm{bb},N}$ of Construction~\ref{constr:boundary-evaluation}
  stabilize to a linear map
  $$\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}:\bar A\longrightarrow
    H^0_{\mathrm{Ham}}(\mc O_{\mathrm{brane}}^{\mathrm{cl}}),\qquad
    f\longmapsto \overline{\mathrm{Tr}\,f(\phi_1,\phi_2)}.$$
  This map is a morphism of Lie algebras from the closed Hamiltonian Poisson
  algebra $\bar A$ to the connected open Hamiltonian trace Poisson algebra:
  $$\{\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}(f),\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}(g)\}_{\mathrm{open}}
    =\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}\bigl(\{f,g\}_{\bar A}\bigr).$$
  This is the checked degree-zero Hamiltonian bracket component expected from
  the closed-open map to the Poisson factorization center of the brane
  observables. The full Poisson factorization-center morphism is not constructed
  in this theorem.
\end{thm}

\begin{proof}
  The map is well-defined on $\bar A$ by
  Corollary~\ref{cor:matrix-evaluation-well-defined}; constants map to the
  scalar $N$ and vanish in the Hamiltonian quotient by constants. Lemma
  \ref{lem:trace-bracket-descends} gives the open Hamiltonian trace Poisson
  bracket. For
  $f=z_1^kz_2^l$ and $g=z_1^mz_2^n$, Proposition~\ref{prop:first-order-bracket}
  gives
  $$\{\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}(f),\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}(g)\}_{\mathrm{open}}
    =\overline{(kn-lm)\mathrm{Tr}
      (\phi_1^{k+m-1}\phi_2^{l+n-1})}.$$
  The closed Poisson bracket is
  $$\{f,g\}_{\bar A}=\overline{(kn-lm)z_1^{k+m-1}z_2^{l+n-1}},$$
  and applying $\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}$ gives the same expression. Bilinearity
  proves the identity for all polynomials.
\end{proof}

\begin{cor}[Classical local bulk-boundary coupling]\label{cor:local-bulk-boundary-coupling}

\begin{proof}

  Theorem~\ref{thm:bulk-boundary}. Compact support removes boundary terms.
\end{proof}

\subsubsection{Formal deformation-quantization target}

The degree-zero Hamiltonian sector has a canonical formal
deformation-quantization target. The formal holomorphic disk has constant
Poisson tensor, so its canonical translation-invariant deformation is the
Weyl/Moyal algebra. This fixes the algebraic coefficients one would have to
recover from a quantum closed-open BV construction. It is not, by itself, a
construction of that quantum BV theory. The degree-zero Hamiltonian bracket
part of the classical theorem is the proposed special fibre of this formal
deformation; the cotangent/descendant part remains the separate problem stated
below.
Put
$$A_\hbar=\bigl(\C[[z_1,z_2]][[\hbar]],*\bigr),$$
where $*$ is the Weyl/Moyal product of the next subsection. The symbol
$\bar A_\hbar$ denotes the central quotient of the underlying Moyal Lie algebra
by $\C[[\hbar]]$; it is not used as an associative quotient. We write
$$[f,g]_{\mathrm{raw}}=f*g-g*f,\qquad
  \{f,g\}_\hbar=\hbar^{-1}[f,g]_{\mathrm{raw}}.$$
The degree-zero quantum Hamiltonian Lie algebra is
$$\mathfrak h_\hbar=\bar A_\hbar,\qquad
  [f,g]_{\mathfrak h_\hbar}=\{f,g\}_\hbar.$$
The specialization at $\hbar=0$ is the Poisson Lie algebra
$\mathfrak h=\C[[z_1,z_2]]/\C\cdot1$ used above.

On the brane side, the open action
$$\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\,d\phi_2)$$
makes the matrix entries of $\phi_1$ and $\phi_2$ conjugate variables.
The canonical algebraic quantization of the unconstrained first-order matrix
phase space is the completed Weyl algebra
$\mathcal W_N$ generated by those entries with
$$[(\phi_1)^i{}_j,(\phi_2)^k{}_l]
  =\hbar\,\delta^i_l\delta^k_j,\qquad
  [(\phi_a)^i{}_j,(\phi_a)^k{}_l]=0\quad(a=1,2).$$
The gauge field imposes the conjugation moment-map constraint. With the
commutator convention above, the normal-ordered quantum comoment is
$$\widehat\mu_\epsilon=-\mathrm{Tr}\bigl([\epsilon,\phi_1]\phi_2\bigr),
  \qquad \epsilon\in\lie{gl}_N.$$
This formula is not cyclically reordered: for $\epsilon=1$ it vanishes, as the
scalar subgroup acts trivially. We use the convention
$$\hbar^{-1}[\widehat\mu_\epsilon,a]=\epsilon\cdot a$$
for the induced infinitesimal conjugation action on the Weyl algebra; changing
to the opposite commutator convention changes the displayed comoment by the
corresponding sign. The quantum brane local algebra is the BRST
quantum Hamiltonian reduction
$$\mc O^{\mathrm q}_{\mathrm{brane},N}
  =C^\bullet_{\mathrm{BRST}}
    \bigl(\lie{gl}_N,\mathcal W_N,\widehat\mu\bigr),$$
whose degree-zero cohomology is the normalizer quotient
$$H^0\mc O^{\mathrm q}_{\mathrm{brane},N}
  =\mathrm N_{\mathcal W_N}
    \bigl(\mathcal W_N\widehat\mu(\lie{gl}_N)\bigr)/
    \mathcal W_N\widehat\mu(\lie{gl}_N).$$
The constant Hamiltonian trace is projected out in degree zero as before. The
classical special fibre is the reduced BV algebra of
Construction~\ref{constr:reduced-bv-algebra}.

The quantum boundary map is Weyl-ordered trace evaluation:
$$\partial_{\mathrm{bb},\hbar,N}(f)
  =\overline{\mathrm{Tr}\,\operatorname{Op}_{\mathrm W}(f)(\phi_1,\phi_2)},
  \qquad f\in\bar A_\hbar,$$
where $\operatorname{Op}_{\mathrm W}$ denotes total symmetrisation before
passing to the trace. At $\hbar=0$ this recovers
Construction~\ref{constr:boundary-evaluation}. After dividing the commutator by
$\hbar$ and reducing modulo $\hbar$, the raw commutator identity recovers
Theorem~\ref{thm:bulk-boundary}.

\begin{prop}[BRST descent of Weyl-ordered Hamiltonian traces]\label{prop:quantum-boundary-descends}
  For every $f\in\bar A_\hbar$, the Weyl-ordered trace
  $$F_{f,N}=\mathrm{Tr}\,\operatorname{Op}_{\mathrm W}(f)(\phi_1,\phi_2)$$
  is invariant under the quantum comoment:
  $$[\widehat\mu_\epsilon,F_{f,N}]=0,\qquad \epsilon\in\lie{gl}_N.$$
  Hence $F_{f,N}$ lies in the normalizer of the quantum moment-map ideal and
  defines a degree-zero class in
  $H^0\mc O^{\mathrm q}_{\mathrm{brane},N}$ after removing the scalar
  Hamiltonian.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Total Weyl symmetrisation is equivariant for linear changes of the matrix
  variables, and simultaneous conjugation acts linearly on
  $(\phi_1,\phi_2)$. Therefore
  $\mathrm{Tr}\,\operatorname{Op}_{\mathrm W}(f)(\phi_1,\phi_2)$ is invariant
  under infinitesimal conjugation. By the defining property of the quantum
  comoment,
  $$\hbar^{-1}[\widehat\mu_\epsilon,a]=\epsilon\cdot a,$$
  this invariance is exactly $[\widehat\mu_\epsilon,F_{f,N}]=0$. If
  $I=\mathcal W_N\widehat\mu(\lie{gl}_N)$ is the left quantum moment-map ideal,
  then for every generator $\widehat\mu_\epsilon$,
  $\widehat\mu_\epsilon F_{f,N}=F_{f,N}\widehat\mu_\epsilon\in I$. Thus
  $F_{f,N}$ normalizes $I$ and descends to the normalizer quotient describing
  $H^0\mc O^{\mathrm q}_{\mathrm{brane},N}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{rmk}[Normal-ordering obstruction]\label{rmk:normal-ordering-obstruction}
  Proposition~\ref{prop:quantum-boundary-descends} is not the quantum Moyal
  theorem. Put $X=\phi_1$ and $Y=\phi_2$. With the normal ordering above,
  $$\widehat\mu_\epsilon=-\mathrm{Tr}([\epsilon,X]Y)
    =\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\epsilon(YX-XY+\hbar N I)\bigr).$$
  For $\epsilon=I$ this comoment is identically zero; equivalently the
  displayed matrix has trace zero inside the Weyl algebra. Thus the nontrivial
  quantum moment equations are represented by the traceless matrix relation
  $$YX-XY+\hbar N I=0,$$
  not by the classical commuting-pair equation. Consequently, for a covariant
  matrix word $A$,
  $$\mathrm{Tr}(A\,XY)-\mathrm{Tr}(A\,YX)
    =\hbar N\,\mathrm{Tr}(A)
    \quad \bmod\ \mathcal W_N\widehat\mu(\lie{gl}_N).$$
  This Capelli-type shift can feed lower-degree traces into the connected
  sector. A stable quantum theorem must prove that the chosen connected
  extraction removes or renormalizes this shift, or else change the sector or
  normalization.
\end{rmk}

\begin{rmk}[Capelli-renormalized trace generators]\label{rmk:capelli-renormalized-traces}
  Let
  $$T_{a,b}=\mathrm{Tr}(X^aY^b)$$
  denote the normal-ordered trace. The Weyl-ordered trace
  $$J_N(z_1^az_2^b)=\mathrm{Tr}\,\operatorname{Op}_{\mathrm W}(z_1^az_2^b)(X,Y)$$
  is triangular over the normal-ordered traces after imposing the quantum
  moment relation:
  $$J_N(z_1^az_2^b)\equiv
    \sum_{r=0}^{\min(a,b)}
      \left(-\frac{\hbar N}{2}\right)^r
      r!\binom ar\binom br\,T_{a-r,b-r}.$$
  For example,
  $$J_N(z_1z_2)=T_{1,1}-\frac{\hbar N^2}{2},\qquad
    J_N(z_1^2z_2)=T_{2,1}-\hbar N\,T_{1,0},$$
  and
  $$J_N(z_1z_2^2)=T_{1,2}-\hbar N\,T_{0,1},\qquad
    J_N(z_1^2z_2^2)=T_{2,2}-2\hbar N\,T_{1,1}
      +\frac{\hbar^2N^3}{2}.$$
  The naive normal-ordered basis therefore has the wrong commutator even in low
  degree; a direct calculation gives
  $$\hbar^{-1}[T_{2,1},T_{0,2}]
    =4T_{1,2}-2\hbar N\,T_{0,1}
    \quad\bmod\ \mathcal W_N\widehat\mu(\lie{gl}_N),$$
  whereas the scalar Moyal bracket would give only $4T_{1,2}$. Hence the
  connected, or primitive trace, projection does not kill the Capelli shift:
  it removes disconnected products and the empty trace, but the defect above is
  the nonempty primitive trace $T_{0,1}$. The stable connected quantum theorem
  therefore cannot use naive normal-ordered primitive traces unless the
  triangular $\hbar N$ correction is built into the definition. The natural
  finite-$N$ generators are the Capelli-renormalized Weyl traces $J_N(f)$,
  equivalently
  $$J_N(f)=T_N\left(
    \exp\left(-\frac{\hbar N}{2}\partial_{z_1}\partial_{z_2}\right)f
  \right)$$
  in the normal-ordered trace notation $T_N(z_1^az_2^b)=T_{a,b}$.
\end{rmk}

\begin{thm}[Finite-$N$ radial-parts Moyal identity]\label{thm:finite-n-reduced-moyal}
  Let $\mathcal W_N$ be the Weyl algebra of
  $T^*\operatorname{Mat}_N$ and let $\widehat\mu$ be the normal-ordered
  conjugation comoment above. For
  $f\in\C[z_1,z_2][[\hbar]]$, put
  $$J_N(f)=\mathrm{Tr}\,\operatorname{Op}_{\mathrm W}(f)(X,Y).$$
  Let $\mathfrak t\subset\lie{gl}_N$ be the diagonal Cartan, with coordinates
  $\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_N$, and let
  $$\Delta=\prod_{i<j}(\lambda_i-\lambda_j).$$
  Put
  $$S_N(f)=\sum_{i=1}^N
      \operatorname{Op}_{\mathrm W}
        (f)(\lambda_i,-\hbar\partial_{\lambda_i}).$$
  Then the Harish-Chandra radial-part map, in the convention conjugated by the
  Vandermonde, satisfies
  $$\operatorname{Rad}_0(J_N(f))=\Delta^{-1}S_N(f)\Delta.$$
  Consequently, for all $f,g$ the defect
  $$D_N(f,g)=
    \hbar^{-1}[J_N(f),J_N(g)]-J_N(\{f,g\}_\hbar)$$
  lies in the quantum moment-map ideal and therefore vanishes in the degree-zero
  quantum Hamiltonian reduction
  $$\mathrm N_{\mathcal W_N}
    \bigl(\mathcal W_N\widehat\mu(\lie{gl}_N)\bigr)/
    \mathcal W_N\widehat\mu(\lie{gl}_N).$$
\end{thm}

\begin{proof}
  Identify $\mathcal W_N$ with
  $\mathcal D_\hbar(\lie{gl}_N)$, where $X$ is multiplication and
  $Y=-\hbar\partial_X^t$. The quantum Hamiltonian reduction for conjugation is
  the normalizer quotient by the left ideal generated by the traceless
  comoment. The scalar matrix has zero comoment, as in
  Remark~\ref{rmk:normal-ordering-obstruction}; equivalently the reduction is
  the $\lie{sl}_N$ reduction together with $GL_N$-invariants. Since $GL_N$ is
  reductive in characteristic zero, invariants are exact.

  Harish-Chandra's radial-part construction, in the form refined by Wallach
  and by Levasseur--Stafford
  \cites{harish-chandra-invariant-differential-operators,wallach-reductive-invariant-differential,levasseur-stafford-harish-chandra,levasseur-stafford-kernel-harish-chandra},
  gives an injective Harish-Chandra map from this quantum Hamiltonian reduction
  to the Weyl-invariant differential operators on the regular Cartan, after
  conjugation by $\Delta$. It is therefore enough to compute the radial part on
  the generators $J_N(f)$.

  Let $\mathcal S$ be the set of $f\in\C[z_1,z_2][[\hbar]]$ for which
  $\operatorname{Rad}_0(J_N(f))=\Delta^{-1}S_N(f)\Delta$. Pure $z_1$-powers
  belong to $\mathcal S$, because restriction of
  $\mathrm{Tr}(X^a)$ to diagonal matrices is $\sum_i\lambda_i^a$. Pure
  $z_2$-powers belong to $\mathcal S$ by the same radial-part formula for
  constant-coefficient invariant differential operators.

  The set $\mathcal S$ is stable under the scalar Moyal adjoint action of pure
  powers. Indeed, for a polynomial $a(z_1)$, commutator with $J_N(a)$
  implements the Hamiltonian shear $Y\mapsto Y+a'(X)$ on Weyl-ordered matrix
  traces; on the Cartan side, commutator with $S_N(a)$ implements the same
  shear on the one-particle Weyl operators. The proof for a polynomial
  $b(z_2)$ is the dual shear. Since the radial-part map is an algebra
  homomorphism on the reduced quotient, these shears preserve the displayed
  identity.

  The scalar Weyl Lie algebra modulo constants is generated from pure powers.
  For $a,b>0$,
  $$\{z_1^{a+1},z_2^{b+1}\}_\hbar
    =(a+1)(b+1)z_1^az_2^b
      +\text{terms of lower total degree}.$$
  Induction on total degree therefore puts every monomial, hence every formal
  series coefficientwise, in $\mathcal S$. This proves the radial formula for
  all $f$.

  Finally,
  $$\hbar^{-1}[S_N(f),S_N(g)]=S_N(\{f,g\}_\hbar),$$
  because the $i$th summand is the ordinary one-particle Weyl algebra and
  distinct summands commute. Conjugation by $\Delta$ preserves commutators, so
  $\operatorname{Rad}_0(D_N(f,g))=0$. Injectivity of the Harish-Chandra
  reduction map gives
  $$D_N(f,g)\in
    \bigl(\mathcal W_N\widehat\mu(\lie{sl}_N)\bigr)^{GL_N},$$
  hence $D_N(f,g)$ is zero in the degree-zero quantum Hamiltonian reduction.
\end{proof}

\subsubsection{Imported graph package and specialization problem}

The graph problem is the analytic form of the same statement. The algebra says
which coefficients must appear; a closed-open BV theory must produce those
coefficients as renormalized observable products, with propagator, counterterm,
anomaly, and trace-normalization choices fixed. Costello's formalism supplies
the general construction. The present paper must still identify the Hamiltonian
input to that construction.

\begin{stmt}[Imported Costello perturbative BV package]\label{stmt:costello-bv-package}
  For an elliptic BV theory with a gauge fixing, heat kernel $K_t$, propagator
  $$P_{\epsilon,L}=\int_{\epsilon}^{L}Q^{\mathrm{GF}}K_t\,dt,$$
  and a renormalization scheme, Costello constructs finite-scale effective
  interactions by the RG operator
  $$W(P_{\epsilon,L},I[\epsilon])
    =\hbar\log\!\left(\exp(\hbar\partial_{P_{\epsilon,L}})
       \exp(I[\epsilon]/\hbar)\right).$$
  The renormalized interaction is obtained by adding local counterterms and
  taking the $\epsilon\to0$ limit. It has the connected-graph expansion
  $$I[L]=\sum_{\Gamma\ \mathrm{connected}}
    \frac{\hbar^{b_1(\Gamma)+\sum_v g_v}}{|\operatorname{Aut}\Gamma|}
    W_\Gamma(P_{\epsilon,L};I_{g_v}[\epsilon]+I^{\mathrm{CT}}_{g_v}(\epsilon)),$$
  satisfies RG flow and locality, and satisfies the quantum master equation
  when the obstruction classes vanish \cite{costello-renormalization}. The
  resulting quantum observables form a factorization algebra
  \cites{costello-gwilliam,costello-gwilliam-vol2}.
\end{stmt}

\begin{stmt}[Imported Costello--Li flat open-closed BCOV theorem]\label{stmt:costello-li-flat-bcov}
  Costello--Li prove a unique perturbative quantization of open-closed BCOV
  theory on flat $\C^d$, for $d$ odd, with open gauge algebra
  $\mathfrak{gl}(N\mid N)$ and compatibility under
  $\mathfrak{gl}(N\mid N)\hookrightarrow\mathfrak{gl}(N+k\mid N+k)$. In that
  flat open-closed BCOV/HCS theory they compute the anomaly cancellation needed
  for the graph construction \cite{costello-li-open-closed-bcov}. Ordinary
  $\mathfrak{gl}_N$ and the mixed $\R^2\times\C^2$ Hamiltonian brane model are
  not covered by this theorem without an additional reduction or summand
  argument.
\end{stmt}

\begin{rmk}
  The two imported statements supply the universal renormalized BV framework and
  one major flat BCOV/HCS instance, subject to their hypotheses. They do not by
  themselves prove the graph realization for the Hamiltonian sector of this
  paper. Here the brane
  $\R\times p$ is a defect in the mixed space, not a spacetime boundary unless a
  half-space model is separately chosen.
\end{rmk}

\begin{lem}[Finite-window obstruction for the full Hamiltonian sector]\label{lem:finite-window-hamiltonian-obstruction}
  The polynomial Hamiltonian Lie algebra of the formal symplectic plane admits
  no naive finite-dimensional Lie truncation compatible with the full sector
  used in this paper.
  More precisely:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item finite degree windows are not Lie subalgebras;
    \item projected degree-window brackets need not satisfy Jacobi;
    \item $\mathfrak m$-adic jet quotients are not Lie quotients in the
      presence of linear Hamiltonians;
    \item there is no directed exhaustion by finite-dimensional Lie subalgebras
      containing the full polynomial Hamiltonian algebra.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  Let $H_d$ be the span of homogeneous polynomials of degree $d$, and let
  $\mathfrak m=(z_1,z_2)$. The Poisson bracket satisfies
  $\{H_p,H_q\}\subset H_{p+q-2}$. Thus
  $$\{z_1^N,z_2^N\}=N^2z_1^{N-1}z_2^{N-1},$$
  which has degree $2N-2>N$ for $N>2$, so degree $\leq N$ windows are not Lie
  subalgebras.

  If one instead projects the bracket back to a degree window, Jacobi can fail.
  For the bracket $\pi_{\leq3}\{-,-\}$, take
  $$f=z_2,\qquad g=z_2^3,\qquad h=z_1^2z_2.$$
  A direct calculation gives the projected Jacobiator
  $$[[f,g]_3,h]_3+[[g,h]_3,f]_3+[[h,f]_3,g]_3=6z_2^3.$$

  The $\mathfrak m$-adic quotients are not stable under translations generated
  by linear Hamiltonians:
  $$\{z_1^{N+1},z_2\}=(N+1)z_1^N\notin\mathfrak m^{N+1}.$$
  Hence $\mathfrak h/\mathfrak m^{N+1}$ is not a Lie quotient for the full
  Hamiltonian algebra.

  Finally, any finite-dimensional Lie subalgebra containing
  $$a=z_1^2z_2^2,\qquad b=z_1^3z_2$$
  contains all iterated brackets
  $$(\operatorname{ad}_a)^n(b)=(-4)^n z_1^{n+3}z_2^{n+1},\qquad n\geq0,$$
  an infinite linearly independent set. Thus no directed finite-dimensional
  Lie-subalgebra exhaustion can contain all polynomial Hamiltonians.
\end{proof}

\begin{rmk}[Linear heat operator versus BV kernel]\label{rmk:linear-heat-versus-bv-kernel}
  On the linear field complex
  $$\Omega^\bullet(\R^2)\widehat\otimes
    \Omega^{0,\bullet}(\C^2)\widehat\otimes
    \bigl(\widehat{\mathfrak h}[1]\oplus
      \widehat{\mathfrak h}^{\vee}_{\mathrm{cont}}[2]\bigr),$$
  the flat gauge fixing
  $$Q^{\mathrm{GF}}=d_{\R^2}^*+\bar\partial_{\C^2}^*,\qquad
    \Delta=[d_{\R^2}+\bar\partial_{\C^2},Q^{\mathrm{GF}}]$$
  gives finite-scale heat operators
  $$K_t=K_t^{\mathrm{base}}\widehat\otimes\mathrm{id},\qquad
    P_{\epsilon,L}^{\mathrm{op}}
      =\int_\epsilon^L Q^{\mathrm{GF}}K_t^{\mathrm{base}}\,dt
        \widehat\otimes\mathrm{id}$$
  satisfying
  $$[d_{\R^2}+\bar\partial_{\C^2},P_{\epsilon,L}^{\mathrm{op}}]
    =K_\epsilon-K_L$$
  as continuous operators on the completed coefficient spaces. This is only an
  operator-level homotopy. To obtain Costello's BV propagator one also needs a
  coefficient coevaluation kernel for the Tate pair
  $\widehat{\mathfrak h}\oplus\widehat{\mathfrak h}^{\vee}_{\mathrm{cont}}$.
  In the product/direct-sum topology used here, the formal sum of homogeneous
  coefficient coevaluations is not a convergent element of the completed tensor
  product. Thus the analytic graph problem requires a genuine Tate or weighted
  coefficient extension of the heat-kernel calculus, or a declared restriction
  to a smaller sector.
\end{rmk}

\begin{lem}[Tate Casimir obstruction for the full Hamiltonian sector]\label{lem:tate-casimir-obstruction}
  In the product/direct-sum Tate topology
  $$\mathfrak h=\prod_{d\geq1}H_d,\qquad
    \mathfrak h^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}=\bigoplus_{d\geq1}H_d^\vee,$$
  the coefficient coevaluation
  $$C_{\mathfrak h}=\sum_{d\geq1}\sum_i e_{d,i}\otimes e^i_{d}$$
  is not an element of
  $\mathfrak h\widehat\otimes\mathfrak h^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}$, nor of
  $\mathfrak h^\vee_{\mathrm{cont}}\widehat\otimes\mathfrak h$. Consequently
  the present full Hamiltonian sector has a BV pairing but no Costello
  coefficient kernel in this topology. The operator-level homotopy of
  Remark~\ref{rmk:linear-heat-versus-bv-kernel} therefore does not define the
  BV propagator, the BV Laplacian, or renormalized graph weights for the full
  sector.
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  For each $d$, choose a basis $\{e_{d,i}\}_i$ of $H_d$ and the dual basis
  $\{e^i_d\}_i$ of $H_d^\vee$. An element of a completed tensor product with
  the direct-sum factor $\bigoplus_d H_d^\vee$ has finite support in that
  direct-sum direction in the present Tate convention. The displayed formal
  Casimir has a nonzero component in every $H_d^\vee$. Hence it is not a
  convergent tensor. The same support obstruction holds with the tensor factors
  reversed. Costello's heat-kernel BV formalism requires the inverse pairing as
  an actual distributional kernel, not merely an abstract continuous operator on
  the field complex.
\end{proof}

\subsubsection{Formal Weyl/Moyal target, all orders}
We use the Weyl/Moyal Rees convention for the formal quantization of the
constant Poisson tensor \cite{kontsevich-dq}:
$$f*g=m\circ\exp\left(\frac{\hbar}{2}
  (\partial_{z_1}\otimes\partial_{z_2}
   -\partial_{z_2}\otimes\partial_{z_1})\right)(f\otimes g).$$
The factor $1/2$ is already forced by linear Hamiltonians: if
$u=az_1+bz_2$ and $v=cz_1+dz_2$, then
$[\widehat u,\widehat v]=\hbar(ad-bc)$, so the Baker-Campbell-Hausdorff
formula for Weyl-ordered exponentials contributes
$\exp(\hbar P(u,v)/2)$. Differentiating these exponentials gives the
cross-contraction $P/2$ in the formal Weyl convention.
Then the star commutator is odd in $\hbar$:
$$[f,g]_*=\hbar\{f,g\}+\frac{\hbar^3}{24}P^3(f,g)+O(\hbar^5),$$
where $P=\partial_{z_1}\otimes\partial_{z_2}
-\partial_{z_2}\otimes\partial_{z_1}$. Thus the $\hbar^2$ coefficient vanishes
in this quantization convention. The normalized quantum Hamiltonian bracket is
therefore
$$\{f,g\}_\hbar=\hbar^{-1}[f,g]_*
  =\sum_{s\geq0}
    \frac{\hbar^{2s}}{2^{2s}(2s+1)!}\,P^{2s+1}(f,g).$$

\begin{prop}[Moyal coefficients on monomials]\label{prop:moyal-monomial}
  Let $f=z_1^kz_2^l$ and $g=z_1^mz_2^n$. For every $r\geq0$,
  $$P^r(f,g)=
    \sum_{a=0}^r(-1)^a\binom{r}{a}
    (k)_{r-a}(l)_a(m)_a(n)_{r-a}
    z_1^{k+m-r}z_2^{l+n-r},$$
  where $(s)_q=s(s-1)\cdots(s-q+1)$ and $(s)_0=1$. Terms with a negative
  displayed exponent are understood to be zero; equivalently the corresponding
  falling factorial already vanishes. Consequently
  $$[f,g]_*=\sum_{\substack{r\geq1\\ r\ \mathrm{odd}}}
    \frac{2}{2^r r!}\,\hbar^r P^r(f,g),$$
  so the second-order coefficient vanishes. The first four odd coefficients are
  $$\hbar P(f,g),\qquad
    \frac{\hbar^3}{24}P^3(f,g),\qquad
    \frac{\hbar^5}{1920}P^5(f,g),\qquad
    \frac{\hbar^7}{322560}P^7(f,g).$$
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Since
  $$P=\partial_{z_1}\otimes\partial_{z_2}
    -\partial_{z_2}\otimes\partial_{z_1},$$
  the two summands commute as differential operators on the tensor product.
  The binomial theorem gives
  $$P^r=\sum_{a=0}^r(-1)^a\binom{r}{a}
    \partial_{z_1}^{r-a}\partial_{z_2}^{a}
    \otimes
    \partial_{z_1}^{a}\partial_{z_2}^{r-a}.$$
  Applying this to $z_1^kz_2^l\otimes z_1^mz_2^n$ gives the displayed falling
  factorial formula. Interchanging $f$ and $g$ changes $P^r(f,g)$ by the sign
  $(-1)^r$, so the star commutator keeps exactly odd $r$ and contributes the
  factor $2/(2^r r!)$ in order $r$.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[Conditional all-order boundary-product normalization]\label{prop:conditional-boundary-product-normalization}
  Suppose a Hamiltonian specialization of the Costello closed-open observable
  product has the following degree-zero boundary restriction: for Weyl-ordered
  Hamiltonian boundary observables, the product is
  $$m\circ\exp\left(\frac{\hbar}{2}P_{\partial}\right),\qquad
    P_{\partial}=P,$$
  and connected single-trace extraction introduces no additional factor of
  $N$, sign, or scalar moment-map term. Then the antisymmetrised boundary
  product has all odd raw commutator coefficients
  $$\frac{2}{2^r r!}\hbar^rP^r(f,g),\qquad r\geq1\text{ odd}.$$
  In particular, the first and third coefficients are
  $$\hbar P(f,g),\qquad \frac{\hbar^3}{24}P^3(f,g).$$
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  This is Proposition~\ref{prop:moyal-monomial} applied to the assumed boundary
  product. The exponential contributes $1/r!$, Weyl normalization contributes
  $2^{-r}$, and antisymmetrisation keeps exactly the odd powers of $P$ and adds
  the factor $2$. Thus the coefficient in odd order $r$ is $2/(2^r r!)$. For
  $r=1$ this gives $1$, and for $r=3$ it gives $2/(2^3 3!)=1/24$.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[Open-line midpoint graph weights]\label{prop:open-line-midpoint-graph-weights}
  In the reduced open first-order line model
  $$S_0=\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\,d\phi_2),$$
  choose the zero-mode-removed midpoint propagator
  $$G(t,s)=\frac12\operatorname{sgn}(t-s),\qquad
    \langle\phi_1(t)\phi_2(s)\rangle=\hbar G(t,s),\quad
    \langle\phi_2(t)\phi_1(s)\rangle=-\hbar G(t,s).$$
  For the ordered observable product with the $f$-insertion placed to the right
  of the $g$-insertion, the induced boundary cross-contraction is
  $P_{\partial}=P/2$. Hence the one-edge ordered graph has weight
  $\hbar P(f,g)/2$, and the three-parallel-edge ordered graph has weight
  $\hbar^3P^3(f,g)/48$. After subtracting the reverse ordered product, the
  commutator contributions are
  $$\hbar P(f,g),\qquad \frac{\hbar^3}{24}P^3(f,g).$$
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  The midpoint value $G(+,-)=1/2$ assigns one copy of
  $$P=\partial_{z_1}\otimes\partial_{z_2}
    -\partial_{z_2}\otimes\partial_{z_1}$$
  to each cross-contraction, with the Weyl factor $1/2$. For one edge there is
  no nontrivial edge automorphism, so the ordered product contributes
  $\hbar P(f,g)/2$. The reverse ordered product contributes
  $\hbar P(g,f)/2=-\hbar P(f,g)/2$, and the commutator gives $\hbar P(f,g)$.

  For three parallel edges between the same two labelled observable vertices,
  the automorphism group permutes the three identical edges and has order
  $3!$. The ordered product contribution is therefore
  $$\hbar^3\frac1{3!}\left(\frac12\right)^3P^3(f,g)
    =\frac{\hbar^3}{48}P^3(f,g).$$
  Since $P^3(g,f)=-P^3(f,g)$, subtracting the reverse ordered product doubles
  this coefficient and gives $\hbar^3P^3(f,g)/24$.
\end{proof}

\begin{rmk}
  Proposition~\ref{prop:open-line-midpoint-graph-weights} is a computation in
  the reduced open-line Weyl model. It is not the Costello closed-open graph
  theorem. The latter still requires an elliptic parent or dimensional
  reduction, a brane-defect condition, finite-scale gauge fixing, the induced
  bulk-to-defect propagator, orientations and measures on configuration spaces,
  counterterms, anomaly cancellation, and compatibility with the quantum
  Hamiltonian reduction.
\end{rmk}

\subsubsection{Third-order target for Costello's graph problem}
For monomials $f=z_1^kz_2^l$ and $g=z_1^mz_2^n$, the third-order Moyal
coefficient from Proposition~\ref{prop:moyal-monomial} is
$$P^3(f,g)=
  \sum_{a=0}^3(-1)^a\binom{3}{a}
  (k)_{3-a}(l)_a(m)_a(n)_{3-a}
  z_1^{k+m-3}z_2^{l+n-3},$$
where $(r)_s=r(r-1)\cdots(r-s+1)$ and $(r)_0=1$. Therefore
$$[f,g]_*=\hbar(kn-lm)z_1^{k+m-1}z_2^{l+n-1}
  +\frac{\hbar^3}{24}P^3(f,g)+O(\hbar^5).$$
The script \texttt{scripts/check\_moyal\_coefficients.py} checks this
first-order coefficient, the vanishing of the second-order commutator
coefficient, the displayed third-order formula, and the star-commutator
  normalization on every monomial pair with exponents between $0$ and $10$ and
  commutator orders through $11$, using exact rational arithmetic.

This algebraic computation is an observable-product target, not an effective
action computation. In Costello's formalism the connected effective action and
the product/OPE of quantum observables are related but distinct. For two
boundary observables $F,G$, the finite-scale free-field model has the schematic
product
$$F\star_L G=m\circ\exp(\hbar\partial_{P_{\partial,L}})(F\otimes G),$$
where $P_{\partial,L}$ is the boundary or brane-defect propagator induced by the
chosen kernel. The connected single-trace part is the cumulant, or primitive
trace, extracted from this product. In this convention the order in $\hbar$ is
the number of boundary cross-contractions; it is not the loop order of the
connected effective interaction.

Thus there are two equivalent normalizations to keep separate. In Costello's
observable-product notation one asks for the boundary contraction kernel
$$P_{\partial,L}\leadsto \frac12P$$
on degree-zero Hamiltonians, so that
$$m\circ\exp(\hbar\partial_{P_{\partial,L}})
  \leadsto m\circ\exp\left(\frac{\hbar}{2}P\right),$$
the Weyl/Moyal product used above. The ordered product has labeled inputs
$(f,g)$; its $r$ identical cross-contractions contribute the denominator $r!$.
No additional division by interchanging the two boundary vertices is made in
the ordered product. The reverse order is the separate product $g*f$ whose
subtraction forms the commutator.

\begin{prob}[First and third graph normalizations inside Costello theory]\label{prob:first-third-graph-normalizations}
  Work inside the observable product supplied by
  Statement~\ref{stmt:costello-bv-package}, after solving the Hamiltonian
  specialization data of Problem~\ref{prob:analytic-graph-realization}. The
  first graph fixes the normalization; the third graph tests whether the
  Costello specialization really lands on the Weyl/Moyal product rather than on
  another deformation quantization convention. The analytic problem is reduced
  to the following checks:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item identify the connected one-propagator brane-defect ribbon graph whose
      tensor contraction is $P(f,g)$, and prove that its antisymmetrised
      boundary product gives the raw coefficient
      $\hbar P(f,g)$;
    \item fix the sign, automorphism factor, boundary orientation, and trace
      normalization in this first-order graph so that the coefficient is
      $\hbar(kn-lm)z_1^{k+m-1}z_2^{l+n-1}$ on monomials;
    \item identify the connected three-propagator brane-defect ribbon graphs
      whose tensor contraction is $P^3(f,g)$;
    \item prove that every other connected order-$\hbar^3$ contribution in the
      Hamiltonian sector vanishes, is counterterm-exact, or lands in the
      discarded scalar trace;
    \item compute the graph weight and automorphism factor after the
      first-order normalization and show that the coefficient is
      $\frac1{24}$;
    \item check that connected large-$N$ extraction introduces no additional
      factor of $N$, sign, or scalar moment-map term.
  \end{enumerate}
  The desired output is the pair of algebraic coefficients
  $$\hbar P(f,g),\qquad \frac{\hbar^3}{24}P^3(f,g)$$
  in the Weyl/Moyal commutator. The formal coefficients are proved above; the
  analytic work is exactly the specialization and normalization check listed
  here.
\end{prob}

\begin{rmk}
  In characteristic zero, $GL_N$ is linearly reductive on rational
  representations. Therefore taking $GL_N$-invariants is exact, and
  $$H\bigl((\mc R_N)^{GL_N},Q\bigr)\cong H(\mc R_N,Q)^{GL_N}.$$
  This justifies the invariant calculation used above. A Chevalley BRST model
  for the quotient stack introduces Lie algebra ghosts as presentation data.
  Linearly reductive exactness identifies derived algebraic invariants with
  ordinary invariants in this setting.
\end{rmk}

\subsection{Further directions}

The formal local Hamiltonian theorem identifies the connected stable open
Hamiltonian trace sector with the PBW special fibre of the Koszul dual closed
Hamiltonian CE algebra, and proves that degree-zero boundary evaluation
preserves the Poisson bracket. The next closed-open step is the cochain-level
Poisson factorization-center lift of Problem~\ref{prob:formal-factorization-center}.

The quantum Weyl/Moyal formulas give the algebraic target for deformation, and
Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-n-reduced-moyal} proves the finite-$N$ reduced
radial-parts identity for Weyl/Capelli generators. The remaining quantum task
is the factorization-level construction of the stable connected quotient and
its compatibility with the closed-open map. The analytic task is to identify
the Costello graph package with this mixed Hamiltonian BF sector, including the
brane-defect kernel, anomaly comparison, connected trace extraction, and the
first/third graph normalizations of
Problem~\ref{prob:analytic-graph-realization}. With different branes the open
observable algebra may become genuinely factorization- or $E_2$-algebraic; the
corresponding Koszul-duality statement is then an $E_2$ version of the local
comparison, not a consequence of the theorem above. The same caveat applies to
the expected $D_0$-brane analogue on $\R^2\times\C^4$.
