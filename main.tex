\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Dreams}
  One of the prevalent themes at play in both contemporary theoretical physics
  and mathematics is that of \textit{duality}. In particular, we will adopt a
  form of \textit{Koszul Duality} as a guiding light in our quest to understand
  some of the salient characteristics of \textbf{twisted M-Theory}.\par

  \begin{rmk}
    This introduction serves a motivational purpose and consequently de-emphasises
    rigour in favour of intuition; the precise statements appear in the body.
  \end{rmk}

  Let $X$ be a holomorphic symplectic manifold. We will consider a topological
  twist of M-theory on $\R \times X$ in the presence of a stack of $N$
  one-dimensional topological branes supported on $\R \times p$, for some point
  $p \in X$.

  In this scenario one expects to be able to construct two algebras:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The algebra $\mc{A}_N$ of operators on the stack of $N$ branes
      supported on $\R \times p$.
    \item The algebra $\mc{B}$ of local operators of the gravitational theory
      on $\R \times X$. Operator product expansions in the direction of $\R$
      equip $\mc{B}$ with the structure of an associative algebra.
  \end{itemize}

  Twisted holography then leads us to expect:
  \begin{conj}
    As $N \to \infty$, $\mc{A}_N$ is the Koszul dual of $\mc{B}$.
  \end{conj}

  \begin{rmk}
    One should be able to exploit Witten's proposal \cite{witten-cs} for
    holographic calculations of the OPE.
  \end{rmk}

  These notes isolate one local mixed A/B example and verify the classical
  stable operator-algebra match. The quantum discussion is restricted to the
  canonical first-order bracket and the formal odd-power Moyal targets; the
  full closed-string Feynman integral calculation is not claimed here.

\subsection{The local theorem}

Let
$$\bar A=\C[z_1,z_2]/\C\cdot1,\qquad
  \mathfrak h=\bar A,\qquad
  \mathfrak g=\mathfrak h\ltimes\mathfrak h^\vee[1],$$
where $\mathfrak h$ carries the Poisson bracket
$$\{f,g\}=\partial_{z_1}f\,\partial_{z_2}g
  -\partial_{z_2}f\,\partial_{z_1}g,$$
understood after projection modulo constants, and $\mathfrak h^\vee$ is the continuous Taylor dual with its coadjoint
$\mathfrak h$-module structure. Constants are removed because a constant
Hamiltonian has zero Hamiltonian vector field, while on the open side
$\mathrm{Tr}(1)=N$ is a scalar parameter rather than a nontrivial operator.
The symbol $\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}$ denotes an abstract copy of $\bar A$ whose
basis vectors are represented by the first descendant cycles constructed below.

\begin{thm}[Local stable Hamiltonian-sector Koszul duality]\label{thm:main-local}
  In the formal local mixed A/B model on $\R^2\times\C^2$ with brane
  $\R\times p$, the stable large-$N$ scalar-reduced open Hamiltonian
  degree-zero trace algebra, enlarged by the distinguished first Koszul
  descendants, is
  $$\mc{O}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{brane}}
    \cong \mathbf{S}\bigl(\bar A\oplus\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}[1]\bigr).$$
  The closed local operator algebra is the continuous Chevalley-Eilenberg
  algebra
  $$\mc{O}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{grav}}
    \cong C^\bullet_{\mathrm{CE},\mathrm{cont}}(\mathfrak g)
    =\widehat{\mathbf{S}}\bigl(\mathfrak g^\vee[-1]\bigr).$$
  Its Koszul dual is the completed graded enveloping algebra
  $$\bigl(\mc{O}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{grav}}\bigr)^!
    \simeq \widehat{U(\mathfrak g)}.$$
  The Rees algebra $U_\hbar(\mathfrak g)$ has special fibre
  $$U_\hbar(\mathfrak g)/(\hbar)\cong
    \mathbf{S}\bigl(\bar A\oplus\mathfrak h^\vee[1]\bigr),$$
  which identifies the PBW classical limit of the closed Koszul dual with the
  open stable Hamiltonian-sector algebra after choosing the monomial-dual basis
  $\mathfrak h^\vee\cong\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}$. On degree-zero generators the
  identification is induced by the scalar-reduced bulk-to-boundary evaluation
  map
  $$\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}(f)=\overline{\mathrm{Tr}\,f(\phi_1,\phi_2)},$$
  which sends a closed Hamiltonian to the corresponding brane Hamiltonian and
  intertwines Poisson brackets. The shifted generators are sent to the first
  Koszul descendants $\Psi_{k,l}$. On monomials,
  $$z_1^kz_2^l\longleftrightarrow
    \overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)},\qquad k+l>0,$$
  the first bracket is
  $$\{\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)},
      \overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^m\phi_2^n)}\}
    =\overline{(kn-lm)\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^{k+m-1}\phi_2^{l+n-1})},$$
  matching the Poisson bracket on $\bar A$.
\end{thm}

\subsection{Status of assertions}

The theorem above is the precise local assertion proved in these notes; the
terms appearing in it are constructed in the subsections below. The proof
consists of the affine Ext calculation, the open BV reduction, the stable
invariant-theory calculation of the scalar-reduced Hamiltonian trace algebra,
the construction of the first descendant cycles, the formal Hamiltonian BF model
for the closed side, the construction of the bulk-to-boundary Hamiltonian
evaluation map, the CE/Koszul-duality computation, and the first bracket
calculation.

The Moyal $\hbar^3$ formula below is a formal deformation-quantization target
checked by an exact script. It is not presented as the value of a closed-string
Feynman integral. The ambient twisted-holography and M-theory discussion is
motivation for this local theorem, not an input to its proof.

\subsection{Conventions for the local calculation}

Everything below is over $\C$. The phrase \emph{formal local model} means the
completed Taylor model at the chosen point of $\C^2$; equivalently, one may
work polynomially degree by degree and then complete at the augmentation ideal.
The symbol $\widehat{\mathbf S}$ denotes this completed symmetric algebra, and
all Chevalley-Eilenberg cochains are continuous cochains for the same
filtration.

Shifts follow the BV convention used in the field descriptions: an element of
$V[1]$ has parity reversed and cohomological degree one lower than the
corresponding element of $V$. The notation $\mathbf S(V)$ always means the free
graded-commutative algebra on the graded vector space $V$.

We write $\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}$ for an abstract copy of $\bar A$ whose basis
vector labelled by $z_1^kz_2^l$ is represented on the brane by the first
descendant $\Psi_{k,l}$. On the closed side the cotangent field gives the
continuous Taylor dual $\mathfrak h^\vee$, not an adjoint copy of
$\mathfrak h$. Choosing the monomial Taylor basis identifies
$\mathfrak h^\vee$ with $\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}$ as a filtered vector space;
this is a basis identification, not a claim that the coadjoint module is
canonically the adjoint module.

Boundary terms on $\R$ are discarded by taking compactly supported variations,
or equivalently by working on an interval with fixed boundary values and then
passing to local operators in the interior. The term \emph{stable large-$N$}
means the degreewise stable value of the invariant algebras under block
restriction as $N\to\infty$; no finite-$N$ trace identity is being suppressed
inside a fixed finite matrix algebra. The scalar trace $\mathrm{Tr}(1)=N$ is
treated as a parameter and is removed from the reduced operator algebra.
If $T$ is a trace expression, $\overline T$ denotes its class modulo the scalar
line $\C\cdot\mathrm{Tr}(1)$.

On the open side we compute the scalar-reduced degree-zero Hamiltonian traces
and adjoin the distinguished first Koszul descendants. The full stable BV
invariant Koszul homology, including possible higher antifield sectors and
possible boundaries involving two or more $\psi$'s, is not computed here and is
not used in the bracket comparison.

\subsection{The concrete dictionary}

The comparison is the following explicit dictionary. For $k,l\geq0$ and
$k+l>0$, set
$$H_{k,l}=z_1^kz_2^l\in\bar A,\qquad
  E_{k,l}=\delta_{k,l}[1]\in\mathfrak h^\vee[1],$$
where $\delta_{k,l}$ is dual to the Taylor monomial $z_1^kz_2^l$.
On the brane side set
$$O_{k,l}=\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)},$$
and let $\Psi_{k,l}$ be the symmetrised trace obtained by inserting one
$\psi$ into the word with $k$ copies of $\phi_1$ and $l$ copies of $\phi_2$;
the odd matrix $\psi$ is the Koszul variable for the moment-map equation,
introduced in Construction~\ref{constr:reduced-bv-algebra}.
The theorem proves that the assignment
$$H_{k,l}\longmapsto O_{k,l},\qquad E_{k,l}\longmapsto\Psi_{k,l}$$
extends to an isomorphism of the scalar-reduced Hamiltonian descendant algebra
with
$$\mathbf S(\bar A\oplus\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}[1])
  \xrightarrow{\ \sim\ }\mc O_{\mathrm{brane}}^{\mathrm{cl}}$$
in the sector considered here. On the closed side the symbols
$H_{k,l},E_{k,l}$ form PBW generators of the Rees specialization of
$U_\hbar(\bar A\ltimes\mathfrak h^\vee[1])$.

Thus every later step has a concrete target: construct the symbols
$O_{k,l},\Psi_{k,l}$ from the open fields, construct $H_{k,l},E_{k,l}$ from
Hamiltonians and cotangent Hamiltonians, prove that commutators of words reduce
to commutative monomials, and check that the bracket coefficient is
$(kn-lm)$.

\subsection{String Field Theory and Koszul Duality}
  We expect Koszul duality for string field theories to be reflected in
  stable trace invariants and cyclic homology. The algebraic template is the
  Loday-Quillen-Tsygan theorem \cites{loday-quillen}{tsygan}:
  \begin{thm}[Loday-Quillen-Tsygan]
    Let $A$ be a unital associative algebra over a field of characteristic
    zero. The stable Chevalley-Eilenberg homology of
    $\lie{gl}_{\infty}(A)$ is the free graded-commutative algebra generated by
    the shifted cyclic homology of $A$:
    $$H_*^{\mathrm{CE}}\bigl(\lie{gl}_{\infty}(A)\bigr)
      \cong \mathbf{S}\bigl(HC_{*-1}(A)\bigr).$$
  \end{thm}

  Thus stable matrix invariants are controlled by cyclic words. In the
  particular reduced model below, BRST exactness of $[\phi_1,\phi_2]$ turns
  cyclic words into ordinary commutative monomials, and the surviving
  single-trace generators are represented by
  $\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)$ with $k+l>0$.

  The calculation below is the corresponding classical shadow: closed
  Hamiltonian modes match stable single-trace generators, and the first
  nontrivial bracket agrees with the Poisson bracket.

\subsection{Flavours of Topological Strings}
Topological strings manifest in various flavours. Common examples include:

\begin{itemize}
  \item The \textbf{A-model}, where branes are objects of a Fukaya category:
    Lagrangians equipped with the grading, spin or Pin, local-system, and
    unobstructedness data required to define Floer theory.
  \item The \textbf{B-model}, where branes are objects of
    $D^b\mathrm{Coh}(X)$, or of $\mathrm{Perf}(X)$ in the affine local model.
  \item \textbf{Mixed A-B models}, e.g.\ on $X \times X'$ with branes given by
    $L\times Z$ for $L \inj X$ Lagrangian and $Z \inj X'$ holomorphic.
\end{itemize}

\begin{rmk}
  The A-model is only interesting with world-sheet instantons taken into
  account.
\end{rmk}

Regardless of the context we will work in, we need to understand:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The open string theory, which is the theory on a brane, and
  \item The closed string field theory, of a gravitational nature.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Open Strings}
If $\mc{C}$ is a cyclic Calabi-Yau $A_\infty$ category and
$\mc{F}\in\mathrm{Ob}\,\mc{C}$, set $A=\RHom(\mc{F},\mc{F})$. The open-string
states are $A$. The BV fields are\footnotemark\footnotetext{If we produce a category, the resulting field
  theory is defined on a point. If instead we produce a sheaf of categories,
  we get a field theory on the support of this sheaf, i.e.\ a field theory on
  the given brane.}
$$A[1].$$
The Calabi-Yau structure supplies a cyclic trace $\mathrm{Tr}:A\to\C[-d]$.
For a cyclic $A_\infty$ algebra with operations $m_n$, the formal
Chern-Simons action is
$$S(a)=\sum_{n\geq1}\frac{1}{n+1}\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(a\,m_n(a,\ldots,a)\bigr),
  \qquad a\in A[1].$$
This is the algebraic core of Witten's open string field theory
\cite{witten-cs} and of the AKSZ packaging \cite{aksz}.

  \section{The Local Model}

\subsection{Setup}
  Let $X = \R^2_A \times \C^2_B$. We use the exact A-brane given by the
  standard line $\R\subset\R^2_A$ with trivial brane data and the B-brane
  $\mc{O}_p\in\mathrm{Perf}(\C^2_B)$. Consider the product brane
  $$\R \times p \inj \R^2_A \times \C^2_B$$
  for some point $p \in \C^2_B$. The A-model lives on $\R^2_A$ (equipped with
  its standard symplectic structure) and the B-model lives on $\C^2_B$
  (equipped with its standard holomorphic-symplectic structure $\omega =
  dz_1\wedge dz_2$).

\subsection{Open String Field States}
  The open string states are the tensor product of A-model and B-model states.

  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{A-model factor.} In the exact self-Floer model for
      $\R\subset T^*\R\cong\R^2$, a small Hamiltonian perturbation identifies
      $CF^*(\R,\R)$ with the Morse complex of $\R$. Hence
      $HF^*(\R,\R)\cong H^*(\R;\C)$, and the de Rham complex $\Omega^*(\R)$ is
      the chain-level model used below.
    \item \textit{B-model factor.} Put affine coordinates at $p=0$ and write
      $R=\C[z_1,z_2]$, $\mathfrak m=(z_1,z_2)$, and $\mc{O}_p=R/\mathfrak m$.
      The skyscraper sheaf has open string states
      $$\Ext^*_R(R/\mathfrak m,R/\mathfrak m),$$
      computed in Proposition~\ref{prop:skyscraper-ext}.
  \end{itemize}

\begin{prop}[Affine skyscraper self-Ext]\label{prop:skyscraper-ext}
  Let $R=\C[z_1,z_2]$, $\mathfrak m=(z_1,z_2)$, and $k=R/\mathfrak m$. Then
  the Yoneda algebra of the skyscraper sheaf at the origin is
  $$\Ext^*_R(k,k)\cong\Lambda_{\C}(\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2),$$
  where $|\varepsilon_i|=1$. In particular this is the free
  graded-commutative algebra on two odd generators.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  The pair $(z_1,z_2)$ is a regular sequence in the polynomial ring $R$, so the
  affine Koszul complex
  $$K=\bigl(0\to R\,e_{12}\xrightarrow{d_2}R\,e_1\oplus R\,e_2
        \xrightarrow{d_1}R\to0\bigr)$$
  is a free resolution of $k$, with
  $$d_1(ae_1+be_2)=az_1+bz_2,\qquad
    d_2(e_{12})=z_2e_1-z_1e_2.$$
  Applying $\Hom_R(-,k)$ gives
  $$0\to k\xrightarrow{0}k\,\varepsilon_1\oplus k\,\varepsilon_2
    \xrightarrow{0}k\,\varepsilon_{12}\to0,$$
  because $z_1$ and $z_2$ act by zero on $k$. Hence the graded vector space of
  Ext groups is $k$ in degree $0$, $k\varepsilon_1\oplus k\varepsilon_2$ in
  degree $1$, and $k\varepsilon_{12}$ in degree $2$.

  The product is the Yoneda product. The Koszul resolution is the exterior
  dg-algebra $R\otimes\Lambda(e_1,e_2)$ with $d(e_i)=z_i$; its diagonal sends
  $e_i$ to $e_i\otimes1+1\otimes e_i$. Dualising this diagonal gives
  $\varepsilon_i^2=0$ and
  $\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2=-\varepsilon_2\varepsilon_1$, while
  $\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2$ spans the top Ext group. Thus the Yoneda
  algebra is $\Lambda_{\C}(\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2)$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Open String Field Theory}

The total space of fields (in the Batalin-Vilkovisky formalism) is
$$\alpha \in \Omega^*(\R)\otimes\Lambda(\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2)
  \otimes \lie{gl}_N[1]$$
with action
$$S(\alpha) = \int_{\R \times \C^{0|2}}\frac{1}{2}\,\mathrm{Tr}(\alpha\, d\alpha) + \frac{1}{3}\,\mathrm{Tr}\,\alpha^3.$$
Here $d = d_{\mathrm{dR}}$; the Ext algebra
$\Lambda(\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2)$ has zero differential. The integral
over $\C^{0|2}$ is the Berezin integral $\int d\varepsilon_1\,d\varepsilon_2$,
which picks out the top component in the odd directions.

\begin{constr}[The open field from the Ext basis]\label{constr:open-field-basis}
  Write $\varepsilon_{12}=\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2$. Before imposing ghost
  number, an open field is a finite sum
  $$\alpha=a_0+a_1\varepsilon_1+a_2\varepsilon_2+a_{12}\varepsilon_{12},$$
  where each $a_I$ is a de Rham form on $\R$ with values in $\lie{gl}_N$,
  shifted by $[1]$. The ghost-number-zero components are exactly
  $$A\in\Omega^1(\R)\otimes\lie{gl}_N,\qquad
    \phi_1,\phi_2\in\Omega^0(\R)\otimes\lie{gl}_N,$$
  and they appear as
  $$\alpha_0=A+\phi_1\varepsilon_1+\phi_2\varepsilon_2.$$
  The Berezin integral kills every monomial except the coefficient of
  $\varepsilon_{12}$, so every term in the ghost-zero action is obtained by
  multiplying the displayed three summands and keeping exactly one real
  one-form and exactly the product $\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2$.
\end{constr}

\begin{rmk}
  The shift $[1]$ acts on the total cohomological degree: a form of de Rham
  degree $p$ and Grassmann degree $q$ has total degree $p+q$, which after the
  shift sits in ghost number $p+q-1$. In particular, the 1-form component of
  $\alpha$ (de Rham degree 1, Grassmann degree 0) sits in ghost number 0 and
  is the bosonic gauge field. The scalar components proportional to
  $\varepsilon_i$ (de Rham degree 0, Grassmann degree 1) also sit in ghost
  number 0 and represent the motion of the brane in the two normal directions.
\end{rmk}

\subsubsection{De-BV-fying the open string field theory}
  The ghost-number-zero part of the BV field is
  $$\alpha_0=A+\phi_1\varepsilon_1+\phi_2\varepsilon_2,$$
  where $A\in\Omega^1(\R)\otimes\lie{gl}_N$ and
  $\phi_i\in\Omega^0(\R)\otimes\lie{gl}_N$.

  \begin{lem}\label{lem:open-action-reduction}
    With the Berezin convention
    $\int_{\C^{0|2}}\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2=1$, the restriction of the
    BV action to $\alpha_0$ is, up to a boundary term on $\R$,
    $$S(\alpha_0)=\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2
      +A[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr).$$
  \end{lem}

  \begin{proof}
    Only monomials containing one real one-form and the factor
    $\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2$ survive the integration over
    $\R\times\C^{0|2}$. In the quadratic term this leaves
    $$\frac12\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2-\phi_2\,d\phi_1\bigr),$$
    where the sign is the Koszul sign from interchanging the two odd
    generators. Since
    $d\,\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\phi_2)=\mathrm{Tr}(d\phi_1\,\phi_2+\phi_1\,d\phi_2)$,
    the second summand differs from $\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\,d\phi_2)$ by an exact
    one-form. In the cubic term the six orderings of
    $A,\phi_1\varepsilon_1,\phi_2\varepsilon_2$ reduce by cyclicity of the trace
    and the odd relation $\varepsilon_2\varepsilon_1=-\varepsilon_1\varepsilon_2$
    to $\mathrm{Tr}(A\phi_1\phi_2-A\phi_2\phi_1)$. This is
    $\mathrm{Tr}(A[\phi_1,\phi_2])$.
  \end{proof}

  The ghost-number-zero truncation of the BV master action is closed under the
  classical equations and gives a quantum-mechanical system with a connection
  form
  $$A \in \Omega^1(\R)\otimes \lie{gl}_N,$$
  two adjoint scalars
  $$\phi_1,\phi_2 \in \Omega^0(\R)\otimes\lie{gl}_N,$$
  and action
  $$S = \int_\R \mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2
    + A[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr).$$
  This action is invariant under
  $$\delta_{\varepsilon}\phi_i=[\varepsilon,\phi_i],\qquad
    \delta_{\varepsilon}A=d\varepsilon+[A,\varepsilon],$$
  for $\varepsilon\in\Omega^0(\R)\otimes\lie{gl}_N$. Indeed the variation of
  $\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1d\phi_2)$ containing $d\varepsilon$ is
  $-\mathrm{Tr}(d\varepsilon[\phi_1,\phi_2])$, and this cancels the
  $d\varepsilon$ variation of $\mathrm{Tr}(A[\phi_1,\phi_2])$; the remaining
  terms cancel by invariance of the trace pairing.

\begin{rmk}
  One can also proceed directly within the Calabi-Yau category: take a given
  object, tensor with $\C^N$, and pass to endomorphisms. More generally, one
  should work with the cyclic cohomology of the category; the 3-point function
  is computable via the cup-product in cyclic cohomology together with the
  trace, while higher $n$-point functions require additional structure.
\end{rmk}

\begin{rmk}
  In AKSZ language this is the one-dimensional gauged sigma model with
  Hamiltonian target $\lie{gl}_N\oplus\lie{gl}_N$ and symplectic potential
  $\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\,d\phi_2)$. We use the resulting Poisson bivector
  $$\Pi=\sum_{i,j}\frac{\partial}{\partial(\phi_1)^i{}_j}
    \wedge\frac{\partial}{\partial(\phi_2)^j{}_i},$$
  so $\{(\phi_1)^i{}_j,(\phi_2)^k{}_l\}=\delta^i_l\delta^k_j$. Equivalently,
  with the convention $\iota_{X_f}\omega=df$, the symplectic form is
  $-\mathrm{Tr}(d\phi_1\wedge d\phi_2)$. The $GL_N$ moment map is
  $\mu=[\phi_1,\phi_2]$, up to the same harmless sign convention for the
  infinitesimal conjugation action. The classical reduced phase space is the
  stack of commuting pairs of matrices modulo conjugation.
\end{rmk}

\begin{rmk}
  Equivalently, take Chern-Simons theory on $\R\times T^2$ and restrict to the
  harmonic forms $1,\eta_1,\eta_2,\eta_1\eta_2$ on $T^2$. Writing
  $A_{3d}=A_t\,dt+\phi_1\eta_1+\phi_2\eta_2$ and integrating over $T^2$
  gives the same action; the odd variables $\varepsilon_i$ are the algebraic
  avatars of the degree-one cohomology classes $\eta_i$.
\end{rmk}

\subsection{Closed String States}
Similarly, the closed string state space is of the form
$$\text{A-model closed states}\otimes \text{B-model closed states.}$$
For the A-model on $\R^2$, we take the de Rham cohomology complex
$\Omega^*(\R^2)$.

For the B-model on $\C^2$, we use the local holomorphic-symplectic
Kodaira-Spencer model obtained from Hamiltonian holomorphic vector fields. It
is BCOV-like in its use of polyvectors and the divergence operator, but it is
not the Calabi-Yau threefold BCOV theory with one sector simply deleted.

\begin{rmk}
  The original BCOV analysis \cite{bcov} is written for Calabi-Yau threefolds.
  On a holomorphic symplectic surface the natural local cotangent theory is the
  BF theory for the dg Lie algebra of Hamiltonian vector fields. The
  holomorphic symplectic form supplies the Poisson bracket, while the
  holomorphic volume form supplies the divergence operator on polyvectors.
\end{rmk}

We consider the space of polyvector fields
$$\mathbf{PV}^{*,*}(\C^2) \cong \Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)[\partial_{z_1},\partial_{z_2}]$$
where $\partial_{z_i}$ are odd generators of poly-vector degree 1 (corresponding
to the two holomorphic vector fields on $\C^2$). The operator
$$\partial: \mathbf{PV}^{i,*} \to \mathbf{PV}^{i-1,*}$$
is the divergence operator determined by the holomorphic volume form
$\Omega=dz_1\wedge dz_2$:
$$\partial_{\Omega}=\iota_{\Omega}^{-1}\circ\partial_{\mathrm{hol}}\circ
  \iota_{\Omega},\qquad
  \iota_{\Omega}:\mathbf{PV}^{i,*}\xrightarrow{\sim}\Omega^{2-i,*}.$$
Thus for $X=a_1\partial_{z_1}+a_2\partial_{z_2}$,
$$\partial_{\Omega}X=(\partial_{z_1}a_1+\partial_{z_2}a_2),$$
while $\partial_{\Omega}$ vanishes on functions.

We work in the formal polynomial local model at $0\in\C^2$, so Dolbeault
cohomology is represented by holomorphic Taylor polynomials. In this model the
small closed-string state space is the $\partial_{\Omega}$-closed part of
polyvectors. Analysing degree by degree:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\mathbf{PV}^{0,*}(\C^2) = \Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)\subset\Ker\partial$
    trivially.
  \item $\mathbf{PV}^{1,*}(\C^2)\cong\Omega^{1,*}(\C^2)$ via
    $\iota_{(-)}\omega$. Under this identification, with the Hamiltonian
    convention $\iota_{X_f}\omega=-df$ used below,
    $\Ker\partial_{\Omega}$ is the space of closed holomorphic one-forms.
    Lemma~\ref{lem:polynomial-poincare} identifies these with $df$, uniquely
    modulo constants.
  \item The constant Hamiltonians act trivially, since their Hamiltonian vector
    fields vanish. We therefore use the reduced polynomial algebra
    $$\bar A=\C[z_1,z_2]/\C\cdot1$$
    for nontrivial Hamiltonian fields.
\end{itemize}

\begin{lem}[Polynomial Poincare primitive]\label{lem:polynomial-poincare}
  Let
  $$\theta=a(z_1,z_2)\,dz_1+b(z_1,z_2)\,dz_2$$
  be a polynomial one-form on $\C^2$. If
  $\partial_{z_2}a=\partial_{z_1}b$, then there is a polynomial
  $f\in\C[z_1,z_2]$, unique modulo constants, such that $\theta=df$.
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  Define
  $$f(z_1,z_2)=\int_0^1
    \bigl(z_1a(tz_1,tz_2)+z_2b(tz_1,tz_2)\bigr)\,dt.$$
  Since $a$ and $b$ are polynomials, the integral is just division of each
  homogeneous term by its degree plus one, hence $f$ is polynomial. Differentiate
  under the integral sign. Closedness says
  $\partial_{z_2}a=\partial_{z_1}b$, so
  \begin{align*}
  \partial_{z_1}f
    &=\int_0^1\bigl(a(tz)+tz_1\partial_{z_1}a(tz)
       +tz_2\partial_{z_1}b(tz)\bigr)\,dt\\
    &=\int_0^1\bigl(a(tz)+t z_1\partial_{z_1}a(tz)
       +t z_2\partial_{z_2}a(tz)\bigr)\,dt\\
    &=\int_0^1\frac{d}{dt}\bigl(t\,a(tz)\bigr)\,dt
      =a(z).
  \end{align*}
  The same calculation, with $a$ and $b$ interchanged, gives
  $\partial_{z_2}f=b(z)$. Therefore $df=\theta$.
  If $df=dg$, then $d(f-g)=0$, hence $f-g$ is constant.
\end{proof}

\begin{rmk}
  A global analytic theory on noncompact $\C^2$ requires boundary conditions,
  a gauge slice, and a choice of Green kernel. The computations below use only
  the formal local algebra of polynomial Hamiltonians and do not require a
  global propagator.
\end{rmk}

For the closed string field theory we replace $\Ker\partial$ (on
poly-1-vectors) by the Dolbeault-valued Hamiltonian vector fields:
$$\Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)\text{-valued Hamiltonian vector fields},$$
i.e.\ sections of $\Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)$ identified with Hamiltonian vector
fields via the symplectic form $\omega = dz_1\wedge dz_2$. Concretely, a
section $f\in\Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)$ corresponds to the Hamiltonian vector field
$X_f=\{f,-\}_\omega$, where
$$\{f,g\}_\omega=
  \partial_{z_1}f\,\partial_{z_2}g-\partial_{z_2}f\,\partial_{z_1}g.$$
With this convention
$$X_f=(\partial_{z_1}f)\partial_{z_2}
  -(\partial_{z_2}f)\partial_{z_1},\qquad \iota_{X_f}\omega=-df.$$

\begin{rmk}
  The replacement is the elementary statement
  $$\Ker(\partial_{\Omega}:\mathbf{PV}^{1}\to\mathbf{PV}^{0})
    \cong \{df:f\in\C[z_1,z_2]\}
    \cong \bar A.$$
  The first isomorphism uses the holomorphic symplectic form and the second
  is the polynomial Poincare lemma on affine space. Constants are removed
  because $X_f=0$ exactly when $f$ is constant.
\end{rmk}

\subsection{Closed String Field Theory}

\subsubsection{The Action Functional}

Let
$$D=d_{\R^2}+\bar\partial_{\C^2}.$$
Write
$$\alpha\in\Omega^*(\R^2)\,\hat{\otimes}\,\Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)\otimes\bar A[1]$$
for the Hamiltonian connection field and
$$\beta\in\Omega^*(\R^2)\,\hat{\otimes}\,\Omega^{0,*}(\C^2)\otimes\bar A^{\vee}[2]$$
for its cotangent field. The bracket on form-valued Hamiltonians is obtained
by wedging the form parts and taking the Poisson bracket of the Hamiltonian
parts. The action is
$$S=\int_{\R^2\times\C^2}\Bigl\langle\beta,\,
  D\alpha+\frac12\{\alpha,\alpha\}\Bigr\rangle\,dz_1\,dz_2.$$
The pairing is the evaluation pairing between $\bar A^{\vee}$ and $\bar A$
(or, in a formal residue model, the corresponding continuous residue pairing).
For ghost-number-zero fields, $\alpha$ has total real/Dolbeault form degree
$1$ and $\beta$ has total degree $2$. Hence
$\beta\wedge D\alpha$ and $\beta\wedge\{\alpha,\alpha\}$ have total
real/Dolbeault degree $4$; multiplying by $dz_1\wedge dz_2$ gives a top real
degree $6$ form on $\R^2\times\C^2$.

\begin{rmk}
  Before, the quantum mechanical system lived on a line (the extent of the
  brane), while this gravitational theory occupies all of space-time.
\end{rmk}

\subsubsection{De-BV-fying the closed string field theory}

This theory is closely related to a 4-dimensional BF theory: $\beta$ plays the
role of the $B$-field (Lagrange multiplier) and the equations of motion of
$\beta$ enforce $F_\alpha = 0$, where
$$F_\alpha = D\alpha + \tfrac{1}{2}\{\alpha,\alpha\}$$
is the ``curvature'' of $\alpha$ (viewed as a connection on a bundle of
holomorphic-symplectic surfaces over $\R^2$).

Consider the space-time $\R^2_{x_i}\times\C^2_{z_i}$. The ghost-number-zero
components of $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are:
$$\alpha = \alpha_{x_i}\,dx_i + \alpha_{\bar z_j}\,d\bar z_j,\qquad
  \beta = \beta_{x_1 x_2}\,dx_1\,dx_2 + \beta_{x_i\bar z_j}\,dx_i\,d\bar z_j
  + \beta_{\bar z_1\bar z_2}\,d\bar z_1\,d\bar z_2.$$
Here the coefficients of $\alpha$ are Hamiltonian functions modulo constants.
The gauge symmetries are the BF-type transformations
$$\delta_{\varepsilon}\alpha=D\varepsilon+\{\alpha,\varepsilon\},\qquad
  \delta_{\varepsilon}\beta=\operatorname{ad}^{\vee}_{\varepsilon}\beta,$$
and
$$\delta_{\lambda}\alpha=0,\qquad
  \delta_{\lambda}\beta=D_{\alpha}\lambda,$$
where $D_{\alpha}=D+\{\alpha,-\}$ and $\lambda$ is a one-form valued in
$\bar A^{\vee}$. The second symmetry is reducible on shell: if
$\lambda=D_{\alpha}\mu$, then
$D_{\alpha}\lambda=D_{\alpha}^2\mu=\{F_{\alpha},\mu\}$, which vanishes when
$F_{\alpha}=0$.

Analysing $\alpha$ as a Hamiltonian function on $\C^2$: the component
$\alpha_{\bar z_j}d\bar z_j$ is the Hamiltonian potential whose vector field
$X_{\alpha_{\bar z_j}}d\bar z_j$ is the Beltrami differential encoding a
deformation of the complex structure of $\C^2$; the component
$\alpha_{x_i}dx_i$ similarly defines a connection on $\R^2$ valued in
Hamiltonian vector fields on $\C^2$, encoding a bundle of $\C^2$s over
$\R^2$. Together, the
equation of motion $F_\alpha=0$ says this data constitutes an integrable
deformation of complex structure with flat holonomy. Since $\beta$ appears
only linearly in the action, it is a Lagrange multiplier enforcing exactly this
equation.

\begin{rmk}
  This is entirely analogous to BF theory, where $B$ is a Lagrange multiplier
  enforcing $F_A=0$, and the phase space is $T^*\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{flat}}$.
\end{rmk}

\subsubsection{Phase Space}
Compactify one real direction and put the theory on $\R\times S^1\times X$
where $X$ is holomorphic symplectic. The equations of motion $F_\alpha=0$
cut out the Maurer-Cartan moduli problem for Hamiltonian vector fields on
$S^1\times X$. The perturbative phase space is the derived cotangent stack
$$T^*_{\mathrm{der}}\mathrm{MC}\bigl(\Omega^*(S^1)\widehat{\otimes}
  \Omega^{0,*}(X,\mathrm{Ham}(X)),D,\{-,-\}\bigr).$$
After passing to the underived tangent space at the trivial solution this
reduces to the ordinary cotangent bundle of the linearized deformation complex.

\subsection{Closed String Fields and Brane Deformations}

At the algebraic level, a closed Hamiltonian supplies a candidate deformation
of the gauge theory on the brane by evaluation on the normal matrix fields. For
example, if the brane lies along $x_1$ and the closed string field is
$dx_1\,z_1^k z_2^l$, the boundary-evaluation prescription gives the deformed
action
$$\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2+A[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr)
  + \int_\R dx_1\,\mathrm{Tr}\,\phi_1^k\phi_2^l.$$
Deriving this coupling from a full BV bulk-boundary action requires boundary
conditions and a gauge-fixing choice; it is not part of the local algebra
theorem below.
Construction~\ref{constr:boundary-evaluation} is the algebraic form of this
bulk-to-boundary prescription, and Theorem~\ref{thm:bulk-boundary} below proves
that the resulting Hamiltonian evaluation preserves the classical bracket.

\begin{rmk}
  Geometrically, specifying a closed string field on $\C^2$ amounts to
  equipping $\C^2$ with a connection, so that the brane can parallel-transport
  its internal degrees of freedom as it moves along $\R$.
\end{rmk}

\subsection{Operators in the Gauge Theory and the Large-$N$ Limit}

The local-operator calculation is performed in the reduced BV model with
fields $\phi_1,\phi_2$ and the antifield $\psi=A^*$ for the Lagrange
multiplier $A$. We take $GL_N$-invariants in characteristic zero, so the
Chevalley part of the gauge BRST differential acts trivially on trace
functions by cyclicity.

\begin{constr}[The reduced BV algebra at the insertion point]\label{constr:reduced-bv-algebra}
  At a fixed point of the brane line, introduce three matrix variables
  $$\phi_1,\phi_2\in\lie{gl}_N,\qquad \psi\in\lie{gl}_N[1].$$
  The reduced local polynomial algebra is
  $$\mc R_N=\C[(\phi_1)^i{}_j,(\phi_2)^i{}_j]
    \otimes\Lambda\bigl((\psi)^i{}_j\bigr).$$
  Its BRST differential is the derivation determined on generators by
  $$Q(\phi_1)^i{}_j=0,\qquad Q(\phi_2)^i{}_j=0,\qquad
    Q(\psi)^i{}_j=([\phi_1,\phi_2])^i{}_j.$$
  This formula is the algebraic shadow of the $A$-equation of motion:
  varying
  $$\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2+A[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr)$$
  with respect to $A$ gives $[\phi_1,\phi_2]=0$, and the antifield $\psi=A^*$
  records that equation as a Koszul differential.
\end{constr}

\begin{lem}[De Rham jets]\label{lem:derivative-jets}
  Positive jets in the topological direction do not contribute to local
  operator cohomology. Equivalently, every cohomology class has a representative
  depending only on the values of the fields and antifields at the insertion
  point.
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  For a coordinate $t$ on the line, the de Rham part of the BRST operator is
  $d_{\R}$. On the jet algebra one has the contraction homotopy
  $[d_{\R},\iota_{\partial_t}]=\partial_t$. Hence a local expression containing
  a positive $t$-derivative is cohomologous to zero after applying the usual
  Euler homotopy on jet degree. This kills topological-direction jets, not the
  holomorphic $z_i$-derivatives used later on the closed side.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}[Stable trace invariants]\label{prop:stable-trace-invariants}
  Let $F=\C\langle x_1,x_2\rangle$ and let $\mathrm{Cyc}(F)=F/[F,F]$ be the
  vector space of cyclic words. For each $N$, $GL_N$ acts on
  $\lie{gl}_N^2$ by simultaneous conjugation. The stable invariant algebra
  $$\C[\lie{gl}_\infty^2]^{GL_\infty}
    :=\varprojlim_{N}\C[\lie{gl}_N^2]^{GL_N}$$
  with respect to block-restriction maps
  is the free commutative algebra generated by the nonempty cyclic words:
  $$\C[\lie{gl}_\infty^2]^{GL_\infty}
    \cong\mathbf S\bigl(\mathrm{Cyc}(F)_{\mathrm{nonempty}}\bigr),$$
  where a cyclic word $w$ maps to the trace function
  $(\phi_1,\phi_2)\mapsto\mathrm{Tr}\,w(\phi_1,\phi_2)$.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Procesi--Razmyslov invariant theory \cites{procesi}{razmyslov} gives two
  inputs. First, $\C[\lie{gl}_N^2]^{GL_N}$ is generated by trace functions of
  words in the two generic matrices. Second, all polynomial relations among
  these trace functions are consequences of the fundamental trace identities
  of matrix size $N$; in particular every homogeneous trace relation has total
  word-degree at least $N+1$. Fix a total word-degree $d$. For every
  $N\geq d$ there are therefore no relations in degree $d$ beyond cyclicity
  of the trace. Passing degree by degree to the stable inverse limit removes
  all finite-$N$ trace identities, and the only surviving identification is
  $\mathrm{Tr}(uv)=\mathrm{Tr}(vu)$. This is exactly the symmetric algebra on
  cyclic words.

  For the one-$\psi$ sector used below, apply the same theorem to three even
  generic matrices and take the part linear in the third matrix. This gives
  traces of words with one marked letter and no stable relations beyond
  cyclicity. Declaring the marked letter odd only changes the
  graded-commutative signs in products of such traces; no relation with two
  marked letters is used in the Hamiltonian descendant sector.
\end{proof}

Varying
$$S=\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2+A[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr)$$
with respect to $A$ gives the moment-map equation
$[\phi_1,\phi_2]=0$. In the reduced BV complex,
$$Q\psi=[\phi_1,\phi_2],\qquad Q\phi_1=Q\phi_2=0$$
on invariant trace functions.

\begin{constr}[Boundary evaluation of a closed Hamiltonian]\label{constr:boundary-evaluation}
  Let $f=\sum_{k,l}c_{k,l}z_1^kz_2^l\in\C[z_1,z_2]$. The brane replaces the
  two normal coordinates $z_1,z_2$ by the two adjoint scalar fields
  $\phi_1,\phi_2$. Choosing the ordered lift with all $\phi_1$'s to the left
  of all $\phi_2$'s gives the invariant function
  $$\widetilde\partial_{\mathrm{bb},N}(f)
    =\sum_{k,l}c_{k,l}\,\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)
    \in\mc R_N^{GL_N}.$$
  Let
  $$H^0_{\mathrm{red}}(\mc R_N^{GL_N},Q)
    :=H^0(\mc R_N^{GL_N},Q)/\C\cdot\mathrm{Tr}(1).$$
  Constants give the scalar $c_{0,0}N$ and therefore vanish in this quotient.
  Thus the construction descends to a linear map
  $$\partial_{\mathrm{bb},N}:\bar A\to
    H^0_{\mathrm{red}}(\mc R_N^{GL_N},Q),\qquad
    [f]\longmapsto \overline{\widetilde\partial_{\mathrm{bb},N}(f)}.$$
  Let $\mathsf W_{k,l}$ be the set of words in two letters containing $k$
  copies of the first letter and $l$ copies of the second. The first descendant
  is obtained by inserting one Koszul variable and summing over all ordered
  lifts:
  $$\partial_{\mathrm{bb},N}^{(1)}(z_1^kz_2^l[1])=\Psi_{k,l},\qquad
    \Psi_{k,l}:=\sum_{w\in\mathsf W_{k,l}}
      \mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\psi\,w(\phi_1,\phi_2)\bigr).$$
\end{constr}

\begin{lem}[First descendants are cycles]\label{lem:first-descendant-cycles}
  For every $k,l\geq0$, the symmetrised trace $\Psi_{k,l}$ satisfies
  $Q\Psi_{k,l}=0$.
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  Write $x=\phi_1$ and $y=\phi_2$. Since $x$ and $y$ are even and
  $Q\psi=[x,y]$, one has
  $$Q\Psi_{k,l}
    =\sum_{w\in\mathsf W_{k,l}}\mathrm{Tr}\bigl((xy-yx)w(x,y)\bigr).$$
  Group the resulting traces by cyclic word of multidegree $(k+1,l+1)$. The
  coefficient of a cyclic word in the positive sum is the number of cyclic
  adjacent occurrences of the block $xy$; the coefficient in the negative sum
  is the number of cyclic adjacent occurrences of the block $yx$. On a circle
  the number of transitions from an $x$-run to a $y$-run equals the number of
  transitions from a $y$-run to an $x$-run. The two grouped sums are therefore
  equal, so $Q\Psi_{k,l}=0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lem}[Adjacent swaps]\label{lem:adjacent-swaps}
  In the invariant reduced BV cohomology,
  $$\mathrm{Tr}(u\phi_1\phi_2v)-\mathrm{Tr}(u\phi_2\phi_1v)$$
  is $Q$-exact for all words $u,v$.
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  By cyclicity of the trace,
  \begin{align*}
  Q\,\mathrm{Tr}(\psi vu)
    &=\mathrm{Tr}([\phi_1,\phi_2]vu)\\
    &=\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\phi_2vu-\phi_2\phi_1vu)\\
    &=\mathrm{Tr}(u\phi_1\phi_2v)-\mathrm{Tr}(u\phi_2\phi_1v).
  \end{align*}
\end{proof}

\begin{cor}[Matrix evaluation is commutative in cohomology]\label{cor:matrix-evaluation-well-defined}
  If two words in $\phi_1,\phi_2$ have the same numbers of $\phi_1$ and
  $\phi_2$, then their traces define the same class in the reduced BV
  cohomology. Consequently $\partial_{\mathrm{bb},N}(f)$ depends only on the
  commutative polynomial $f\in\C[z_1,z_2]$, not on the chosen ordered lift.
\end{cor}

\begin{proof}
  Adjacent transpositions generate all reorderings of a finite word. Each
  adjacent transposition of the adjacent letters $\phi_1,\phi_2$ changes the
  trace by a $Q$-exact term by Lemma~\ref{lem:adjacent-swaps}; cyclicity moves
  the chosen adjacent pair into the displayed position of that lemma. Summing
  the resulting exact differences proves the claim.
\end{proof}

Let $\bar A=\C[z_1,z_2]/\C\cdot1$. The removed constant corresponds on the
gauge side to the scalar $\mathrm{Tr}(1)=N$, not to a nontrivial Hamiltonian
generator.

\begin{prop}[Brane Hamiltonian sector]\label{prop:brane-ops}
  In the stable large-$N$ scalar-reduced BV model, the Hamiltonian descendant
  algebra generated by the nonconstant ghost-zero traces and the distinguished
  first Koszul descendant cycles is
  $$\mc{O}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{brane}}
    \cong \mathbf{S}\bigl(\bar A\oplus \bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}[1]\bigr).$$
  The class of $z_1^kz_2^l$ with $k+l>0$ corresponds to
  $\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)}$. The corresponding descendant
  basis vector corresponds to the symmetrised trace with one $\psi$ and $k,l$
  copies of $\phi_1,\phi_2$.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Proposition~\ref{prop:stable-trace-invariants} gives the free commutative
  algebra on cyclic words.
  Lemma~\ref{lem:adjacent-swaps} identifies any two cyclic words with the same
  numbers of $\phi_1$ and $\phi_2$, because adjacent transpositions generate
  all reorderings. Thus the scalar-reduced ghost-zero cyclic words reduce to
  the monomials $\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)}$ with $k+l>0$.
  Lemma~\ref{lem:first-descendant-cycles}
  gives the distinguished closed one-$\psi$ class
  $\Psi_{k,l}$ for each multidegree $(k,l)$. Products of these single-trace
  classes give the free graded-commutative multi-trace algebra in the
  Hamiltonian sector.
  By definition of the Hamiltonian sector, single traces containing two or
  more occurrences of $\psi$ are not part of this algebra.
\end{proof}

\begin{rmk}
  The word ``sector'' is literal. The calculation above presents the stable
  algebra generated by the no-$\psi$ Hamiltonians and the distinguished first
  descendants $\Psi_{k,l}$. It does not assert a presentation of the full
  invariant Koszul homology including all classes and boundaries with two or
  more occurrences of $\psi$.
\end{rmk}

\subsection{Operators in the Gravitational Theory}\label{sec:grav-ops}

Let $\mathfrak h=\bar A$ with Poisson bracket, and let
$\mathfrak h^\vee$ be the continuous Taylor dual. The cotangent field is a
coadjoint module, so the local Hamiltonian Lie algebra is
$$\mathfrak g=\mathfrak h\ltimes\mathfrak h^\vee[1].$$
For $f,g\in\mathfrak h$ and $\lambda,\mu\in\mathfrak h^\vee$, the brackets are
$$[f,g]=\{f,g\},\qquad [f,\eta_\lambda]=\eta_{\operatorname{ad}^{\vee}_f\lambda},
  \qquad [\eta_\lambda,\eta_\mu]=0,$$
where $(\operatorname{ad}^{\vee}_f\lambda)(g)=-\lambda(\{f,g\})$.
On monomials this reads
$$[z_1^kz_2^l,z_1^mz_2^n]
  =\overline{(kn-lm)z_1^{k+m-1}z_2^{l+n-1}},$$
where the bar means projection to $\bar A$; in particular the constant bracket
of $z_1$ and $z_2$ is zero in the reduced Hamiltonian Lie algebra.

The kinematic BRST operator is
$$Q_0=d_{\R^2}+\bar\partial_{\C^2}.$$
The same jet contraction as in Lemma~\ref{lem:derivative-jets}, together with
the Dolbeault Poincare lemma in the formal local model, leaves only
holomorphic Taylor coefficients. Thus the linear local coordinates are the
functionals
$$\partial_{z_1}^k\partial_{z_2}^l\alpha|_0,\qquad
  \partial_{z_1}^k\partial_{z_2}^l\beta|_0.$$
These are dual to the Taylor monomials $z_1^kz_2^l$ up to the factorial
normalisation fixed by the chosen pairing. Since zero-form components of
$\alpha$ and $\beta$ have negative field ghost numbers, the corresponding
linear functions have positive ghost numbers.

\begin{prop}\label{prop:grav-ops}
  The classical local operators of the formal closed theory are the continuous
  Chevalley-Eilenberg cochains
  $$\mc{O}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{grav}}
    \cong C^\bullet_{\mathrm{CE},\mathrm{cont}}(\mathfrak g)
    =\widehat{\mathbf{S}}\bigl(\mathfrak g^\vee[-1]\bigr),$$
  with CE differential dual to the Lie brackets above.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Work at the formal insertion point. The jet algebra of the fields is the
  completed free graded-commutative algebra on the Taylor coefficients of
  $\alpha$ and $\beta$. The kinematic differential
  $Q_0=d_{\R^2}+\bar\partial_{\C^2}$ contracts all positive real and
  anti-holomorphic jets: in the real directions the homotopy is
  $[d_{\R^2},\iota_{\partial_{x_i}}]=\partial_{x_i}$, and in the
  anti-holomorphic directions it is the formal Dolbeault Poincare homotopy.
  The $Q_0$-cohomology of the jet algebra is therefore generated by the
  holomorphic Taylor coefficients alone.

  The remaining $\alpha$-coefficients are Hamiltonians modulo constants, hence
  elements of $\mathfrak h=\bar A$. The cotangent field $\beta$ supplies the
  odd shifted continuous dual $\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$. Thus the linear space of
  residual local modes is
  $\mathfrak g=\mathfrak h\ltimes\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$.

  It remains to identify the nonlinear BRST differential. The BV Hamiltonian
  is
  $$S=\int\left\langle\beta,D\alpha+\frac12\{\alpha,\alpha\}\right\rangle.$$
  The linear part is exactly $Q_0$, already killed in cohomology. The quadratic
  term sends two Hamiltonian $\alpha$-modes $f,g$ to their reduced Poisson
  bracket $\{f,g\}$, and the action on the cotangent variable is the coadjoint
  action displayed above. There is no quadratic term with two cotangent inputs,
  so $[\eta_\lambda,\eta_\mu]=0$. Hence the residual BRST differential on
  functions is precisely the derivation dual to the Lie bracket on
  $\mathfrak g$, which is the Chevalley-Eilenberg differential. Continuity
  records the completion over polynomial degree.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Koszul Duality}

\begin{defn}
  For an augmented complete dg algebra $B$, its Koszul dual is
  $$B^!=\RHom_B(\C,\C).$$
  We regard commutative Chevalley-Eilenberg cochains as $E_1$ algebras by
  restriction. All bar and cobar constructions below are taken in the category
  of complete filtered vector spaces, with continuous maps and the polynomial
  degree filtration; degree by degree the CE coalgebra is conilpotent, so the
  completed cobar algebra is well-defined.
\end{defn}

\begin{prop}\label{prop:ce-koszul}
  Let $\mathfrak g=\mathfrak h\ltimes\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$ be the completed
  graded Lie algebra above, and let
  $$B=C^\bullet_{\mathrm{CE},\mathrm{cont}}(\mathfrak g)
    =\widehat{\mathbf{S}}\bigl(\mathfrak g^\vee[-1]\bigr).$$
  Then
  $$B^!\simeq \widehat{U(\mathfrak g)},$$
  the completed graded universal enveloping algebra.
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Let
  $$C_*^{\mathrm{CE}}(\mathfrak g)=\mathbf S^c(\mathfrak g[1])$$
  be the Chevalley-Eilenberg coalgebra, with coderivation determined by the
  bracket of $\mathfrak g$. By definition
  $$B=\Hom_{\mathrm{cont}}\bigl(C_*^{\mathrm{CE}}(\mathfrak g),\C\bigr).$$
  The augmentation $B\to\C$ has the standard bar resolution, and continuous
  duality identifies
  $$\RHom_B(\C,\C)\simeq
    \Omega\,C_*^{\mathrm{CE}}(\mathfrak g),$$
  the completed cobar algebra of the CE coalgebra. The Lie bar-cobar
  comparison \cites{priddy}{loday-vallette} identifies this cobar algebra
  with the completed associative algebra generated by $\mathfrak g$ modulo the
  graded Lie relations; this is $\widehat{U(\mathfrak g)}$.

  Thus, for the generators $f,g\in\mathfrak h$ and
  $\eta_\lambda,\eta_\mu\in\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$, the completed enveloping
  algebra has relations
  \begin{align*}
    fg-gf&=\{f,g\},\\
    f\eta_\lambda-\eta_\lambda f&=\eta_{\operatorname{ad}^{\vee}_f\lambda},\\
    \eta_\lambda\eta_\mu+\eta_\mu\eta_\lambda&=0.
  \end{align*}
\end{proof}

For comparison with the classical brane algebra introduce the Rees enveloping
algebra $U_{\hbar}(\mathfrak g)$ with relations
$$xy-(-1)^{|x||y|}yx=\hbar[x,y].$$
At $\hbar=0$ one obtains
$$U_{\hbar}(\mathfrak g)/(\hbar)\cong
  \mathbf{S}(\mathfrak g)
  =\mathbf{S}\bigl(\bar A\oplus\mathfrak h^\vee[1]\bigr).$$
After choosing the monomial-dual Taylor basis
$\mathfrak h^\vee\cong\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}$, this is the stable classical
brane Hamiltonian descendant algebra of Proposition~\ref{prop:brane-ops}. Thus
the free, abelian closed theory is Koszul dual directly to this symmetric
algebra, while the interacting Hamiltonian bracket deforms it to the completed
enveloping algebra via PBW.

\subsubsection{First-order corrections ($\hbar^1$)}

Under the Koszul duality correspondence we have
$$z_1^kz_2^l \leftrightarrow
  \overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)}.$$
The reduced Poisson bracket on the gravity side is:
$$\{z_1^kz_2^l,\, z_1^mz_2^n\}_{\bar A}
  =\overline{(kn - lm)\,z_1^{k+m-1}z_2^{l+n-1}}.$$
If an exponent in the displayed monomial is negative then the coefficient
$kn-lm$ is already zero; if the displayed monomial is constant, its class in
$\bar A$ is zero.
The matching bracket on the open side is not an $A$-propagator computation:
$A$ is a Lagrange multiplier. It is the canonical bracket induced by the
first-order kinetic term $\int\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1\,d\phi_2)$:
$$\{(\phi_1)^i{}_j,(\phi_2)^k{}_l\}
  =\delta^i_l\delta^k_j,\qquad
  \{(\phi_1)^i{}_j,(\phi_1)^k{}_l\}=0=\{(\phi_2)^i{}_j,(\phi_2)^k{}_l\}.$$

\begin{lem}[Hamiltonian reduction of the trace bracket]\label{lem:trace-bracket-descends}
  The canonical matrix Poisson bracket descends to the scalar-reduced
  invariant quotient used above.
\end{lem}

\begin{proof}
  For $\epsilon\in\lie{gl}_N$, the component
  $\mu_\epsilon=\mathrm{Tr}(\epsilon[\phi_1,\phi_2])$ of the moment map
  generates the infinitesimal conjugation action, with the sign fixed by the
  convention for the bracket. Hence $\{F,\mu_\epsilon\}=0$ for every invariant
  function $F$. If $I_\mu$ is the ideal generated by all $\mu_\epsilon$, then
  $\{F,\mu_\epsilon K\}=\mu_\epsilon\{F,K\}$ lies again in $I_\mu$. Therefore
  the bracket of invariant functions descends to
  $(\mc R_N/I_\mu)^{GL_N}$, equivalently to the degree-zero reduced BV
  cohomology. The scalar line $\C\cdot\mathrm{Tr}(1)$ is central, so quotienting
  by it is also compatible with the bracket.
\end{proof}

\begin{prop}\label{prop:first-order-bracket}
  In the stable scalar-reduced cohomology,
  $$\{\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)},
      \overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^m\phi_2^n)}\}
    =\overline{(kn-lm)\mathrm{Tr}
      (\phi_1^{k+m-1}\phi_2^{l+n-1})}.$$
\end{prop}

\begin{proof}
  Write $F_{k,l}=\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^k\phi_2^l)$. The matrix Poisson bracket
  induced by
  $$\{(\phi_1)^i{}_j,(\phi_2)^k{}_l\}=\delta^i_l\delta^k_j$$
  is
  $$\{F,G\}=\mathrm{Tr}\left(
      \frac{\partial F}{\partial\phi_1}\frac{\partial G}{\partial\phi_2}
      -\frac{\partial F}{\partial\phi_2}\frac{\partial G}{\partial\phi_1}
    \right),$$
  where the derivatives are cyclic matrix derivatives. Varying one occurrence
  at a time gives
  \begin{align*}
  \frac{\partial F_{k,l}}{\partial\phi_1}
    &=\sum_{a=0}^{k-1}\phi_1^{k-1-a}\phi_2^l\phi_1^a,\\
  \frac{\partial F_{k,l}}{\partial\phi_2}
    &=\sum_{b=0}^{l-1}\phi_2^{l-1-b}\phi_1^k\phi_2^b.
  \end{align*}
  Therefore the first summand in the bracket is a sum of $kn$ traces, each
  obtained by removing one $\phi_1$ from the first word and one $\phi_2$ from
  the second word. The second summand is a sum of $lm$ traces obtained by
  removing one $\phi_2$ from the first word and one $\phi_1$ from the second.
  Every trace appearing in either nonzero sum has multidegree
  $(k+m-1,l+n-1)$. Lemma~\ref{lem:adjacent-swaps} identifies all cyclic words
  with this multidegree in reduced BRST cohomology. The coefficient is
  therefore $kn-lm$, and the common class is
  $\overline{\mathrm{Tr}(\phi_1^{k+m-1}\phi_2^{l+n-1})}$. When the common word
  has multidegree $(0,0)$, this class is the scalar trace and is zero in the
  scalar-reduced quotient.
\end{proof}

\begin{thm}[Classical bulk-to-boundary Hamiltonian map]\label{thm:bulk-boundary}
  In the stable scalar-reduced Hamiltonian sector, the boundary evaluation maps
  $\partial_{\mathrm{bb},N}$ of Construction~\ref{constr:boundary-evaluation}
  stabilize to a linear map
  $$\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}:\bar A\longrightarrow
    H^0_{\mathrm{red}}(\mc O_{\mathrm{brane}}^{\mathrm{cl}}),\qquad
    f\longmapsto \overline{\mathrm{Tr}\,f(\phi_1,\phi_2)}.$$
  This map is a morphism of Lie algebras from the closed Hamiltonian Poisson
  algebra $\bar A$ to the scalar-reduced open trace Poisson algebra:
  $$\{\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}(f),\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}(g)\}_{\mathrm{open}}
    =\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}\bigl(\{f,g\}_{\bar A}\bigr).$$
\end{thm}

\begin{proof}
  The map is well-defined on $\bar A$ by
  Corollary~\ref{cor:matrix-evaluation-well-defined}; constants map to the
  scalar $N$ and are removed in the scalar-reduced sector. Lemma
  \ref{lem:trace-bracket-descends} gives the open reduced Poisson bracket. For
  $f=z_1^kz_2^l$ and $g=z_1^mz_2^n$, Proposition~\ref{prop:first-order-bracket}
  gives
  $$\{\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}(f),\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}(g)\}_{\mathrm{open}}
    =\overline{(kn-lm)\mathrm{Tr}
      (\phi_1^{k+m-1}\phi_2^{l+n-1})}.$$
  The closed Poisson bracket is
  $$\{f,g\}_{\bar A}=\overline{(kn-lm)z_1^{k+m-1}z_2^{l+n-1}},$$
  and applying $\partial_{\mathrm{bb}}$ gives the same expression. Bilinearity
  proves the identity for all polynomials.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:main-local}]
  The open Hamiltonian-sector algebra is obtained from the product of the exact
  A-model self-Floer model for $\R\subset T^*\R$ and the affine Koszul
  calculation for $\Ext^*_R(R/\mathfrak m,R/\mathfrak m)$.
  Lemma~\ref{lem:open-action-reduction} identifies the ghost-zero reduction
  with the gauged first-order matrix model
  $$\int_\R\mathrm{Tr}\bigl(\phi_1\,d\phi_2+A[\phi_1,\phi_2]\bigr).$$
  Lemma~\ref{lem:derivative-jets} removes positive topological jets, and
	  Lemma~\ref{lem:adjacent-swaps} identifies cyclic words with the same
	  commutative monomial. Proposition~\ref{prop:stable-trace-invariants} then
	  gives Proposition~\ref{prop:brane-ops}:
	  $$\mc{O}^{\mathrm{cl}}_{\mathrm{brane}}
	    \cong \mathbf{S}(\bar A\oplus\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}[1]).$$

	  On the closed side, the divergence computation and
	  Lemma~\ref{lem:polynomial-poincare} identify the local Hamiltonian modes
	  with $\bar A$. The cotangent BF field supplies the shifted coadjoint copy, so
	  the local Lie algebra is
	  $\mathfrak g=\mathfrak h\ltimes\mathfrak h^\vee[1]$. Proposition~\ref{prop:grav-ops}
	  computes local observables as
	  $C^\bullet_{\mathrm{CE},\mathrm{cont}}(\mathfrak g)$.
	  Proposition~\ref{prop:ce-koszul} gives the Koszul dual
	  $\widehat{U(\mathfrak g)}$, and the Rees/PBW specialization gives
	  $\mathbf{S}(\mathfrak g)=\mathbf{S}(\bar A\oplus\mathfrak h^\vee[1])$ at
	  $\hbar=0$. Choosing the monomial-dual basis identifies
	  $\mathfrak h^\vee$ with $\bar A_{\mathrm{desc}}$ as a filtered vector space.
  Finally Theorem~\ref{thm:bulk-boundary} constructs the map from closed
  Hamiltonians to brane Hamiltonians and shows that it preserves the Poisson
  bracket. On monomials this gives the coefficient $(kn-lm)$ on both sides.
\end{proof}

\subsubsection{Formal Moyal target at order $\hbar^2$}
We use the Weyl/Moyal Rees convention for the formal quantization of the
constant Poisson tensor:
$$f*g=m\circ\exp\left(\frac{\hbar}{2}
  (\partial_{z_1}\otimes\partial_{z_2}
   -\partial_{z_2}\otimes\partial_{z_1})\right)(f\otimes g).$$
Then the star commutator is odd in $\hbar$:
$$[f,g]_*=\hbar\{f,g\}+\frac{\hbar^3}{24}P^3(f,g)+O(\hbar^5),$$
where $P=\partial_{z_1}\otimes\partial_{z_2}
-\partial_{z_2}\otimes\partial_{z_1}$. Thus there is no $\hbar^2$ term in
this quantization convention.

\subsubsection{Formal Moyal target at order $\hbar^3$}
For monomials $f=z_1^kz_2^l$ and $g=z_1^mz_2^n$, the third-order Moyal
coefficient is completely explicit:
$$P^3(f,g)=
  \sum_{a=0}^3(-1)^a\binom{3}{a}
  (k)_{3-a}(l)_a(m)_a(n)_{3-a}
  z_1^{k+m-3}z_2^{l+n-3},$$
where $(r)_s=r(r-1)\cdots(r-s+1)$ and $(r)_0=1$. Therefore
$$[f,g]_*=\hbar(kn-lm)z_1^{k+m-1}z_2^{l+n-1}
  +\frac{\hbar^3}{24}P^3(f,g)+O(\hbar^5).$$
The script \texttt{scripts/check\_moyal\_coefficients.py} checks this
first-order coefficient, the vanishing of the second-order commutator
coefficient, the displayed third-order formula, and the star-commutator
normalisation on every monomial pair with exponents between $0$ and $6$, using
exact rational arithmetic.

\begin{rmk}
  In characteristic zero, passing to underived $GL_N$-invariants is exact on
  rational representations. This is the convention used in the reduced
  brane-operator computation above. Keeping the full derived quotient would
  retain the Chevalley ghosts and produces a larger BRST complex; that
  refinement is not used in the stable comparison made here.
\end{rmk}

\begin{rmk}
  On the gravity side we did not choose a brane: we worked with string states.
  On the gauge theory side we chose a brane of the form a point cross a
  Lagrangian. By choosing a different B-brane, we move away from the structure
  of an associative algebra to a more general factorization algebra. Koszul
  duality should extend to this setting. For example, with two topological
  directions (instead of one) we would obtain an $E_2$ algebra and hence $E_2$
  Koszul duality.
\end{rmk}

\begin{rmk}
  One could also study $D_0$ branes on $\R^2\times\C^4$ from this point of view.
\end{rmk}
